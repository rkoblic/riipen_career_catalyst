# Before the Direction Check

The Direction Check is a 30-minute meeting with your employer. It's not a formal presentation with slides. Your employer has already received your Context Analysis & Project Plan. This meeting is a structured conversation where you walk them through your thinking, highlight what matters most, and get their honest reaction before you commit to production.

---

## How to run this meeting

[VIDEO: How to walk through a document with a stakeholder]

The video above walks through how to structure a 30-minute document-based meeting with a stakeholder. Here's a recap of the key moves:

- **Don't walk through the document from start to finish.** You'll run out of time before you get to the parts where you need input. Highlight 2-3 key areas instead.
- **Choose what to highlight based on where the employer's perspective matters most.** Your strongest finding and why it matters, the recommendation you're most confident about, and the area where you're least certain.
- **Skip what the employer can read on their own.** Don't restate the organizational context. Don't read research findings like a list. Don't walk through the timeline item by item.
- **Transition to feedback with specific questions, not vague ones.** "We chose to focus on this segment because of our research. Does that match what you're seeing internally?" gets a real answer. "Any questions?" gets silence.
- **Assign roles before the meeting.** Someone opens and frames, someone presents key points, someone takes notes (exact language, not summaries), someone tracks time.

## Managing expectations and difficult conversations

At some point during a project, you may encounter a gap between what the employer expects and what's feasible. Maybe the scope has grown beyond what your team can deliver in the time remaining. Maybe the data they assumed you'd have access to doesn't exist. Maybe their vision for the final deliverable doesn't match the skills or resources available to your team.

This is normal. It happens in every professional engagement, not just course projects. The difference between a team that handles it well and one that doesn't comes down to how early and how clearly they name the gap.

Saying "we can't do that" is professional when it comes with context. "Based on what we've found so far, the original scope would require access to data we don't have. Here's what we can do instead, and here's why we think it still addresses your core question." That's not a failure. That's a team doing its job.

The worst version is silence: agreeing to something you can't deliver because the conversation feels uncomfortable. The employer would rather hear a realistic adjustment now than a disappointing deliverable later.

## Preparing for the Direction Check

Preparation makes the difference between a meeting that clarifies and one that wastes everyone's time. You have 30 minutes. Here's how to use them well.

**Decide what to highlight (before the meeting):**

Go through your Context Analysis & Project Plan and identify the 2-3 areas where the employer's input matters most. Good candidates:

- A finding that surprised you or that might surprise the employer
- The recommendation you're most confident about and the evidence behind it
- An assumption you're making that could change your whole approach if it's wrong
- A tradeoff you made (e.g., narrowing scope) where you want the employer to confirm the choice

**Prepare specific questions:**

- Questions that couldn't be answered from the project brief alone
- Questions about priorities: "If we had to choose between X and Y, which matters more to your team?"
- Questions about audience: who will actually use or read the deliverable, and what do they care about?

**A rough time structure:**

- Opening and framing (~2 min): "You've read our document. We'd like to highlight three areas and get your feedback."
- Key highlights (~8-10 min): Your 2-3 most important points, with reasoning
- Employer feedback and discussion (~15-18 min): This is the most valuable part. Protect this time.
- Confirming next steps (~2 min): What changes, what stays, and what you'll follow up on

**Assign roles:**

- Who opens and frames the conversation
- Who presents which highlights
- Who takes notes (capturing exact language, not summaries)
- Who tracks time — 30 minutes goes fast, and you don't want to eat into feedback time

If your team uses an AI note-taking tool, set it up before the meeting starts. It's a useful backup, but it's not a replacement for a human notetaker. AI transcripts miss nuance, misattribute speakers, and sometimes get key phrases wrong. You'll want both the transcript and someone on your team actively capturing what matters.

**When the meeting doesn't go as planned:**

Sometimes the employer arrives without having read your document. Sometimes they raise a concern that shifts the entire conversation off your prepared agenda. These are not emergencies.

If the employer hasn't reviewed your work, offer a brief verbal summary of your key findings and proposed approach, then shift your questions to be more exploratory than confirmatory. If the conversation shifts unexpectedly, follow the employer's lead. What they want to talk about is usually what matters most to them, even if it's not what you prepared for.

## Confirming logistics

Before the meeting, send a brief message to the employer through the Riipen platform confirming the time, meeting link, who will be attending from your team, and a short agenda. The agenda doesn't need to be elaborate. Something like: "We'd like to highlight a few key areas from our Context Analysis and Project Plan and get your feedback on our proposed approach" is enough. It tells the employer what to expect and signals that you have a plan for the time.

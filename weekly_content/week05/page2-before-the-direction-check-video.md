# Video script: How to walk through a document with a stakeholder

You have 30 minutes. The employer has your document. The temptation is to start at Part 1 and walk through everything in order. That's a mistake. You'll run out of time before you get to the parts where you actually need their input.

Here's a better approach. Open by framing the conversation: "Thanks for joining. You've read our Context Analysis and Project Plan. Rather than walk through the whole document, we'd like to highlight three things and then get your feedback." That tells the employer what to expect and signals that you're going to use the time well.

For those three highlights, choose the areas where the employer's perspective matters most. That usually means your strongest finding and why it matters, the recommendation you're most confident about and the reasoning behind it, and the area where you're least certain and want their input.

Here's what that actually sounds like. Say your team is working on a community outreach project for a nonprofit. Your first highlight might go like this: "The most important thing our research found is that the primary barrier to participation in programs like yours isn't awareness. It's transportation and scheduling. Three published studies on similar communities found the same pattern, and the organization's own data shows that sign-ups are highest in the neighborhoods closest to the current location. That's what's driving our recommendation."

That took about 30 seconds. You've given the employer your key finding, the evidence behind it, and how it connects to your approach. Now they have something specific to react to. They might confirm it, add context you didn't have, or tell you their internal data tells a different story. All of those responses are useful.

For your second highlight, walk through the recommendation itself: "Based on that finding, we're recommending a satellite-location pilot at two community centers that are accessible by public transit. We chose those locations because they're in the neighborhoods with the lowest current participation rates but the highest population density in the target age group." Again, lead with the reasoning. The employer can see what you chose and why, which makes it easy for them to redirect if needed.

For your third highlight, name where you're uncertain: "The assumption we're least sure about is whether the community centers have the space and willingness to host programming. We haven't been able to confirm that yet. If that assumption doesn't hold, we'd need to rethink the location strategy. That's something we'd especially like your perspective on."

Notice what you didn't do in any of that. You didn't restate the organizational context. They know their own organization. You didn't read your research findings like a list. And you didn't walk through the project plan timeline item by item. The employer can read those sections on their own. You used the meeting for the things that only a live conversation can accomplish: testing your assumptions, hearing what you missed, and finding out what matters most to them.

After your highlights, transition to feedback with a specific question, not a vague one. "Does the transportation barrier match what you're seeing, or is there something else driving the participation gap?" gets a real answer. "Any questions?" gets silence.

One more thing. Assign roles before the meeting. One person opens and frames the conversation. One or two people present the highlights. One person takes notes, capturing exact language, not summaries. And someone tracks time, because 30 minutes goes fast.

# Professional email basics

Email is still the backbone of professional communication. Messaging apps and collaboration tools handle the day-to-day, but when it comes to first impressions, formal requests, and communication across organizational boundaries, email sets the tone. How you write to a colleague, a client, or an employer contact signals your professionalism before they've ever met you.

You'll put this skill into practice soon when your team sends its first message to your employer contact through the Riipen platform's messaging system. The delivery mechanism is different from email, but treat it the same way: professional structure, clear purpose, appropriate tone. The skills are identical, and your employer contact will form their first impression of your team based on what you write. That's why this module is here now, before teams have even formed: it's better to learn the fundamentals before that moment than after a poorly received first impression.

## Common mistakes in professional email

Most email mistakes aren't about grammar. They're about awareness: knowing who you're writing to, what they need from you, and how much of their time you're asking for.

**Being vague about what you need.** Every professional email should make it clear what action you're expecting from the reader. If you're asking a question, ask it directly. If you need something by a specific date, say so. Emails that end with "let me know your thoughts" when you actually need a decision by Friday waste everyone's time.

**Burying the point.** Put the most important information in the first two sentences. Busy professionals skim. If they have to read three paragraphs to figure out what you're asking, they'll put it off or miss it entirely.

**Getting the tone wrong.** Too casual reads as unprofessional. Too formal reads as stiff or distant. The right tone is usually somewhere in between: respectful, clear, and warm enough to sound like a real person. When in doubt, match the tone of the person you're writing to.

**Forgetting context.** The recipient may not remember the details of your project, your last conversation, or what you agreed to. A brief line of context ("Following up on our meeting last Thursday about the stakeholder interviews") saves them from having to search through their inbox.

**Sending without reviewing.** Read your email once before sending. Check for clarity, tone, and whether you've actually attached the attachment you mentioned. This takes 30 seconds and prevents the most common professional embarrassment.

## Example: a well-structured professional email

Here's an example of a strong introductory message from a project team to an employer contact. Notice how it covers the essentials without being long, and how the tone balances professionalism with warmth.

**Subject: Career Catalyst project team introduction**

> Dear Ms. Ramirez,
>
> My name is Alex Chen, and I'm writing on behalf of our project team at [university]. We've been assigned to work with GreenPath Solutions on the community engagement strategy project through the Career Catalyst program.
>
> Our team includes four members with backgrounds in marketing, communications, and public policy. We've reviewed the project brief and are looking forward to learning more about GreenPath's goals and how we can contribute.
>
> Would you be available for a 30-minute introductory meeting sometime during the week of [date]? We're flexible on timing and happy to work around your schedule.
>
> Thank you for the opportunity to work with your organization. We're looking forward to getting started.
>
> Best regards,
> Alex Chen
> On behalf of Team 4, Career Catalyst

**What makes this work:**

- **Clear purpose.** The reader knows within the first two sentences who is writing and why.
- **Brief context.** The team establishes that they've read the brief without over-explaining.
- **Specific ask.** Instead of "let's find a time to meet," they propose a timeframe and format.
- **Professional but human.** The tone is respectful and warm without being stiff or overly casual.
- **Short.** Everything the reader needs, nothing they don't.

## Using AI to refine professional messages

AI is genuinely useful for professional communication, and using it here is encouraged. The reason you're learning the fundamentals first isn't because AI can't write a decent email. It's because you need to recognize when AI gets the tone wrong, buries the point, or misses context that matters. Those judgment calls are yours.

The best approach: draft your message yourself first, then use AI to refine it. Writing the first draft keeps your voice and intent in the work. AI is excellent at tightening language, catching awkward phrasing, and suggesting a more professional tone.

Here's an example prompt you can adapt:

*"I'm on a student project team writing our first message to an employer contact through a professional platform. Here's my draft: [paste draft]. Review it for professional tone, clarity, and structure. Flag anything that's too casual, too formal, or unclear. Suggest a revised version and explain what you changed and why."*

Asking AI to explain its changes is the key move. It turns the revision into a learning opportunity rather than a black box. Over time, you'll internalize the patterns and need less help.

How professionals use AI right now

AI isn't a future trend; it's current practice. The shift has been fast enough that the workplace you're preparing to enter looks meaningfully different from even a few years ago.

A nonprofit program director uses AI to analyze survey responses from hundreds of community members, identifying themes that would have taken days to surface manually. A journalist uses AI to cross-reference public financial filings while investigating a story, then verifies every finding against original documents before publishing. An urban planner uses AI to model traffic patterns under different scenarios, then presents the tradeoffs to city council with her own recommendations about which approach best serves the community. A human resources team uses AI to draft initial job descriptions, then rewrites them to reflect the organization's culture and values in ways AI can't capture.

The common thread isn't any one tool or industry. It's that AI is changing the shape of professional work: what used to take days might take hours, and what used to be impossible for a small team is now within reach. But in every case, the professional brings the judgment, the context, and the accountability. AI accelerates the work. It doesn't replace the thinking.

This course's AI policy

This course treats AI as a professional tool, not a shortcut. You're expected to learn how to use it well, which means using it in ways that strengthen your thinking rather than replace it.

What's permitted. You may use AI tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, and similar) for most course activities. This includes using AI to brainstorm ideas, refine your writing, organize research, prepare for meetings, and explore unfamiliar topics. When you use AI for a graded submission, you must be able to explain and defend everything in your work. If someone asks you why you made a particular choice or what a specific finding means, you need a real answer, not "the AI said so."

What requires disclosure. For graded deliverables, you must disclose how you used AI in your process. This doesn't mean listing every prompt. It means being transparent about the role AI played: Did it help you brainstorm? Did it draft text that you then rewrote? Did you use it to check your reasoning? The disclosure requirement is itself a learning exercise. Describing how you used AI forces you to reflect on your own process, distinguish between AI's contribution and your own thinking, and develop the professional habit of documenting your methodology. In consulting, research, and many other fields, documenting how you arrived at your conclusions is standard practice. It's also how your instructor can give you better feedback on your actual skill development.

KEY PRINCIPLE

The defend-your-work standard. If you can explain your reasoning, describe your process, and defend the choices in your work, it's yours, regardless of what tools you used to produce it. If you can't, it doesn't matter whether a human or an AI generated it. The issue is the same: you're submitting work you don't understand.

This isn't about catching you using AI. It's about ensuring that AI use actually contributes to your learning. Professionals who rely on AI without understanding the output make expensive mistakes. Building the habit of understanding and owning your work starts here.

Where AI is off-limits or restricted

AI USE

The Your Starting Point Questionnaire must be completed without AI assistance. This covers both the self-assessment and your project preference rationale. The questionnaire captures your honest starting point, which you'll reference back to throughout the course in your reflections. If AI generates your answers, that baseline is fiction. The entire reflection sequence depends on this starting point being genuinely yours. (You can use AI to help you compare project briefs before writing your rationale — that's a thinking tool, not a shortcut. But the rationale itself must be yours.)

AI USE

DEAL reflections must be written by you, not drafted by AI. The experience you describe, the examination of what happened, and the learning you articulate must come from your own thinking. AI cannot supply any of that. However, you can use AI as a thinking partner during your reflection process: asking it to challenge your reasoning, push you to go deeper, or help you identify assumptions you might be missing. The distinction is between using AI to sharpen your thinking (permitted) and using AI to generate the reflection itself (not permitted). You'll learn more about this approach when you write your first reflection.

Quick-reference AI policy

AI policy checklist

☐ Use AI as a thinking partner. — Brainstorm, refine, explore. Don't outsource your judgment.

☐ Draft first, then refine with AI. — Write your own version before asking AI to polish or improve. Drafting first forces you to organize your own thinking and identify gaps in your understanding. If you start with AI, you anchor on its framing and lose the opportunity to develop your own analytical voice.

☐ Disclose AI use on graded submissions. — Describe the role AI played in your process.

☐ Be able to explain and defend everything. — If it's in your work, you should be able to explain why.

☐ Verify AI-generated facts. — AI's training data has a cutoff. Check claims against current, primary sources.

☐ Complete the Your Starting Point Questionnaire yourself. — No AI on the self-assessment or preference rationale. (You can use AI to compare briefs before writing your rationale.)

☐ Write your DEAL reflections yourself. — AI can help you think deeper, but it cannot draft the reflection. The experience and learning must be yours.

☐ No disclosure needed for ungraded activities. — Day-to-day AI use for learning and practice doesn't require formal disclosure.

# Project browsing and your starting point

Every professional project starts the same way: understanding what you're working on and why. This week, you'll review the available project briefs, decide which ones interest you most, and submit your preferences along with the Your Starting Point Questionnaire that captures where you're beginning this experience.

---

## Understanding your project and team

In this course, you'll work as part of a small team on a project for a real organization. Your employer contact is a professional who has a genuine need for the work your team will produce. This isn't a simulation.

The project arc follows a pattern common in professional consulting and agency work: you'll start by understanding the problem, then research and plan your approach, produce deliverables, gather feedback, iterate, and present your final work. Along the way, you'll communicate regularly with your employer contact through the Riipen platform, just as you would with a client in a professional setting.

Your team will be assigned based on the preferences you submit this week and other matching criteria. You won't get to choose your teammates, which is also realistic. Most professionals don't get to pick who they work with. Learning to collaborate effectively with the team you have, not just the team you'd choose, is one of the most valuable parts of this experience.

---

## Reviewing project briefs

You'll have access to multiple project briefs, each describing a different organization and the work they need done. Before you commit to your preferences, take a systematic approach to reviewing them.

**Skim first, then deep-read.** Start by reading through all of the available briefs quickly. Get a sense of the range: the industries represented, the types of deliverables, the scope of work. Resist the urge to fall in love with the first brief that sounds interesting. Anchoring on your first impression is one of the most common decision-making biases, and the skim-first strategy is a deliberate countermeasure. Professionals use the same approach in grant review, hiring, and vendor selection: see the full range of options before committing attention to any one of them. That exposure to the full set is what makes genuine comparison possible.

After your initial skim, pick two or three briefs that genuinely interest you and read them carefully. For each one, think about:

### What to look for in a project brief
- [ ] **Scope.** What is the organization actually asking for? Is it clearly defined, or does it leave room for your team to shape the direction?
- [ ] **Skills.** What skills would this project develop? Do those align with what you want to learn?
- [ ] **Industry.** Is this a field you're curious about or want exposure to?
- [ ] **Challenge level.** Does the project feel like the right stretch, demanding enough to be interesting, but not so far outside your experience that you'd struggle to contribute?
- [ ] **What excites you.** Sometimes the best reason to choose a project is that something about it genuinely interests you. That motivation carries you through the harder weeks.

**Using AI to compare briefs.** Once you've narrowed your options, AI can be a useful thinking tool here. Try asking it to help you generate comparison criteria, identify questions you should ask about each brief, or surface tradeoffs between your finalists. This is a good use of AI: it helps you think more systematically, not replace your judgment.

## Your Starting Point Questionnaire

The Your Starting Point Questionnaire has two parts: a self-assessment and your project preferences. Together, they capture who you are at the start of this experience and what you want to get out of it.

### Part 1: About you

The first part captures where you're beginning: your skills, your experience, your goals, and your expectations for this course. It might seem like a routine onboarding form, but it serves a more important purpose than that.

Your responses inform project matching, helping place you on a team and project where your interests and growth goals align with the work. More importantly, they become the baseline for your reflection practice. Throughout the course, you'll reference back to your Starting Point responses in your reflections, examining how your thinking, skills, and assumptions have changed since you wrote them. This is where the growth becomes visible.

This means the questionnaire is only useful if it's honest. Aspirational answers or polished self-descriptions don't help. If you're unsure about something, say so. If you've never done something before, that's worth noting. The goal is an accurate starting point, not an impressive one.

### Part 2: Your project preferences

The second part asks for your ranked project selections with a brief written rationale for each. This isn't a formality. It's your first exercise in evidence-based reasoning in this course.

A strong rationale goes beyond "this sounds interesting." It connects the project to something specific: a skill you want to develop, an industry you're curious about, a type of deliverable that aligns with your career direction. Requiring written justification isn't a hoop to jump through. It forces you to move from gut feeling to articulated reasoning, which surfaces assumptions and makes them available for examination. It also creates a record you can revisit later in your reflections to see how your criteria and priorities evolved. Writing decision memos, justifying recommendations, and defending your reasoning are professional skills that start here.

A useful structure: name the project, state what draws you to it, and explain what you'd hope to gain from working on it. Two or three sentences per selection is sufficient. Be specific and honest.

### AI use on this questionnaire

The self-assessment (Part 1) and your preference rationale (Part 2) must be completed without AI assistance. If AI generates your self-assessment, that baseline is fiction. When you sit down to write a reflection later in the course and look back at what you said here, you need it to be real. You can't reflect meaningfully on growth from a starting point that was never yours.

You *can* use AI to help you compare and evaluate the project briefs themselves before writing your rationale — generating comparison criteria, identifying questions about each brief, or surfacing tradeoffs between your options. That's a legitimate use of AI as a thinking tool. But the rationale you write should be yours: your reasoning, in your words, reflecting your actual priorities.

## Submission requirements

Complete the Your Starting Point Questionnaire (both parts) through the Riipen platform. It's completion-based, meaning it's assessed on whether you submitted it, not on a quality rubric. As with other completion-based items in this course, that's a deliberate choice: removing quality pressure lets you be genuinely honest rather than performing competence. But "completion-based" doesn't mean "low effort." The honesty of your responses directly affects how useful your reflections will be for the rest of the course.

# DEAL sorting exercise

## Purpose

This exercise practices distinguishing between the three stages of the DEAL model: Describe, Examine, and Articulate Learning. The most common mistake in early reflections is mixing description with examination, or jumping straight to "what I'll do differently" without analyzing why something happened. Sorting statements into the correct stage builds the habit of separating what happened from why it happened from what you learned, which is the core skill the DEAL framework develops.

## Interaction model

**Preferred: Card sorting.** Students see a set of 8 reflection statements, one at a time (or all at once if the tool supports drag-and-drop). For each statement, they sort it into one of three categories: Describe, Examine, or Articulate Learning. After sorting all 8, they submit and receive per-item feedback plus a debrief.

**Fallback: Sequential multiple-choice.** Each statement is presented one at a time with three options (Describe / Examine / Articulate Learning). Students select one, receive immediate feedback, and move to the next.

## Context setup

You've just read about the DEAL model and seen a worked example. Now it's your turn to practice. Below are eight statements pulled from different reflections. Each one belongs to a single DEAL stage: Describe, Examine, or Articulate Learning. Your job is to sort each statement into the correct stage.

This is harder than it sounds. The most common mistake is confusing description with examination, or thinking that a plan for the future counts as examination when it's actually articulated learning. Pay attention to what each statement is actually doing, not just what it's about.

## Stimulus

The eight statements below are drawn from reflections on different team project experiences. They are not in any particular order, and the three stages are not equally represented.

## Questions

### Statement 1

**Statement:** "Our team met for an hour on Tuesday to divide up the research tasks for the context analysis. Each person chose a topic area, and we agreed to share notes in a shared document by Friday."

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) Describe
- B) Examine
- C) Articulate Learning

**Correct answer:** A

**Feedback if correct:** Right. This is pure description: what happened, when, who did what, what was agreed. There's no interpretation or analysis, just a factual account of the event.

**Feedback if incorrect — B (Examine):** This one is a common mix-up. It might feel like there's analysis here because it mentions a decision the team made (dividing tasks, setting a deadline). But the statement is reporting what happened, not analyzing why or what it meant. Examination would ask: why did we divide the work this way? Was it the right approach?

**Feedback if incorrect — C (Articulate Learning):** This is description, not articulated learning. There's no statement about what was learned or how the writer will approach things differently. It's a factual account of what the team did.

---

### Statement 2

**Statement:** "I assumed that dividing the research by topic would be the most efficient approach, but it meant none of us had the full picture when we came back together. We each knew our piece well but struggled to see how the pieces connected."

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) Describe
- B) Examine
- C) Articulate Learning

**Correct answer:** B

**Feedback if correct:** Right. This is examination. The writer is analyzing an assumption they brought to the situation ("dividing by topic would be most efficient") and identifying the unintended consequence (fragmented understanding). The key signal is "I assumed... but..." which shows the writer interrogating their own thinking.

**Feedback if incorrect — A (Describe):** This goes beyond description. Yes, it references what happened (they divided by topic, they struggled to connect), but it's doing more than reporting. The phrase "I assumed" signals that the writer is analyzing their own thinking. Description would just state what happened without examining the reasoning behind it.

**Feedback if incorrect — C (Articulate Learning):** Close, but not quite. The writer is identifying a problem with their assumption, which is examination. Articulated learning would go further: naming a specific takeaway and how they'll apply it in the future. This statement stops at the analysis.

---

### Statement 3

**Statement:** "Going forward, I'll suggest that our team start each research phase with a 15-minute alignment meeting where we identify the key questions we need to answer collectively, before anyone dives into their individual section."

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) Describe
- B) Examine
- C) Articulate Learning

**Correct answer:** C

**Feedback if correct:** Right. This is articulated learning. The writer names a specific action they'll take in the future ("suggest a 15-minute alignment meeting"), grounded in what they learned from examining the experience. The key signals are "going forward" and a concrete, actionable plan.

**Feedback if incorrect — A (Describe):** This isn't describing something that happened. It's projecting forward, naming a specific change the writer plans to make based on what they learned. Description stays in the past; articulated learning points toward the future.

**Feedback if incorrect — B (Examine):** This goes beyond examination. Examination analyzes what happened and why. This statement has already moved past analysis into application: here's what I'll do differently next time. That forward-looking, actionable quality is what makes it articulated learning.

---

### Statement 4

**Statement:** "The employer contact mentioned during the meeting that their previous student team had struggled with scope creep. She said it twice, once at the beginning and once when we were discussing deliverables."

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) Describe
- B) Examine
- C) Articulate Learning

**Correct answer:** A

**Feedback if correct:** Right. This is description. It reports what the employer said, when she said it, and how many times. There's no interpretation of why she said it or what it means for the team. The specificity (she said it twice, at two different points) is good description — it captures a detail that could become important during examination.

**Feedback if incorrect — B (Examine):** It might feel like examination because the detail seems loaded with meaning (she said it twice, which probably means something). But the statement itself doesn't interpret the meaning. It just reports the observation. Examination would ask: why did she emphasize this? What does it tell us about her expectations or concerns?

**Feedback if incorrect — C (Articulate Learning):** This is description. There's no statement about what was learned or how the writer plans to act on this information. It's capturing a factual detail from the meeting.

---

### Statement 5

**Statement:** "I've learned that when someone repeats a concern in a professional meeting, it's usually a signal that this is a priority for them, even if they don't frame it that way explicitly. I'll pay closer attention to repeated themes in future meetings and raise them directly with the team afterward."

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) Describe
- B) Examine
- C) Articulate Learning

**Correct answer:** C

**Feedback if correct:** Right. This is articulated learning. It names a specific insight ("repeated concerns signal priorities") and a concrete plan for applying it ("pay closer attention to repeated themes, raise them with the team"). Both elements — the named learning and the forward-looking application — are what distinguish this from examination.

**Feedback if incorrect — A (Describe):** This isn't reporting what happened. It's stating what the writer learned from the experience and how they'll act on it. Description stays in the facts; this statement draws a lesson and projects it forward.

**Feedback if incorrect — B (Examine):** This is a common confusion. The statement does involve analysis (interpreting repeated concerns as signals). But it goes further than examination by naming the learning explicitly ("I've learned that...") and stating a plan for future action. Examination would stop at the interpretation without committing to a specific change.

### Statement 6

**Statement:** "I think the reason our presentation fell flat was that we focused on what we'd done rather than what we'd found. The employer wanted insights, not a process summary. We spent eight minutes on methodology and two minutes on findings."

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) Describe
- B) Examine
- C) Articulate Learning

**Correct answer:** B

**Feedback if correct:** Right. This is examination. The writer is analyzing why the presentation didn't land ("we focused on what we'd done rather than what we'd found") and identifying a mismatch between what the team delivered and what the employer wanted. The phrase "I think the reason" signals interpretation and analysis, not just reporting.

**Feedback if incorrect — A (Describe):** This includes some descriptive detail (eight minutes on methodology, two on findings), but the statement is doing more than reporting. It's interpreting why the presentation didn't work. The phrase "I think the reason" signals analysis. Description would just say what happened without diagnosing why.

**Feedback if incorrect — C (Articulate Learning):** The writer has identified the problem (process over insights) but hasn't yet named a specific lesson or stated how they'll approach it differently. Articulated learning would add something like: "Next time, I'll structure our presentation around findings first and only include methodology if the audience asks for it."

---

### Statement 7

**Statement:** "During the direction check, our employer contact said she was 'generally happy with the direction' but asked three follow-up questions about our data sources and methodology."

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) Describe
- B) Examine
- C) Articulate Learning

**Correct answer:** A

**Feedback if correct:** Right. This is description. It reports what the employer said and did during a specific interaction: her stated reaction and her follow-up behavior. There's no interpretation of the gap between "generally happy" and three pointed questions. That gap is interesting, but the statement just captures it — examination would interpret it.

**Feedback if incorrect — B (Examine):** This is a tricky one. The statement captures something that feels like it *should* be examined (the gap between "generally happy" and three follow-up questions about methodology). But the statement itself doesn't analyze that gap. It just reports it accurately. Good description often captures details that are ripe for examination, and that's exactly what this is.

**Feedback if incorrect — C (Articulate Learning):** This is description. There's no lesson named and no plan for the future. It's reporting what happened in a specific meeting interaction.

---

### Statement 8

**Statement:** "I need to get better at asking follow-up questions in meetings. I always think of the right question ten minutes after the meeting ends."

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) Describe
- B) Examine
- C) Articulate Learning

**Correct answer:** B

**Feedback if correct:** Right, and this is one of the trickiest items. It feels like articulated learning because the writer is identifying an area for growth ("I need to get better at asking follow-up questions"). But it's actually examination: the writer is analyzing a pattern in their own behavior without naming a specific, actionable change. "I need to get better at X" is a diagnosis, not a plan. Articulated learning would specify how: "I'll write down questions as they come up during the meeting so I can ask them before we adjourn."

**Feedback if incorrect — A (Describe):** This isn't describing a specific event. It's reflecting on a behavioral pattern across multiple meetings ("I always think of the right question ten minutes after"). That self-analysis makes it examination, not description.

**Feedback if incorrect — C (Articulate Learning):** This is the most common incorrect answer, and it's an important distinction. "I need to get better at X" identifies a growth area, but it isn't articulated learning because it doesn't name a specific lesson or an actionable plan. It's a diagnosis without a prescription. Strong articulated learning would say what the writer will do differently and how, not just what they need to improve.

## Debrief

The trickiest distinctions in DEAL are between description and examination (statement 7 is a good example — rich detail that feels analytical but is actually just precise reporting) and between examination and articulated learning (statement 8 — diagnosing a problem is not the same as naming what you learned and how you'll apply it).

When you write your own DEAL reflections, use these as gut checks: Am I reporting what happened, or analyzing why? Am I analyzing a pattern, or naming a specific lesson with a concrete plan? If you can answer those questions about each paragraph you write, you're using the framework well.

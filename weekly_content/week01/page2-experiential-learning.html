What Is Experiential Learning?

In most professional roles, nobody hands you a textbook and a test date. You're given a problem, a team, and a deadline. You figure it out by doing the work, getting feedback, and adjusting. That cycle of experience, reflection, and application is what experiential learning formalizes, and it's how this entire course is built.

Why This Matters in an AI-Enabled Workplace

AI can summarize a report, draft an email, or generate a project plan in seconds. What it can't do is navigate a difficult conversation with a teammate, read the room in a client meeting, or decide which of three imperfect options your team should pursue. Those judgment calls come from experience, and experience only develops when you actually do the work.

That's the gap this course is designed to close. You'll develop the skills that AI can't replace: professional communication, team collaboration, critical thinking under real constraints, and the ability to reflect on your own performance and improve. These are the capabilities employers consistently say they're looking for, and they're the ones that are hardest to build in a traditional classroom.

The AI tools available to you will keep getting better. Your ability to work effectively with people, make sound judgments, and learn from experience is what makes those tools useful rather than just fast.

How This Course Works Differently

Traditional coursework tends to follow a familiar pattern: absorb content, practice with exercises, demonstrate understanding on an assessment. This course doesn't follow that sequence. Here's what's different.

  • The work is real. You're not completing a simulation or a case study. Your team will work on a project for a real organization with a real contact person who has a genuine need for what you produce. The stakes are different when someone is actually going to use your work.
  • You're graded on what you produce and how you think. There are no exams or final papers. Your assessment is based on the artifacts you create (research, deliverables, presentations), your professional conduct, and your ability to reflect on your own growth. If you can explain and defend your work, that's what matters.
  • AI is part of the toolkit. You'll learn how to use AI as a professional tool throughout the course. There's a full module on this later in the week.
  • Content arrives when you need it. Instead of front-loading all the material, the course introduces skills and frameworks at the point where you'll apply them. What you learn in any given week is directly connected to the work you're doing that week.
  • There are regular checkpoints. You're not on your own until a final deadline. The course is structured with frequent, smaller deliverables and reflection points that keep you on track and give you feedback along the way. The checkpoints aren't there to micromanage you. They're built in because sustained project work is easier to manage in small, regular increments than in one big push at the end, and because early feedback prevents small misunderstandings from compounding into major rework.
  • Your team depends on you. Most of what you produce is collaborative. That means your reliability, communication, and ability to work through disagreements are part of the learning, not just the project output.
  • Ambiguity is normal. Real projects don't come with clear instructions and a single right answer. Learning to ask good questions, make decisions with incomplete information, and adapt when things change is part of what you're here to practice.

The Skills You're Building

This course is structured around the NACE Career Readiness Competencies, a framework developed by the National Association of Colleges and Employers based on what employers consistently identify as essential for career success. These aren't abstract learning outcomes. They're the specific capabilities that hiring managers look for and that professionals use every day.

The course focuses on five competency areas:

  • Career & Self-Development. Understanding your own strengths, goals, and growth areas, and being able to articulate them clearly.
  • Communication. Writing professionally, listening actively, presenting ideas, and adapting your message to different audiences and contexts.
  • Critical Thinking. Analyzing information, framing problems, weighing evidence, and making reasoned decisions under real constraints.
  • Professionalism. Showing up reliably, receiving feedback well, taking initiative, and producing quality work.
  • Teamwork. Contributing to a team, holding yourself and others accountable, giving constructive feedback, and collaborating effectively.

Every activity in this course maps to one or more of these competencies, and we'll make those connections visible as you go. You'll see the specific skills tagged at the top of each page throughout the course — those badges show which competencies that page develops. Research on transparent course design shows that when you understand the purpose behind an activity, not just the task, you engage with it more deeply and get more out of it. The syllabus review later this week includes the full breakdown of specific subskills within each area.

What This Means for You

A few things will feel different from day one:

  • Ambiguity is part of the process. Real projects don't come with answer keys. Your employer contact won't always give you perfectly clear instructions, and that's realistic. Learning to ask the right clarifying questions is part of the skill set you're building.
  • Your team is your primary learning environment. Most of what you produce will be collaborative. That means the quality of your team's working relationship directly affects the quality of your learning.
  • Reflection is not optional. The reflection assignments in this course aren't afterthoughts. Writing about what you experienced forces you to move from a vague sense of "that went well" or "that was hard" to a specific understanding of what happened and why. That process of articulation turns tacit experience into knowledge you can actually apply to the next situation. It's where some of the deepest learning happens.
  • Progress looks different. You won't feel like you've "mastered" something after one module. Skills like professional communication and team collaboration develop gradually, over repeated cycles of practice and feedback. Trust the process.

The payoff is that by the end of this course, you won't just know about professional skills. You'll have practiced them, reflected on them, and have concrete evidence of your growth to show for it.

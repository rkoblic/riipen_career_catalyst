The Course Arc: Five Phases

The course is organized into five phases, each with a different focus. Understanding where you are in this arc at any given point will help you see how individual activities connect to the bigger picture.

  • Foundation. You'll build your team, establish working agreements, make first contact with your employer, and develop the professional skills you'll rely on throughout the course. The deliverables during this phase are lighter, giving you space to build the habits and relationships that sustain the rest of the work.
  • Discovery. Your team digs into the project. You'll research the organization, clarify the problem, and develop a plan for how you'll approach the work. This is where you move from understanding the brief to understanding the context behind it.
  • Development. The most intensive phase. Your team produces the core project deliverables, working through research, analysis, and iteration. You'll apply the skills and frameworks from earlier phases under real constraints: deadlines, team dynamics, and employer expectations. Regular check-ins with your employer contact keep the work on track.
  • Refinement. You'll polish your deliverables based on feedback, strengthen your analysis, and prepare your work for final presentation. This phase is about quality, coherence, and making sure your work meets professional standards.
  • Integration. The final phase brings everything together. You'll present your finished work to the employer and complete a capstone reflection that looks back across the entire experience, connecting what you learned to where you're headed next.

Each phase builds on the one before it. The foundation work makes discovery possible, discovery informs development, development gives you something to refine, and refinement prepares you for a strong final delivery.

Competency Framework

As introduced in the experiential learning module, this course is built around the NACE Career Readiness Competencies. Here's the full breakdown of the five competency areas and the specific subskills you'll develop throughout the course.

Career & Self-Development

  • Self-Awareness. Recognizing your strengths, limitations, and development areas honestly.
  • Reflection. Examining your experiences to extract meaningful lessons and apply them going forward.
  • Growth Orientation. Actively seeking opportunities to develop new skills and expand your capabilities.
  • Career Articulation. Communicating your skills, experiences, and professional value clearly and convincingly.

Communication

  • Professional Correspondence. Writing clear, context-appropriate messages to colleagues, clients, and stakeholders.
  • Active Listening. Fully engaging with what others are saying, asking good follow-up questions, and confirming understanding.
  • Written Clarity. Producing well-organized, concise written work that communicates complex ideas effectively.
  • Oral Presentation. Presenting ideas, findings, and recommendations clearly and confidently to a professional audience.

Critical Thinking

  • Prioritization. Evaluating competing demands and allocating time and effort to what matters most.
  • Research and Analysis. Gathering relevant information from credible sources and synthesizing it into actionable insight.
  • Evidence-Based Reasoning. Making decisions and recommendations grounded in evidence rather than assumption.
  • Problem Framing. Defining problems clearly before jumping to solutions, including identifying constraints, stakeholders, and root causes.

Professionalism

  • Dependability. Following through on commitments, meeting deadlines, and being someone your team and employer can count on.
  • Feedback Reception. Receiving constructive criticism productively and using it to improve your work.
  • Quality & Attention to Detail. Producing polished, thorough work that reflects care and professional standards.
  • Initiative. Taking action without being asked, anticipating needs, and contributing beyond the minimum.

Teamwork

  • Shared Accountability. Taking collective ownership of team outcomes, not just your individual contributions.
  • Constructive Feedback. Giving teammates honest, specific, and actionable feedback that helps them improve.
  • Reliability. Completing your portion of team work on time and at the standard your team agreed to.
  • Collaborative Contribution. Actively participating in team processes, building on others' ideas, and supporting group decision-making.

Every major activity and deliverable in this course connects to one or more of these subskills. You won't develop all of them equally in every week, but by the end of the course, you'll have practiced and reflected on each one.

How You'll Be Assessed

The assessment structure reflects how professional work is actually evaluated: through the quality of what you produce and your ability to explain the thinking behind it.

Graded deliverables

These are the major assignments that carry weight toward your final grade. They include project deliverables your team produces for the employer (research, plans, presentations) and individual reflections where you demonstrate your learning. Each graded deliverable has a rubric that's available in advance so you know exactly what's expected.

Completion-based items

Some activities are graded on completion rather than quality. These include the Your Starting Point Questionnaire, the team charter, and certain process checkpoints. Making these completion-based rather than quality-graded is a deliberate choice: it removes performance pressure so you can be genuinely honest (especially on self-assessments), while the completion requirement ensures the course infrastructure that depends on them actually works. Grading the questionnaire on quality, for example, would incentivize polished answers, which would undermine its purpose as an honest baseline. Complete them honestly and on time.

The reflection portfolio

Throughout the course, you'll complete a series of structured reflections using the DEAL model (which you'll learn about later this week). These reflections are individually graded and build on each other. Together, they form a portfolio that tracks your growth across the full experience. The portfolio is cumulative and spaced throughout the semester rather than a single final reflection because capturing learning as it happens is far more accurate than trying to reconstruct it at the end. The progressive structure also lets you see your own growth trajectory over time, which reinforces the skills you're building. This mirrors professional practice as well: performance reviews, project retrospectives, and career development portfolios all depend on ongoing documentation rather than end-of-term summaries.

The defend-your-work standard

For any graded submission, you should be prepared to explain your reasoning, your process, and the choices you made. This is especially relevant for work where AI was used as a tool. If you can explain and defend it, it's your work. If you can't, it isn't.

Key Policies

  • Attendance and participation. Consistent engagement matters. This course depends on teamwork, and your team depends on you showing up, prepared and on time. Specific attendance requirements are outlined in the syllabus.
  • Late submissions. Deadlines in this course often connect to other people's work. A late submission from you can delay your team's progress or miss a window for employer feedback. Review the late submission policy in the syllabus and plan accordingly.
  • Academic integrity. The academic integrity policy for this course aligns with your institution's standards. The AI policy (covered in the next module) is part of this: it specifies what AI use is permitted, what requires disclosure, and where AI is off-limits. Read both carefully.

Teamwork: Shared Accountability

What's coming up

In the upcoming week, you'll be assigned to a team and a project. Your first task as a team will be to build a Team Charter, a written agreement about how you'll work together: communication norms, decision-making processes, roles, and expectations. You'll also draft and send your team's first message to your employer contact through the Riipen platform, introducing yourselves and requesting the kickoff meeting.

Both of those tasks go more smoothly when everyone arrives with some shared vocabulary about what makes teams work well. The reading below will give you that.

Pre-reading: team norms and working agreements

Before your first team meeting, take some time with this resource on how teams establish norms and working agreements. Reading about team norms before meeting your team is intentional: if you first encounter these ideas during the charter meeting itself, you're simultaneously learning the concept and trying to apply it under social pressure. Separating the two gives you time to form your own positions before group dynamics kick in. Pay attention to the difference between assumed norms (what people expect but never say out loud) and explicit norms (what the team actually discusses and agrees on). Most team friction comes from the gap between the two.

Reflection prompts

As you think ahead to your first team experience, consider these questions. You don't need to write anything down, but spending a few minutes with them will make the team charter conversation more productive. When you've already articulated your own values and preferences to yourself, you arrive at the conversation with prepared positions rather than making them up on the spot, which reduces the tendency to just go along with whoever speaks first.

  • What kind of teammate do you want to be? Think about past group experiences, both good and bad. What did the best collaborators do that made the team work? What behaviors frustrated you?
  • What matters to you in how a team works together? Some people care most about reliability. Others prioritize open communication, or equal distribution of work, or how decisions get made. There's no wrong answer, but knowing your own priorities helps you advocate for them.
  • What's your default under pressure? When a deadline is tight or something goes wrong, do you take over, go quiet, push back, or something else? Understanding your default reaction is the first step to managing it.
  • What do you need from your teammates to do your best work? This is the question most people never ask out loud, and it's one of the most useful things you can bring to a charter conversation.

Team Charter template preview

When you meet with your team, you'll work together to complete a Team Charter. The charter covers:

  • Communication norms. How and when the team will communicate, which platforms you'll use, and expected response times.
  • Decision-making. How the team will make decisions when you disagree, whether by consensus, majority, or delegation.
  • Roles and responsibilities. Not rigid job titles, but a shared understanding of who takes point on what and how work will be distributed.
  • Accountability. What happens when someone misses a deadline or doesn't follow through. This is the hardest part of the charter to write and the most important.
  • Meeting norms. When you'll meet, how long, what preparation is expected, and how you'll handle it when someone can't attend.
KEY PRINCIPLE

You don't need to fill out the charter now. Just review it so you know what's coming and can start thinking about what you want to propose to your team. Previewing the structure of an upcoming task before doing it reduces anxiety and improves performance, especially for collaborative work where expectations can feel uncertain. It also makes the pre-reading above more purposeful: you'll read about team norms with the specific charter categories in mind.

# Reflection #2

The past several weeks gave you real material to work with: a Direction Check where the employer responded to your work, a transition into production with incomplete information, team decisions under pressure, moments where something didn't go the way you expected. That's not background context for this reflection — it's the point.

Reflection is how experience becomes learning. Without it, the experiences you just had are just things that happened. You completed a Direction Check. You started drafting. The weeks passed. With reflection, those same experiences become a specific insight about how you work — how you respond when expectations are unclear, what you do when a teammate's approach diverges from yours, what you assumed going into the employer meeting and whether those assumptions held.

The difference is the examination. Most people move from "what happened" to "what's next" without spending any time on the step in between — the one where the actual learning lives. That step is the whole point of this assignment, and of the four reflections still ahead of you.

This isn't a completion task. It's a thinking task. Done well, it produces something you'll actually carry forward — not a grade, but a specific, transferable insight about yourself as a professional. That's what the portfolio is building toward.

---

This is your second DEAL reflection. You've used the framework before — this one asks more of you because you're no longer new to it. The cognitive overhead of learning how to use DEAL is gone. What replaces it is the expectation that you'll use the space it frees up for genuine depth and honesty in your examination.

The DEAL framework moves through three stages: Describe (what happened), Examine (what it reveals), and Articulate Learning (what you're taking forward). You've worked through those stages once. The prompt and the rubric are the same; the standard is higher.

---

## The prompt

Choose a specific experience from the Discovery phase — the research process, the Direction Check, the transition into production, a moment where your team's dynamic shifted, a moment of ambiguity that tested your judgment, or anything from the past several weeks that stayed with you. Use DEAL to describe what happened, examine what it reveals about how you work, and articulate what you're taking forward.

The anchor experience can be anything that was real and specific enough to examine. What matters is that you were in it — not observing it.

**Describe**

The description standard is the same as your first reflection: specific, factual, grounded in a named experience rather than a summary of a phase. One experience, examined closely. "The Discovery phase went well" is not a description. "The moment we presented our research direction to the employer and they told us our framing was too narrow" is.

If you're returning to the kickoff meeting or a similar formative moment, the description needs to capture something specific enough that a reader can picture the situation — what was said, what happened, what the context was. Breadth in the Describe stage produces nothing to examine.

**Examine**

This is where the expectation shifts most clearly between your first and second reflections. The framework is no longer new, which means there's no excuse for staying on the surface. Push further than you did before.

What assumptions were you operating under that the experience revealed? What patterns do you notice about how you respond in similar situations — including situations from earlier in the course or your broader experience? What were you feeling that you didn't acknowledge at the time?

The test for a strong Examine section: if your description were removed, would the examination still contain insight — something you didn't fully see while the experience was happening? If the Examine section reads like a retelling of the description with different words, push deeper.

**Articulate learning**

The standard here is the same as Reflection #1: the learning should be transferable, specific, and visibly connected to the examination. It shouldn't be something you could have written before having the experience, and it shouldn't be something that could apply to any learner in any project.

"I learned that I work better under uncertainty when I have a clear next action" is more useful than "I learned to communicate more." One of those emerges from a specific examination. The other was available before the experience happened.

## What changes for your second reflection

In your first reflection, some cognitive effort went toward using the framework correctly — understanding what belongs in Describe versus Examine, calibrating how much detail is enough. That's no longer a factor.

The rubric dimensions are the same: Descriptive Specificity, Critical Examination, Personal Accountability, and Articulation of Learning, equally weighted. But the benchmark for each one has moved because you have a baseline. Your instructor will read this reflection with your first one in view. Growth is evidence of learning — staying at the same depth is not the same as "meeting the standard again."

The most common way reflections plateau between the first and second attempt: holding the same level of depth in the Examine stage because it was sufficient before. It was sufficient as a first attempt. This is a second attempt. Push the examination further than felt necessary last time.

Personal accountability — examining your own role, decisions, and assumptions rather than narrating events from outside — is where most first reflections fall short. If you placed yourself inside the experience in your first reflection, go deeper. If you didn't, this is the time to correct that.

## Using AI as a thinking partner

AI was introduced as a Socratic thinking partner for reflection earlier in the course, and this is its second application. The boundary is the same: AI cannot supply the experience, the examination, or the articulated learning. A reflection that could have been written by any learner in any project hasn't met the standard regardless of what tools were used.

Where AI can genuinely help is in deepening the Examine stage. Three specific approaches:

- **Surfacing unnamed assumptions.** Describe your experience to an AI tool and ask: "What assumptions might I be making that I haven't explicitly named?" Use what it identifies as a prompt for your own thinking — not as conclusions to copy.
- **Pushing back on surface-level examination.** Share a draft Examine section and ask: "Where am I still on the surface here? What's a harder question I could be asking myself?" The friction is the point.
- **Anticipating a skeptical reader.** Ask the AI to ask you the questions a skeptical reader would have after reading your draft. Then answer those questions in your revision — not by explaining yourself to the AI, but by going back into the document and addressing them directly.

In all three cases, the intellectual work is yours. AI helps you see where you haven't gone far enough yet.

## Format and submission

**Length:** 800–1200 words. Under 700 words rarely achieves sufficient depth in the Examine stage. Over 1,400 words usually indicates the description is too broad — pick one experience and go deeper, not wider.

**Structure:** Use the three DEAL stages as organizing sections. Headers are encouraged but not required — what matters is that a reader can tell where description ends and examination begins.

**Submission:** Individually through the Riipen platform.

**AI disclosure:** If you used AI at any point in your reflection process, include a brief description of what you used and how. If you did not use AI, a one-sentence statement to that effect is sufficient. Disclosure is required either way.

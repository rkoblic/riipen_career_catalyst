# Navigating disagreement

Production surfaces disagreements that earlier phases kept latent. Once teams start building, differences in approach, interpretation, and judgment become concrete and visible. How you handle those moments — with teammates and with the employer — is a skill that follows you far beyond any single project.

This module covers something specific: production-phase disagreement. It's different from the general collaboration norms covered earlier in the course. This is about what to do when a teammate's draft is going the wrong direction, or when the employer's Direction Check feedback doesn't match what your team believes would produce the best result.

---

## Why production surfaces disagreement

When the work was abstract — research notes, planning documents, discussions about what the deliverable might look like — most disagreements stayed latent. People had different mental models of what they were building, but those differences weren't visible enough to require resolution.

Once someone writes a draft or makes a concrete directional choice, the disagreement becomes specific. Now there's something to agree or disagree with.

The instinct in many teams is to avoid naming the disagreement to preserve harmony. But unspoken disagreements don't resolve themselves. They surface later as tension in team meetings, as a deliverable that doesn't cohere across sections, or as one person quietly reworking another person's section without explanation.

The professional skill is naming disagreements early, specifically, and without making them personal. Early disagreement is a working disagreement. Late disagreement — surfacing the week before submission — is a crisis.

---

## Disagreeing with teammates

Production-phase disagreements between teammates tend to fall into a few categories.

**Direction.** "I think this section is going the wrong way." This is a difference in interpretation of the brief, the research, or the employer feedback — you and your teammate read the same material and reached different conclusions about what the section should do.

**Quality.** "This draft doesn't meet the standard we agreed on." A difference in evaluation. You think the section is under-developed; your teammate may think it's sufficient.

**Workload.** "This section turned out to be much bigger than we thought — the current division isn't fair anymore." A structural or practical problem, not a judgment about anyone's work.

For all three: the approach is the same. Name what you're seeing specifically, and lead with a question before a conclusion.

"I noticed your section is organized around X, but I read the brief as asking for Y — am I missing something?" gives your teammate a chance to explain their reasoning. "I don't think this is right" doesn't.

Most production disagreements are about interpretation, not about one person being wrong. Leading with curiosity rather than evaluation keeps the conversation productive and preserves the relationship. In a professional context, that matters — you're still working together after the conversation.

---

## When employer direction feels wrong

Sometimes the Direction Check feedback doesn't match what your team believes would produce the best work. This is a different situation from disagreeing with a teammate — you're navigating a professional relationship where the other party has authority over the project.

**Default to the employer's direction** when it's a question of preference, format, or framing. They know their audience and their context better than you do. Receiving direction you didn't expect is not the same as receiving direction that's wrong.

**Push back gracefully** when your team has a specific reason — based on your research — to think a different approach would better serve the project goal. The framing matters enormously here:

"Based on what we found about [topic], we were thinking [approach] might address the goal more directly — but we wanted to check that with you before we proceed." This offers a perspective and invites dialogue. It doesn't position the employer as having made an error.

What "pushing back gracefully" is not: arguing, expressing frustration about the feedback in your team channel, going quiet and hoping the issue resolves itself, or unilaterally continuing with your original direction regardless of what the employer said.

**Document the decision.** Whatever your team decides — whether you follow the employer's direction as given or raise a question about it — note it. If the team proceeds under employer direction and someone had a concern, that concern should be in your own records. If the project takes an unexpected turn later, documentation of what was decided and why is worth having.

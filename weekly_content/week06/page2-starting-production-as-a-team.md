# Starting production as a team

Multi-week collaborative documents fail in a predictable way. Each team member writes their section in isolation, someone stitches the pieces together before submission, and the result reads as what it is: four independent drafts with different voices, inconsistent terminology, and mismatched formatting, combined rather than integrated. The failure happens at the start, not the end. The setup decisions your team makes before the first word goes into a shared document are the ones that determine whether your deliverable reads as a professional document or a collection of student work.

---

## Set up the document before anyone starts writing

When you produced the Context Analysis, you could run the full research → synthesis → draft → integration cycle in roughly one week. The Development phase deliverable is different. It will grow across multiple weeks, with sections at different stages simultaneously — some at outline, some at rough draft, some being revised based on employer feedback. That requires a different kind of coordination from the start.

Before your team begins drafting, make four decisions together:

**One shared document, not separate files.** Merging independently-maintained documents introduces formatting conflicts, duplicate content, and version control problems. One document, shared from day one, means everyone always sees the full picture — not just their section.

**Section ownership assigned before anyone starts writing.** Each section should have one owner who is responsible for the first draft and for keeping it current as the project evolves. This isn't the same as "only that person writes it" — other team members review and contribute. But without a named owner, sections stall or get written twice.

**Formatting standards applied before content is added.** The formatting page in this week's module gives you the specific standards. Apply them to the document template before anyone adds content. Retroactively reformatting a document that's already half-written is far more disruptive than setting it up correctly from the start.

**Agreed terminology documented.** What do you call your employer's organization? Their product or service? The population they serve? Inconsistent terminology across sections signals that a document was assembled, not authored. Decide on terms before drafting begins and make the list visible to the whole team.

---

## Map section dependencies

Some sections depend on others being developed first. Your strategic implications section can't be finalized until your research findings have a clear direction. Your executive summary can't be written until the rest of the document is stable. Other sections can proceed in parallel.

Spend a few minutes mapping these dependencies before production begins. Which sections need to be developed first? Which can proceed independently? Which sections will need to be revised once a dependent section is further along? Knowing this in advance prevents one team member from stalling because they're waiting on someone else's work to progress — or, worse, committing to a direction in one section that conflicts with a direction another section is moving toward.

---

## Use your team sync as a coordination tool

The team sync built into this week is an internal check-in — there's no employer involved. Use it for coordination, not reporting. Specifically:

- Surface blockers before they compound. If someone is stuck, the sync is where it gets named and resolved — not at the end of the week when the draft is due.
- Align on directions that affect multiple sections. If your research is shifting toward a particular finding, the team members whose sections depend on that finding need to know before they draft in the wrong direction.
- Catch divergence early. Two sections written in isolation often contradict each other in subtle ways that are hard to fix late. Brief alignment mid-week is easier than major revision at the end.

---

## Before you start drafting — setup checklist

- [ ] One shared document created; all team members have access and edit permissions
- [ ] Section ownership assigned — every section has a named owner before drafting begins
- [ ] Formatting page completed; agreed formatting standards applied to the document template before content is added
- [ ] Agreed terminology documented and visible to the full team (in the document, in a shared note, or in your Team Charter)
- [ ] Section dependencies mapped — which sections must be developed before others can be written, which can proceed in parallel
- [ ] Team sync scheduled before the end of the week

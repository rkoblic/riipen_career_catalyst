# What's next

The upcoming Work-in-Progress (WIP) presentation is where your drafts move from private to visible. It's the first time the employer sees where you are, which means this week's production work has a concrete audience and a concrete deadline.

---

## The Work-in-Progress presentation

The WIP presentation is a live accountability check — your team shows the employer where you stand on the deliverable and gets feedback before the final stretch of production. It's not graded on polish. It's graded on progress: can your team show meaningful forward movement?

That framing changes what "a good week" means right now. Teams who use this week well will arrive at the WIP with something substantive to discuss — a structured draft, a defensible direction, named gaps that the employer can help clarify. Teams who don't will find themselves scrambling to produce something presentable under time pressure that should have been used for revision.

Your goal for the rest of this week: get your assigned section to a point where you could talk through it in a meeting. Not perfect — presentable. Know what you've written, know what's still missing, and be able to explain your direction.

---

## What "presentable" means for a work-in-progress

A common misconception about WIP presentations is that the work needs to be polished to be shown. It doesn't.

A rough draft with a clear structure and a defensible direction is exactly what this stage calls for. Not perfect sentences — evidence that the team has been working and has something to show. The employer understands what a work-in-progress is. What they're looking for is forward movement, not a finished product.

A polished introduction and nothing behind it is less useful at this stage than a messy draft that covers your whole section. Focus on coverage this week. Polish is what revision is for.

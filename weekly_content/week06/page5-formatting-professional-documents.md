# Formatting professional documents

How a document is presented shapes how its content is received. A well-formatted document signals that its authors are organized and professional; an inconsistent one signals the opposite, before a reader has engaged with a single idea.

This module is placed at the start of the first production week deliberately. Formatting habits formed now will persist through the rest of the project. The internal review gate later in the course catches formatting problems, but catching them is different from preventing them — retroactively fixing an inconsistently formatted document under deadline pressure is far more costly than building consistent habits from the first draft. Read this before you open the shared document.

---

## Why formatting matters from the start

Readers make judgments about document quality before they read a word of it — based on visual hierarchy, white space, and consistency. For a team-authored document, inconsistent formatting communicates something specific: it signals that the sections were written independently and never integrated. Readers don't experience a team; they experience a document.

Two rubric dimensions are directly affected. Deliverable Quality — which includes presentation and visual coherence — is worth 30% of your final grade. Communication — which includes written clarity and professional presentation — is worth 15%. Formatting is the infrastructure that both dimensions depend on. Get it right from the start, before there's content to untangle it from.

---

## Practical standards

These aren't aesthetic preferences. They're the conventions that professional documents follow in most workplace contexts, and departures from them require a reason.

**Headings and hierarchy**

Use a consistent heading structure throughout the document. H1 for the document title, H2 for major sections, H3 for subsections. Don't use a heading level for visual emphasis if it doesn't represent a structural relationship — using H2 because you want something to look important, rather than because it's a major section, breaks the logic of the hierarchy and confuses navigation.

**White space**

Separate paragraphs with space. Walls of text signal that a document hasn't been edited for readability — even before a reader tries to parse the content. Keep paragraphs short: four to six lines is a reasonable target. If a paragraph is running longer, look for the natural break.

**Tables**

Use tables to compare multiple items across consistent attributes. Not to present a single list — a list is cleaner for that. Every table needs a brief descriptive title placed above it. A table without a title forces the reader to infer what they're looking at.

**Charts and visuals**

Every chart needs a title and clear axis labels. If a visual came from another source, cite it — same standard as written content. Before including a visual, ask: does this communicate something more clearly than a well-written sentence or two would? If the answer is no, the sentence is probably the better choice. A visual that requires a paragraph of explanation to interpret hasn't done its job.

**Callout boxes**

Useful for key findings, important caveats, or information a reader might want to reference without reading the surrounding section. Use them sparingly. If everything is called out, nothing is.

**Terminology and style**

Decide on terms before drafting begins and coordinate across the team. If the employer calls their product a "platform," use "platform" throughout — not "tool," "system," or "solution." Inconsistent terminology doesn't just look careless; it raises real questions for a professional reader about whether the document's authors understand what they're describing.

## Formatting checklist

Use this before submitting your section to the shared document, and again when the team merges sections.

- [ ] Heading hierarchy is consistent throughout the document (H1 → H2 → H3, no levels skipped)
- [ ] No heading is used purely for visual emphasis without representing a structural relationship
- [ ] Paragraph spacing is consistent throughout
- [ ] Paragraphs are 6 lines or fewer; longer paragraphs are split at natural breaks
- [ ] All tables have descriptive titles placed above them
- [ ] All charts have titles and clearly labeled axes
- [ ] All data sources are cited for visuals and statistics
- [ ] Callout boxes are used sparingly — no more than one per section
- [ ] Terminology is consistent throughout (coordinate with your team before drafting)
- [ ] The document uses the same font and font size throughout
- [ ] Page margins are consistent
- [ ] All team members' sections use the same heading styles (verify before merging)
- [ ] Each section ends with a transition or bridge to what follows — no abrupt stops
- [ ] All lists use parallel grammatical structure (all items begin with the same part of speech)
- [ ] Capitalization is consistent within headings and lists
- [ ] Acronyms are defined on first use
- [ ] Numbers under ten are spelled out; numbers 10 and above use numerals (unless a different convention applies to your document type)
- [ ] The document has been reviewed in print preview or PDF view before submission
- [ ] Running headers or footers, if used, are consistent throughout
- [ ] Page numbers are present

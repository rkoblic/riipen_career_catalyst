# From research to draft

Most professional writing doesn't start from a blank page — it starts from notes, documents, and the gap between "I have a lot of material" and "I know what I'm going to say." Closing that gap is where the real analytical work happens, and it's the step most people either rush or skip entirely.

---

## Why people stall between research and writing

Research and writing are different cognitive modes. Research is accumulative and exploratory — you're gathering, following leads, adding to what you know. Writing is selective and argumentative — you're deciding what matters, what order it goes in, and what claim you're making.

The step between them is synthesis: the process of finding the structure in your material before you commit to prose. Synthesis is easy to skip. When you skip it, you get one of two things: a data dump (everything you found, in the order you found it) or paralysis in front of a blank document (because you haven't yet figured out what you're going to say).

The notes → outline → draft sequence is a discipline, not a suggestion. Each step is genuinely different from the one before it, and trying to collapse them saves no time — it just moves the difficulty later, when you're more attached to what you've already written.

---

## From notes to outline: finding the structure in your material

Before you can draft, you need to know what you're drafting. This four-step process turns a set of research notes into an outline you can actually write from.

1. **Review your notes once before organizing.** Read through everything you've collected without trying to sort it yet. You're getting a sense of the landscape — what you know, what keeps coming up, what feels central versus peripheral. Don't highlight or annotate on this pass. Just read.

2. **Identify recurring ideas and themes.** Look for patterns across your notes: ideas that appear in multiple sources, concepts that connect things you wouldn't have grouped together, questions that your research keeps circling back to. These recurring patterns are your sections — they come from your material, not from a predetermined structure you're trying to fill.

3. **Label each cluster with a working name.** Give each group of related ideas a name that describes the idea, not the source. "Three studies on pricing transparency" is a source label. "How pricing transparency affects trust" is an idea label. The distinction matters: when you draft, you're writing about ideas, not reporting sources.

4. **Look for what's missing.** Once you've clustered everything you have, ask: what would a reader of this section want to know that my material doesn't answer? Gaps identified at the outline stage are fixable with a few minutes of targeted research. Gaps identified mid-draft cost significantly more.

---

## Is your outline ready to draft from?

An outline that looks complete isn't always one you can write from. Before you start producing prose, ask three questions:

- **What is this section's main claim or purpose?** If you can't state it in one sentence — not a topic, a claim or purpose — the outline isn't ready. You don't yet know what the section is for.
- **Is the order defensible?** Not just logical, but defensible: can you articulate why A comes before B? If the order is arbitrary, a reader will feel it.
- **Are the transitions obvious?** What connects each part to the next? If the answer is "they're just in the same section," that's not a transition — it's proximity. A strong outline already knows how its parts connect.

If you can't answer all three, the outline needs more work. The most common production mistake is starting to write too early and having to restructure once you're already attached to what you've written.

---

## Moving from outline to prose

The first draft's job is to get your ideas into a form you can work with, not a form you'd submit. Hold that standard and the blank page gets less intimidating.

- **Write sections in order.** Working through your outline sequentially maintains logical flow and surfaces any setup that's missing — if section three requires something established in section two and you skipped it, writing in order catches that.
- **Skip the introduction until the end.** Introductions are genuinely hard to write before you know what you're introducing. Draft the body first, then come back.
- **Don't edit while drafting.** Read for comprehension — make sure you understand what you just wrote — but hold language-level edits for the revision pass. Editing while drafting interrupts flow and produces slower, not better, writing.
- **Use placeholders.** When you hit a gap, mark it and keep moving: `[EXPAND THIS]`, `[NEED DATA HERE]`, `[TRANSITION]`. A draft full of named gaps is more useful than a draft that stops at the first one.

## Using AI in the notes-to-draft process

AI can reduce the structural overhead of synthesis without doing the analysis for you. Three specific uses:

**Clustering raw notes**

Paste your notes into an AI tool and ask: *"Here are my raw research notes on [topic]: [notes]. Help me identify the three to five main themes or clusters across these notes. Don't summarize — just group them and label each cluster."*

What this does: speeds up the sorting step. What it doesn't do: generate the analysis or tell you what to say about those clusters. That stays with you.

**Stress-testing the outline**

Once you have a draft outline, ask: *"Here is my outline for my assigned section: [outline]. What gaps do you see? What questions would a reader have after reading this section that the outline doesn't seem to answer?"*

Use the response as a prompt for your own judgment — not a list of instructions. Some of what AI flags will be real gaps. Some will reflect assumptions about your audience or context that it can't make for you.

**Checking sequence**

Before you start drafting, ask: *"I'm about to start drafting from this outline. Is there anything in the structure that seems out of order or that would be confusing to a reader?"*

Again: take the feedback as input, not direction. You know your content and your audience better than a general-purpose tool does.

In all three cases, the intellectual work stays yours. AI helps you see structure. It doesn't build it.

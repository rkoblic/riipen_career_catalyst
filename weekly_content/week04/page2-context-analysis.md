# Context Analysis & Project Plan

Every deliverable you produce in a professional context answers a version of the same question: what do you know, what does it mean, and what should we do about it? The Context Analysis & Project Plan is where you answer that question for the first time in this course.

This page walks you through each part of the deliverable, the thinking behind it, and how to produce it as a team.

---

## Part 1: Organizational and project context

This section establishes that you understand the organization and project well enough to analyze them. It's not a restatement of the brief your employer gave you. It's a demonstration that you've gone beyond the brief and developed genuine working knowledge.

Think of it this way: anyone on your team could hand over the brief. This section should show that you've thought about it, not just read it.

### What to include

Cover the organization itself (what it does, who it serves, where it sits in its sector), the project scope as confirmed during the kickoff meeting, and the key stakeholders your team has identified. If the kickoff revealed nuances that weren't in the original brief, those belong here. Differences between the brief and what you heard directly from the employer are some of the most valuable things you can document.

### Restating vs. demonstrating understanding

The clearest sign of a weak context section is that it reads like a paraphrase of the project brief. A strong one adds information, makes connections, and shows that the team engaged with the material. If you removed the project brief from the equation, could someone still learn something new from your context section? That's the standard to aim for.

## Part 2: Research findings

This is the section where most teams either shine or stumble. The difference almost always comes down to one thing: whether you summarized what you found or synthesized what it means.

[VIDEO: From "what we found" to "what it means"]

### Synthesizing research into insights

Synthesis means identifying patterns across your findings and drawing conclusions from them. It's the difference between "Company X has 40% market share" and "The market is dominated by a single player, which constrains new entrants and shapes what our employer can realistically achieve."

A few concrete moves that help:

- **Look for patterns across sources.** When multiple pieces of research point in the same direction, that's a finding worth highlighting. When they contradict each other, that's also worth naming.
- **Draw conclusions, not just observations.** After stating what you found, say what it means for the project. Every finding should connect to an implication, even if the implication is "this area needs more investigation."
- **Identify gaps honestly.** If there's something important you couldn't find or couldn't verify, say so. Acknowledging gaps is a sign of intellectual honesty, not weakness. It tells the reader you know where the limits of your research are.
- **Avoid the "everything we found" trap.** Not every piece of research belongs in the final document. Include what matters for the analysis you're building, not everything your team happened to read.

### What counts as evidence

In professional analysis, there's a meaningful difference between a claim you've supported and a claim you've asserted. "The target demographic skews younger" is an assertion. "According to the organization's most recent annual report, 68% of program participants are under 25" is a supported claim.

Your research section should demonstrate that you know the difference. Where you have strong evidence, cite it. Where your evidence is thinner, qualify your language. "Our research suggests..." and "Based on limited available data..." are honest framing devices that signal you're reasoning carefully, not guessing.

Uncertainty is not a weakness in professional analysis. Pretending to be certain when you're not is.

[CURATED LINK: How professionals structure research findings — https://youtu.be/Jtx01xQNw5A?si=I9dnEEdQgSWEBXX6]

### Common pitfalls

**The data dump.** The section reads like a list of everything the team found, organized by who researched what rather than by what it means. Fix: organize by theme or implication, not by team member or source.

**The unsupported leap.** The section jumps from a few data points to sweeping conclusions without showing the reasoning in between. Fix: make each step of the logic visible. "We found X. This suggests Y, because Z."

**The missing "so what."** Findings are presented clearly but never connected to what they mean for the project. Fix: every finding should link to a reason the reader should care about it in the context of this specific project.

**The echo chamber.** The section only includes research that supports the team's preferred approach, ignoring evidence that complicates or contradicts it. Fix: include counterevidence and explain how you weighed it.

## Part 3: Strategic implications

[VIDEO: The "so what" move]

### Naming assumptions and constraints

Every strategic recommendation rests on assumptions, and the strongest analyses name them explicitly. What are you assuming about the organization's capacity, audience, timeline, or priorities? What constraints does your research reveal that the brief didn't mention?

Being explicit about assumptions does two things. It shows your employer that you've thought carefully about the conditions under which your approach will work. And it gives them a clear entry point to tell you if something you're assuming is wrong, which is exactly the kind of feedback you want before investing weeks of production work.

### Connecting findings to your proposed approach

This is where you make the case for what your team plans to do and why. Every recommendation should trace back to something you found in your research. If you can't point to specific evidence that supports a recommendation, it's either premature or unsupported.

The logic should be transparent: "Our research found X. This means Y is likely true. Given that, we recommend Z." When readers can follow your reasoning from evidence to conclusion to recommendation, they trust the analysis, even if they disagree with the recommendation itself.

## Part 4: Project plan

A project plan is not a wishlist of everything your team could do. It's a set of deliberate decisions about what you will do, what you won't, and why. The quality of a project plan shows up in what it leaves out as much as what it includes.

### Making tradeoffs explicit

Every project has more work available than time to do it. Your plan should show that you've made conscious tradeoff decisions rather than trying to do everything. Name what you're prioritizing and what you're deprioritizing, and explain the reasoning behind those choices.

"We will focus on X rather than Y because our research indicates X has a higher potential impact given the organization's current priorities" is a strong planning statement. "We will do X, Y, and Z" with no explanation of why those were chosen over other options is a weak one.

### Milestones and timeline

Your timeline should reflect your team's actual capacity, not an aspirational version of what you could accomplish if everything went perfectly. Map your milestones to real dates, account for the other demands on your time this semester, and build in realistic buffers.

A timeline that looks aggressive but slips every week is less useful than a conservative timeline your team can actually meet. Your employer would rather know what to reliably expect than be told what they want to hear.

### Risk identification and mitigation

Things will go wrong. The question is whether your team has thought about what might go wrong and has a plan for when it does.

Risk identification means naming the specific things that could derail or degrade your project. Not vague concerns ("something might go wrong") but concrete risks with real consequences ("our primary data source requires access approval that may take two weeks, which would delay the research phase").

For each risk, think about two dimensions:

- **Likelihood.** How probable is this? Is it something that happens routinely, or would it require unusual circumstances?
- **Impact.** If it happens, how badly does it affect the project? A small delay versus a fundamental change in approach?

Risks that are both likely and high-impact deserve mitigation strategies: concrete steps your team will take to either reduce the probability or limit the damage. Risks that are unlikely and low-impact can be noted and monitored without a detailed plan.

## Using AI for research and synthesis

AI can be a legitimate tool for research and synthesis work, and this week is a good opportunity to use it thoughtfully.

### Where AI adds value

**Landscape research.** AI can generate an initial overview of a competitive landscape, industry trends, or sector dynamics. This gives your team a starting point, not a finished product. Treat AI-generated overviews the way you'd treat a briefing from a colleague who did a quick scan: useful for orientation, but you need to verify the specifics and fill in what it missed.

**Cross-team synthesis.** When your team members have each researched different areas, AI can help identify patterns and connections across those findings. Feed it your team's compiled notes and ask it to surface the most important themes, tensions, or contradictions.

**Important:** AI's training data has a cutoff date and may not reflect recent developments. For anything time-sensitive (recent organizational changes, current market conditions, new product launches), go to primary sources directly. Verification often takes as long as just looking it up yourself.

### Example prompts

If you're using AI for competitive landscape research:

> "I am researching [industry/sector] for a consulting project. Generate an overview of the key players, what they offer, and how they differentiate. Flag any claims I should verify independently."

If you're using AI to synthesize across team members' research:

> "Here are research findings from four team members on [topic]. Identify the three to five most important patterns or tensions across these findings, and explain what each one might mean for how we approach the project."

Both of these position AI as a starting point that requires your judgment, not a finishing point you can submit without thinking.

### The accountability mechanism

There's a practical reason to engage genuinely with AI-generated content rather than passing it through uncritically: the Direction Check. In an upcoming session, your employer will have read this document and will ask your team questions about it. If you can't explain a finding, defend a claim, or walk through the reasoning behind a recommendation, the conversation will make that visible quickly.

The standard from the course AI policy applies here: could you explain and defend every claim in your submission without referring back to what an AI generated for you? If the answer is no, that content shouldn't be in the document.

## Production workflow

### From research to finished document

Producing a strong team document doesn't happen by dividing sections, writing in isolation, and stapling the parts together. Here's a workflow that produces better results:

1. **Individual research.** Each team member takes responsibility for specific research areas. Divide by topic, not by document section, so your research has depth.
2. **Team synthesis meeting.** Come together to share what you found. The goal isn't to present polished summaries; it's to get your findings into the room so the team can think together about what they mean. This is where you agree on the strategic direction before anyone starts drafting.
3. **Individual drafting.** With a shared direction agreed on, each person drafts their assigned sections. This works better after synthesis because everyone is writing toward the same argument.
4. **Async cross-review.** Before integration, each team member reads at least one other person's section. Look for gaps, inconsistencies, unsupported claims, and places where sections contradict each other.
5. **Integration pass.** This is where the document goes from four pieces to one. Someone (or the whole team) reads the full document end to end and fixes transitions, tone shifts, redundancies, and contradictions.
6. **Final review and sign-off.** Everyone reads the final version and confirms it represents the team's thinking. Then submit.

### The integration pass

Most team documents read like exactly what they are: separate pieces written by different people. The integration pass is what turns a collection of sections into a coherent professional document.

During the integration pass, look for:

- **Voice shifts.** Does the writing style change noticeably between sections? Smooth it out so the document reads as though one person wrote it.
- **Transitions.** Does each section connect logically to the next? Add bridging sentences where the reader would otherwise feel a jump.
- **Redundancies.** Did two sections make the same point? Consolidate.
- **Contradictions.** Does the research section claim one thing while the strategic implications section assumes another? Resolve it.

AI can be a useful editor for this step. Try asking it to identify inconsistencies, flag unclear transitions, or highlight places where sections seem to contradict each other.

## Submission

### Deliverable format

Your Context Analysis & Project Plan should have four parts:

- **Part 1: Organizational & project context** (~1 page)
- **Part 2: Research findings** (~1.5–2 pages)
- **Part 3: Strategic implications** (~1 page)
- **Part 4: Project plan** (~1.5–2 pages)

Total length is approximately 5–6 pages. This is a guideline, not a hard limit. Substance matters more than page count.

### How you'll be assessed

The deliverable is assessed on five dimensions:

- **Communication quality (20%).** Clarity of writing, logical organization, professional tone. Does the document read well?
- **Analytical rigor (25%).** Quality of research, strength of evidence, soundness of reasoning. Did you analyze or just summarize?
- **Actionability (20%).** Are the recommendations specific and feasible? Could someone act on this plan?
- **Professionalism (15%).** Presentation quality, attention to detail, consistency of formatting and voice across sections.
- **Project-specific dimension (20%).** An assessment dimension tailored to the specific analytical work your project requires. This dimension recognizes that a team analyzing youth banking trends faces different analytical challenges than a team examining supply chain logistics.

The deliverable is assessed on the quality of your research and reasoning. If the employer later redirects your approach at the Direction Check, that doesn't reflect poorly on this submission. Pivots based on employer feedback are expected and are evidence of responsiveness, not failure.

### AI disclosure

Include an AI disclosure statement with your submission, regardless of whether AI was used. If AI was not used, a single sentence stating that is sufficient. If AI was used, briefly describe how (e.g., "AI was used to generate an initial competitive landscape overview, which the team verified and expanded through primary source research").

### How to submit

Submit your completed Context Analysis & Project Plan through the Riipen platform. One submission per team. Ensure all team members have reviewed and approved the final document before submission.

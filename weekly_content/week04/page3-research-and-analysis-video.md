# Video Script: From "what we found" to "what it means"

There's a common pattern in first-time analysts' reports: they do good research, find useful information, and then present it as a list of things they found. That's a summary. It's not analysis.

The shift you need to make is from reporting what you found to explaining what it means — identifying patterns, drawing conclusions, and connecting everything to what it means for the project.

Here's what this looks like in practice, because the difference is easier to see in examples than in definitions.

Say your team is working on a social media strategy for a small business. You've researched the competitive landscape and you write: "Competitor A has 12,000 Instagram followers. Competitor B has 8,500 followers and posts three times a week. Competitor C launched a TikTok account six months ago."

That's a summary. It's accurate, but it doesn't tell the reader anything about what it means. Now here's the same research, synthesized: "All three direct competitors have shifted toward short-form video content in the past year, and the two that did it earliest have seen the strongest follower growth. This suggests the audience in this space responds to video, and our employer's current approach of static image posts may be falling behind a format shift that's already underway."

See the difference? Same research. But the second version identifies a pattern, draws a conclusion, and connects it to what it means for the project.

Here's another one. Say your team is working on a community outreach program for a nonprofit, and you've researched participation barriers. Summary version: "A 2023 study found that 40% of youth in the target area don't participate in after-school programs. The organization currently promotes programs through school flyers and their website."

Synthesized version: "Published research consistently identifies transportation and scheduling conflicts, not awareness, as the primary barriers to youth program participation in communities like this one. The organization's current outreach relies on flyers and their website, which assume the problem is that families don't know about the programs. But if the real barrier is getting there, increasing awareness won't move the needle. This suggests the project should focus on accessibility, not marketing."

That version does three things the summary didn't. It identifies a pattern across research. It names a gap between what the research says and what the organization is currently doing. And it draws a conclusion about what that means for the project direction.

One more. Say your team is working on process improvement and you've mapped the fulfillment workflow. Summary: "Orders take an average of 47 minutes from pick to ship. The packing station has a manual queue managed on a shared spreadsheet. Three staff members handle packing during peak hours."

Synthesized: "Our workflow mapping shows that the picking stage takes a consistent 12 minutes per order, but time-to-pack varies wildly, from 8 minutes to over 30. The variation traces to the manual queue: when multiple orders finish picking simultaneously, they stack up at the packing station with no system for prioritization. During peak hours, packers spend almost as much time figuring out what to pack next as they spend actually packing. This bottleneck accounts for roughly 60% of the total processing time variation."

The summary gives you facts. The synthesis tells you where the problem actually lives and why.

There are a few concrete moves that help you make this shift as you write. Look for patterns across your sources. When multiple findings point in the same direction, that's worth highlighting. When they contradict each other, that's also worth naming. Draw conclusions, not just observations. Every finding should connect to an implication, even if the implication is "we need to investigate this further." Identify gaps honestly. If you couldn't find something important, say so. That tells the reader you know where the limits of your research are. And resist the urge to include everything. Not every piece of research belongs in the final document. Include what matters for the argument you're building, not everything your team happened to read.

The research findings section is where most teams either shine or stumble. The difference almost always comes down to this one shift: did you tell the reader what you found, or did you tell them what it means?

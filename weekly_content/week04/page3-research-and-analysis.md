# Research and analysis

This page covers the first two parts of the Context Analysis & Project Plan: organizational and project context (Part 1) and research findings (Part 2). These are the sections where you demonstrate what you know and what you've learned.

---

## Part 1: Organizational and project context

### Why this section exists

Your employer already knows their own organization. So why are they asking you to write about it? Because this section answers a question they can't help but have: *does this team actually understand what we do and what we need?*

If the answer is yes, the employer trusts the rest of the document. If the answer is no — if the context section reads like a paraphrase of the brief or gets basic facts wrong — everything that follows is undermined, no matter how good it is. This section earns the credibility that the rest of the document depends on.

It's also where you demonstrate that you were listening during the kickoff. The brief said one thing; the conversation may have revealed something different. Capturing that difference shows the employer that your team is paying attention, not just going through the motions.

### What to include

Cover the organization itself (what it does, who it serves, where it sits in its sector), the project scope as confirmed during the kickoff meeting, and the key stakeholders your team has identified. If the kickoff revealed nuances that weren't in the original brief, those belong here. Differences between the brief and what you heard directly from the employer are some of the most valuable things you can document.

For example, the brief might say the project is about "improving customer engagement." But during the kickoff, the employer mentioned that their biggest challenge is actually retaining first-time users after the initial sign-up. That's a much more specific problem, and naming it here shows you understood the real need, not just the headline.

### What this looks like across different projects

The specifics of Part 1 depend on your project, but the purpose is always the same: show the employer you understand their world well enough to do useful work in it.

- **Social media strategy:** Go beyond "the business sells handmade jewelry online." Describe the business's current social media presence, who their customers are, and what the employer said at the kickoff about what's working and what isn't. If the employer mentioned that they get most of their sales from Instagram DMs but have almost no presence on TikTok, that's the kind of detail that shows you were paying attention.
- **Community outreach:** Don't just restate the nonprofit's mission. Describe the specific community they serve, the programs they currently run, and what the employer told you about participation challenges. If the kickoff revealed that their biggest barrier isn't awareness but transportation access, that changes everything about how you frame the project.
- **Process improvement:** Describe the operation in enough detail that a reader understands how it works today before you start analyzing what's broken. If the employer mentioned that the current fulfillment process was designed for 50 orders a day but they're now processing 200, that context is essential.

### Restating vs. demonstrating understanding

The clearest sign of a weak context section is that it reads like a paraphrase of the project brief. A strong one adds information, makes connections, and shows that the team engaged with the material.

Compare these two approaches:

- **Restating:** "The organization is a nonprofit that provides mentorship programs to youth in underserved communities."
- **Demonstrating understanding:** "The organization runs mentorship programs in three urban regions, primarily serving youth ages 14–19. At the kickoff, the employer noted that their fastest-growing region is also the one with the lowest mentor retention rate, which directly shapes the challenge our project needs to address."

The second version shows the team did more than read — they connected what they heard to what it means. If you removed the project brief from the equation, could someone still learn something new from your context section? That's the standard to aim for.

## Part 2: Research findings

### Why this section exists

Your employer knows their organization from the inside. What they often don't have is an outside perspective — someone looking at their competitive landscape, their sector trends, or their audience with fresh eyes and no internal assumptions. That's what you're providing here.

This section is where the employer sees whether your team brought something new to the table. Research findings that just confirm what the employer already knows are safe but not useful. Findings that surface something the employer hadn't considered, or that challenge an assumption they've been operating on, are what make this deliverable worth reading.

It's also where you build the foundation for everything in Parts 3 and 4. Your strategic implications and project plan are only as strong as the research behind them. If this section is thin, the rest of the document is guesswork.

### Synthesizing research into insights

The video above walks through what synthesis looks like in practice, with examples across different project types. Here's a quick recap of the key moves to keep in mind as you write:

- **Look for patterns across sources.** When multiple findings point in the same direction, highlight it. When they contradict, name that too.
- **Draw conclusions, not just observations.** Every finding should connect to an implication for the project — what does this mean for what you'll do?
- **Identify gaps honestly.** If you couldn't find or verify something important, say so. That's intellectual honesty, not weakness.
- **Be selective.** Not everything you found belongs in the document. Include what matters for the argument you're building.

### What research looks like across different projects

The kind of research that matters depends entirely on the project. The skill — synthesizing findings into insights — is the same, but what you're looking for is different.

- **Social media strategy:** Research the target audience's online behavior, the platforms where similar businesses have traction, and what content formats perform best in this space. Look at what competitors are doing on social media — not to copy them, but to understand what the audience already responds to. If three competing businesses are all seeing strong engagement with short-form video and the employer is still posting static images, that's a pattern worth naming.
- **Community outreach:** Research similar programs in comparable communities — what approaches have worked, what hasn't, and why. Look for barriers to participation that go beyond awareness: transportation, scheduling conflicts, cultural factors, trust. If published research on youth program engagement consistently identifies peer recruitment as the strongest driver and the employer's current model relies entirely on flyers, that's a meaningful finding.
- **Process improvement:** Map the current workflow and identify where delays and errors concentrate. Look at industry benchmarks or case studies from similar operations. If the employer's order error rate is 8% and industry standard is under 2%, that's a data point that frames the rest of your analysis. Talk to (or read feedback from) the people who actually do the work — they usually know where the problems are before anyone else does.

### What counts as evidence

In professional analysis, there's a meaningful difference between a claim you've supported and a claim you've asserted. "The target demographic skews younger" is an assertion. "According to the organization's most recent annual report, 68% of program participants are under 25" is a supported claim.

Your research section should demonstrate that you know the difference. Where you have strong evidence, cite it. Where your evidence is thinner, qualify your language. "Our research suggests..." and "Based on limited available data..." are honest framing devices that signal you're reasoning carefully, not guessing.

Uncertainty is not a weakness in professional analysis. Pretending to be certain when you're not is.

[CURATED LINK: "The Pyramid Principle" (video, ~10 min) — Communicate with IMPACT — https://youtu.be/Jtx01xQNw5A?si=I9dnEEdQgSWEBXX6] This video walks through McKinsey's Pyramid Principle — a framework for structuring any argument or analysis. The core idea: lead with your main point, support it with 2–3 key subpoints, then back each subpoint with evidence. It also covers the MECE principle (mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive), which helps you organize findings into clear, non-overlapping categories. This is directly relevant to structuring your research findings section — it's the difference between a data dump and a well-organized argument that a reader can follow.

### Common pitfalls

**The data dump.** The section reads like a list of everything the team found, organized by who researched what rather than by what it means. Fix: organize by theme or implication, not by team member or source.

**The unsupported leap.** The section jumps from a few data points to sweeping conclusions without showing the reasoning in between. Fix: make each step of the logic visible. "We found X. This suggests Y, because Z."

**The missing "so what."** Findings are presented clearly but never connected to what they mean for the project. Fix: every finding should link to a reason the reader should care about it in the context of this specific project.

**The echo chamber.** The section only includes research that supports the team's preferred approach, ignoring evidence that complicates or contradicts it. Fix: include counterevidence and explain how you weighed it.

## Using AI for research

AI can be a legitimate tool for research work, and this week is a good opportunity to use it thoughtfully. But it's worth being clear about what AI does and doesn't do for your research quality.

AI makes skilled researchers more efficient. It helps you scan a landscape faster, identify patterns across sources, and organize what you've found. But it doesn't make unskilled researchers skilled. If you don't know what a strong competitive analysis looks like, AI will produce something that *looks* like one, which is actually more dangerous than having nothing, because it gives you false confidence in work you can't evaluate or defend.

The research skills you've been building over the past few weeks (identifying what matters, reasoning from evidence, connecting findings to implications) are what make AI useful here. Without those skills, AI output is just plausible-sounding text. With them, AI output is raw material you can evaluate, shape, and build on.

### Where AI adds value

**Landscape research.** AI can generate an initial overview of a competitive landscape, industry trends, or sector dynamics. This gives your team a starting point, not a finished product. Treat AI-generated overviews the way you'd treat a briefing from a colleague who did a quick scan: useful for orientation, but you need to verify the specifics and fill in what it missed.

**Cross-team synthesis.** When your team members have each researched different areas, AI can help identify patterns and connections across those findings. Feed it your team's compiled notes and ask it to surface the most important themes, tensions, or contradictions.

**Important:** AI's training data has a cutoff date and may not reflect recent developments. For anything time-sensitive (recent organizational changes, current market conditions, new product launches), go to primary sources directly. Verification often takes as long as just looking it up yourself.

### Example prompts

If you're using AI for landscape research:

> "I am researching [industry/sector] for a consulting project. Generate an overview of the key players, what they offer, and how they differentiate. Flag any claims I should verify independently."

If you're using AI to synthesize across team members' research:

> "Here are research findings from four team members on [topic]. Identify the three to five most important patterns or tensions across these findings, and explain what each one might mean for how we approach the project."

Both of these position AI as a starting point that requires your judgment, not a finishing point you can submit without thinking.

### The accountability mechanism

There's a practical reason to engage genuinely with AI-generated content rather than passing it through uncritically: the Direction Check. In an upcoming session, your employer will have read this document and will ask your team questions about it. If you can't explain a finding, defend a claim, or walk through the reasoning behind a recommendation, the conversation will make that visible quickly.

The standard from the course AI policy applies here: could you explain and defend every claim in your submission without referring back to what an AI generated for you? If the answer is no, that content shouldn't be in the document.

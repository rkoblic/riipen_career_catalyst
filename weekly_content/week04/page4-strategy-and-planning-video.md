# Video Script: The "so what" move

You've done your research. Now: what does it mean, and what should you do about it?

This is where teams get stuck, because making the leap to a recommendation feels risky. But the point isn't to be right. It's to show your thinking clearly enough that the employer can react to it. Here's what that looks like.

Say your team is working on a social media strategy. Your research found that three direct competitors have shifted to short-form video in the past year and the two that moved first saw the strongest audience growth. The employer is still posting static images. Here's how you build the chain:

Finding: competitors who adopted short-form video saw significantly stronger engagement and follower growth. The employer's current content format doesn't match what the audience responds to in this space.

What it means: there's a format shift happening in this market, and the employer is behind it. The gap isn't about posting frequency or content quality. It's about format.

What we're assuming: the employer has the willingness and capacity to create video content, or is open to learning. We're also assuming the audience behavior we observed in competitors' accounts is transferable to the employer's audience.

What we recommend: a 90-day short-form video pilot on TikTok and Instagram Reels, starting with two posts per week, with performance benchmarks set at 30 and 60 days.

Notice what happened there. The recommendation traces directly back to the research. The assumptions are named explicitly. And the employer now has something concrete to react to. They might say "we can't do video, we don't have the skills." Great — now you know, and you can adjust your strategy before you've spent weeks building a plan around it.

Now community outreach. Your research found that published studies consistently identify transportation and scheduling, not awareness, as the primary barriers to youth program participation. The employer's current model relies on flyers and website promotion.

Finding: the research suggests the real barrier isn't that families don't know about the programs. It's that they can't get to them.

What it means: increasing marketing and awareness — which is what the current model focuses on — probably won't move the needle on participation. The problem is structural, not informational.

What we're assuming: the specific barriers identified in published research apply to this community. We haven't confirmed this with local data or direct input from the families the organization serves.

What we recommend: a satellite-location pilot at community centers accessible by public transit, paired with adjusted programming times that reflect when the target youth population is actually available.

See how naming the assumption — that published research applies here — makes this stronger, not weaker? The employer might say "actually, we ran a survey last year and transportation wasn't the top issue in our area." That's incredibly valuable feedback. You'd rather hear it now than after you've built an entire strategy around the wrong barrier.

One more. Process improvement. Your workflow mapping found that 60% of fulfillment time variation happens at the picking-to-packing handoff, where a manual spreadsheet queue creates bottlenecks during peak hours.

Finding: the bottleneck isn't in picking or packing individually. It's in the handoff between them.

What it means: optimizing either stage in isolation won't solve the problem. The queue management system is the constraint.

What we're assuming: the employer's inventory management software can integrate with a digital queue tool. We're also assuming the packing team would adopt a new system — they've been using the spreadsheet for two years.

What we recommend: replace the manual queue with a digital assignment system that auto-routes orders to available packers, starting with a two-week pilot during off-peak hours to test adoption before rolling out during peak.

In every example, the same three moves: connect findings to a conclusion, name what you're assuming, and propose something concrete. Vague recommendations like "we suggest a more targeted approach" give the employer nothing to work with. Be specific. And if your recommendation turns out to be wrong, that's fine. The Direction Check exists so the employer can redirect you. A specific recommendation that needs adjusting is far more useful than a vague one that can't be evaluated.

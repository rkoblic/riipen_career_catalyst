# Reflection #1

Professionals who grow fastest in their careers share a common habit: they don't just have experiences, they learn from them deliberately. A project debrief, a difficult client conversation, a team dynamic that worked unexpectedly well — the people who extract transferable lessons from these moments build a compounding advantage over time. This reflection is your first structured practice at doing exactly that.

---

## The assignment

Write a DEAL reflection drawing from your experience in the first few weeks of the course. The kickoff meeting is a natural anchor, but you're not limited to it. Any moment where you learned something, were surprised, made a mistake, or noticed something about how you work is valid material.

This is an individual submission, worth approximately 5% of your final grade as part of the Reflection Portfolio. The course values depth and honesty over polish. A thoughtful first attempt that engages genuinely with what happened matters more than a perfectly structured essay that stays on the surface.

---

## DEAL framework reference

You were introduced to the DEAL model earlier in the course. Here's a quick reference for applying it to this reflection.

**Describe.** Give a clear, specific account of what happened. Not your interpretation, not your feelings about it — just the facts. Who was involved, what was the context, what occurred. Good description is more concrete than most people expect. If someone who wasn't there couldn't picture the situation from your description, it's not specific enough.

**Examine.** This is where the real thinking happens. Look at the experience through a critical lens. What assumptions did you bring to the situation? How did those assumptions shape what happened? What would have been different if you'd approached it another way? Push past surface reactions ("it went well" or "it was frustrating") and ask why.

**Articulate Learning.** Name what you learned and explain how you'll apply it going forward. The standard here is specificity and transferability. "I learned the importance of preparation" doesn't meet the bar. "I learned that prioritizing questions by what we genuinely don't know, rather than listing everything we're curious about, leads to more focused meetings. Going forward, I'll sort my team's question list by urgency before any client interaction" does.

Each DEAL stage addresses a specific way informal reflection goes wrong. Most people skip from what happened straight to what they'll do differently, skipping the analysis entirely. Describe forces accuracy. Examine forces genuine analysis. Articulate Learning forces you to name something concrete and transferable. The structure is the point.

## Using AI as a thinking partner

AI can be a useful sounding board for reflection, but it can't reflect for you. The experience is yours, the examination has to come from your own thinking, and the learning has to be something you actually articulate.

Where AI can help: if you're stuck, try describing your experience to an AI tool and asking it to push back. "What assumptions might I be making here?" or "What's another way to interpret what happened?" can help you think more critically. Use AI to challenge your analysis, not to generate it.

Where AI can't help: AI wasn't in the room. It doesn't know what happened, what surprised you, or what you felt. Any reflection where the substance reads like it could have been written by someone who wasn't there will be obvious, both to your instructor and to you.

## Rubric dimensions

Your reflection will be evaluated on four dimensions, calibrated for a first attempt:

- **Accurate description.** Is the experience described with enough specificity and factual accuracy that a reader can understand what happened? Vague or generic descriptions ("we had a meeting and it went fine") don't give you anything to examine.
- **Critical examination.** Does the reflection go beyond surface reactions to analyze assumptions, perspectives, and the dynamics at play? This is where most first reflections have the most room to grow, and that's expected.
- **Honest self-assessment.** Does the reflection engage genuinely with what happened, including uncertainty, mistakes, or surprises? Reflections that present everything as a clean success story miss the point. The most valuable learning often comes from the parts that didn't go as planned.
- **Articulation of learning.** Is the takeaway specific, grounded in the experience, and transferable to future situations? A strong articulation names a concrete insight and explains how it applies beyond this one moment.

This is your first DEAL reflection in the course. The rubric expectations are calibrated accordingly. You're building fluency with a model you'll use throughout the semester, and each reflection is an opportunity to go deeper.

## Submission requirements

Submit your reflection individually through the Riipen platform. There is no required word count, but the rubric dimensions will be difficult to meet in under 400 words.

If you used AI at any point in your reflection process (as a thinking partner, for feedback on a draft, or to help you structure your thoughts), include a brief disclosure describing what tools you used and how. If you did not use AI, a one-sentence statement to that effect is sufficient. Disclosure is required regardless of the extent of use.

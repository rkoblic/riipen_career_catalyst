# Reading between the lines — what did the employer actually confirm?

## Purpose

This exercise practices the skill of distinguishing between what a client explicitly stated and what you're inferring or assuming based on context. In any professional meeting, people hear what they expect to hear, and the gap between confirmed information and assumptions can derail a project before it starts. This interactive gives you a low-stakes practice rep before your actual kickoff meeting, so you can build the habit of asking "Did they actually say that, or am I filling in the blanks?"

## Interaction model

**Format: Narrative with decision points.**

Students read a simulated employer kickoff meeting presented as a series of statements from the employer contact. After each statement or cluster of statements, they answer an interpretation question: what was actually confirmed, what's being assumed, and what needs follow-up.

Each question is multiple-choice with one best answer. After answering, students receive immediate feedback explaining why the correct answer is correct and why tempting alternatives are wrong.

**Fallback format:** If the build tool doesn't support inline narrative with embedded questions, present the full scenario text first, then ask all questions sequentially with reference back to specific passages.

## Context setup

Your team has been matched with Bridgeway Community Health, a regional nonprofit that runs health education programs in underserved communities. You've done your preliminary research and sent your confirmation message. Now you're in the kickoff meeting. Your employer contact, David Park, is the Director of Community Programs.

Below are excerpts from what David shared during the meeting. After each section, you'll answer a question about what was actually confirmed. Pay attention to the difference between what David says directly and what you might be tempted to assume.

## Stimulus

### Section 1: Project overview

David says:

"Thanks for meeting with us. So, the project. We've been running our community health workshops for about six years now, and they've been successful by most measures. Attendance is strong, the feedback forms are positive. But we've been hearing from our partner clinics that the information isn't always sticking. People come to the workshops, they learn things, and then three months later their health behaviors haven't really changed. We want to understand why and figure out what we can do differently."

### Section 2: Scope and deliverables

David says:

"What we're looking for from your team is a research-informed set of recommendations. We want you to look at what other organizations have done with similar programs, dig into the research on health behavior change, especially for the populations we serve, and come back to us with concrete suggestions we can actually implement. We don't need a 50-page report. A focused analysis with clear, actionable recommendations is what would be most useful to us."

### Section 3: Timeline and constraints

David says:

"In terms of timeline, we're flexible, but ideally we'd want to see a draft of your findings before our board meeting in late April. That's not a hard deadline, but it would be great to have something to share with them. Budget-wise, we can't fund any primary research, so this would be secondary research and analysis. We do have some internal survey data we can share with you, though, if that's helpful."

### Section 4: Stakeholders and context

David says:

"The workshops are run by our team of community health educators, about eight people. They're great, really dedicated. The curriculum was developed in-house about four years ago based on CDC guidelines at the time. We haven't done a major update since then. I should mention that our participants are primarily from immigrant and refugee communities, so language and cultural context are huge factors in everything we do."

## Questions

### Question 1

**Question text:** Based on David's project overview, what is the core problem the employer has identified?

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) The workshops are poorly attended and need better marketing.
- B) Workshop attendees aren't translating what they learn into lasting behavior changes.
- C) The partner clinics are unsatisfied with the quality of the workshop content.
- D) The organization needs to redesign its entire workshop curriculum from scratch.

**Correct answer:** B

**Feedback if correct:** Right. David was specific: attendance is strong, feedback is positive, but "the information isn't always sticking" and "health behaviors haven't really changed." The problem is the gap between learning and behavior change, not attendance, content quality, or a need for a complete overhaul.

**Feedback if incorrect:**
- A) David actually said attendance is strong. If you heard a marketing problem, you may have been projecting a common organizational challenge onto what was said. Go back and read his exact words.
- C) David said the partner clinics reported that information "isn't sticking" with participants. That's feedback about participant outcomes, not a complaint about the workshops themselves. It's a subtle but important distinction.
- D) David didn't say the workshops are failing or need to be rebuilt. He said the outcomes aren't matching the effort. That's a much more targeted problem than a total redesign.

---

### Question 2

**Question text:** What deliverable format did David request?

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) A comprehensive research report with literature review and methodology section.
- B) A slide deck presentation for the board.
- C) A focused analysis with actionable recommendations.
- D) A redesigned workshop curriculum based on best practices.

**Correct answer:** C

**Feedback if correct:** Exactly. David said "a focused analysis with clear, actionable recommendations" and explicitly ruled out a lengthy report ("We don't need a 50-page report"). Matching the deliverable to what the client asked for, rather than what you think they need, is a foundational client service skill.

**Feedback if incorrect:**
- A) David specifically said they don't need a 50-page report. When a client names what they don't want, pay attention.
- B) David didn't mention a slide deck. He did mention the board meeting, but as a timeline reference, not a deliverable format. Be careful not to connect two details that weren't actually connected.
- D) David asked for recommendations, not a redesigned curriculum. The gap between "suggest what we can do differently" and "build us a new curriculum" is significant. Overpromising on scope is one of the most common mistakes teams make after a kickoff.

---

### Question 3

**Question text:** What is the status of the late April board meeting deadline?

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) It's a firm deadline. The team must deliver final recommendations before the board meeting.
- B) It's a soft target. David would like a draft to share but said it's not a hard deadline.
- C) It's irrelevant to the project. David mentioned it as context, not as a deliverable milestone.
- D) It's flexible as long as the team communicates about timing in advance.

**Correct answer:** B

**Feedback if correct:** Good listening. David said "ideally" and "that's not a hard deadline, but it would be great to have something." This is a soft target, and the distinction matters. In professional work, treating every preference as a firm requirement creates unnecessary pressure and can compromise quality. Treating firm deadlines as flexible creates trust problems. Learn to hear the difference.

**Feedback if incorrect:**
- A) David used qualifying language throughout: "ideally," "not a hard deadline," "it would be great." Interpreting this as a firm deadline would lead your team to rush unnecessarily. Listen for qualifiers. They're signals.
- C) David brought it up in the context of wanting something to share with the board. That makes it a relevant milestone, even if it's not a hard deadline. Dismissing it entirely would mean missing a real opportunity to deliver value at a natural checkpoint.
- D) David didn't say anything about communication changing the deadline. This answer sounds reasonable, but it's adding information that wasn't in the conversation. Be careful about inventing flexibility that wasn't offered.

---

### Question 4

**Question text:** Which of the following is something David explicitly confirmed about the research approach?

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) The team should conduct interviews with workshop participants.
- B) The analysis should focus exclusively on CDC health education guidelines.
- C) Primary research like surveys or interviews is outside the project scope.
- D) The team should prioritize quantitative data analysis over qualitative findings.

**Correct answer:** C

**Feedback if correct:** David said "we can't fund any primary research, so this would be secondary research and analysis." That's a clear scope constraint. He also offered internal survey data, which is existing data he can share, not new primary research. Hearing constraints clearly prevents your team from planning work that was never on the table.

**Feedback if incorrect:**
- A) David said they can't fund primary research. Interviews with participants would be primary research. Proposing this in your project plan would signal that you weren't listening to a stated constraint.
- B) David mentioned that the curriculum was based on CDC guidelines, but he didn't say your analysis should focus exclusively on CDC materials. That detail was context about the current program, not direction for your research.
- D) David didn't specify a preference for quantitative or qualitative methods. Don't fill in methodological preferences the client didn't express. If it matters for your approach, ask.

---

### Question 5

**Question text:** David mentioned that participants are primarily from immigrant and refugee communities. What should your team do with this information?

**Type:** multiple-choice

**Options:**
- A) Note it as background context but focus your research on general health education best practices.
- B) Treat it as a central factor that should shape your research approach and recommendations.
- C) Ask David in a follow-up whether cultural context is actually relevant to the project scope.
- D) Assume this means the recommendations should focus on translation services.

**Correct answer:** B

**Feedback if correct:** David said "language and cultural context are huge factors in everything we do." When a client tells you something is a "huge factor," believe them. This isn't background context — it's a lens that should shape how you research, what sources you prioritize, and what recommendations you make. Research on health behavior change that doesn't account for the cultural context of the target population would miss the point of the project.

**Feedback if incorrect:**
- A) David said language and cultural context are "huge factors in everything we do." Treating this as background noise would mean missing a core dimension of the project. When clients emphasize something, they're telling you what matters to them.
- C) David already told you it's relevant, in strong terms. Asking whether it's relevant would signal that you weren't listening. If you need clarification on how it affects your specific deliverable, frame it as building on what he shared, not questioning whether it matters.
- D) Translation is one aspect of language access, but David's point was broader: "language and cultural context." Narrowing this to translation services ignores the cultural component entirely. Be careful not to reduce a complex factor to the first concrete solution that comes to mind.

## Debrief

That scenario covered five moments where the gap between "what was said" and "what you might assume" could shape your entire project direction. In professional work, these gaps accumulate quietly. A soft deadline gets treated as firm. A scope constraint gets overlooked. A passing comment about cultural context gets filed as a footnote instead of a guiding principle.

The habit you're building is simple but uncommon: after every meeting, ask your team, "What did the client actually say, and what are we adding?" You'll practice this for real when you debrief after your actual kickoff meeting.

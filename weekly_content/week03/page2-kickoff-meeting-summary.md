# Kickoff meeting & summary

In any client-facing role, the kickoff meeting is where the real work begins. It's the conversation that turns a project brief into a shared understanding between your team and the people you're working for. How well you prepare, listen, and follow up directly shapes the quality of everything that comes after.

This page walks you through the full arc: preparing before the meeting, making the most of the time during it, and processing what you heard afterward. The Kickoff Summary deliverable and post-kickoff research guidance are at the end.

### Setting up AI note capture

Taking detailed notes while staying engaged in a conversation is difficult, especially in a meeting where you also need to be listening carefully and asking questions. AI transcription and summarization tools can help bridge that gap.

Before the meeting, decide how your team will capture what's discussed. There are a few approaches:

- **Live transcription.** If your meeting platform offers built-in transcription (Zoom, Teams, and Google Meet all have this), turn it on. The raw transcript won't be perfect, but it gives you a searchable record to work from afterward.
- **Post-meeting summarization.** If you can't transcribe live, designate one team member to take rough notes during the meeting. Immediately afterward, feed those notes into an AI tool and ask it to organize them by topic. The sooner you do this, the easier it is to fill gaps while the conversation is still fresh.
- **Prompting for structure.** When you ask AI to help organize your notes, give it context. Share the raw notes, the project brief, and the format you want the output in (for example, the Kickoff Summary template further down this page). The more context AI has, the more useful its output will be. This is the "give AI enough context" strategy you practiced earlier in the course, applied here for the first time with real meeting content.

One important boundary: AI can help you organize and structure your notes, but it cannot generate the substance. If a detail wasn't discussed in the meeting, it shouldn't appear in your summary, regardless of how plausible it sounds.

### Pre-meeting prep

The research you've already done on the employer's organization is your foundation. Now your team needs to turn that individual knowledge into a shared plan for the meeting.

### Pre-meeting checklist
- [ ] Review the organizational research you completed earlier in the course. Identify two or three findings that are most relevant to the project.
- [ ] Consolidate your team's questions into a single prioritized list. Start with the most important unknowns, the things you genuinely need answers to before you can begin working.
- [ ] Assign roles for the meeting: who will ask the primary questions, who will take notes, and who will manage time. If your team has more than three members, double up on note-taking so you have multiple perspectives to compare afterward.
- [ ] Identify any assumptions your team is making about the project. Write them down. The meeting is your chance to confirm or correct them.
- [ ] Test your technology. Make sure your camera, microphone, and meeting platform are working. This sounds basic, but fumbling with tech in the first two minutes of a professional meeting is a poor first impression.

---

### Team huddle

Before the employer meeting, have a brief sync as a team. This is separate from the prep work above, which can be done individually. The huddle is about alignment: making sure everyone is on the same page about what you're walking into.

Use this time to confirm your question list, walk through who's asking what, and do a quick verbal run-through if it helps settle nerves. Keep it short. The goal is confidence, not rehearsal.

If your team is nervous, that's normal. This is likely your first professional meeting with someone outside the academic context, and it's reasonable to feel the stakes. The preparation you've already done is what separates a team that stumbles from one that comes across as capable and ready.

## During the meeting

### What to expect

The kickoff meeting is primarily a listening session. Your employer contact will share organizational context, project scope, what success looks like, the timeline, and who on their side you'll be working with. Your role is to absorb this information, take notes, and ask the questions your team prepared.

You may find that some of what the employer says aligns with what you expected from the project brief, and some of it doesn't. Pay particular attention to the surprises: where the employer's priorities differ from what you assumed, where scope is narrower or broader than you anticipated, where they emphasize something you hadn't considered. Those gaps between expectation and reality are where the most valuable information lives.

Don't worry about covering every question on your list. If the conversation is flowing and the employer is sharing useful context unprompted, let it run. Your prioritized question list ensures that if time runs short, you ask the most important things first.

### Note-taking checklist

Staying engaged on camera while capturing key information requires a deliberate approach. You can't write down everything, so focus on what matters most.

### What to listen for and capture
- [ ] The employer's stated priorities. What do they say matters most? Pay attention to emphasis, repetition, and the topics where they seem most invested.
- [ ] Specific deliverable expectations. Formats, scope boundaries, what's in and what's out.
- [ ] Timeline and milestones. Any dates, deadlines, or sequencing they mention.
- [ ] Key contacts. Who on the employer's side will you be working with? Who should you reach out to with questions?
- [ ] Constraints or limitations. Budget, access, data availability, anything that shapes what's possible.
- [ ] Surprises. Anything that doesn't match what you expected from the project brief. These are often the most important notes you'll take.
- [ ] Open questions. Things the employer mentioned but didn't fully explain, or topics that need follow-up.

A practical tip: if your team has assigned a dedicated note-taker, the rest of you can focus on the conversation and jot down only the things that jump out. Compare notes afterward to build a more complete picture.

## After the meeting

### Immediate debrief

As soon as the meeting ends, capture your team's reactions while they're fresh. Memory of conversations fades quickly, and the details that seem obvious right now will be fuzzy by tomorrow.

We recommend debriefing immediately, even if it's just ten minutes of quick notes. Focus on three things: what surprised you, what you still need to find out, and what the employer seemed to care about most. This isn't the formal Kickoff Summary. It's a raw capture that will feed into it.

---

### Processing what you heard

The gap between what was said in a meeting and what each person heard is often wider than teams expect. Two people can sit through the same conversation and walk away with different interpretations of what the employer meant, what was confirmed, and what's still open.

That's not a failure of listening. It's how communication works, especially when you're processing new information under pressure. The professional skill is recognizing this and building a process to reconcile different perspectives before they become embedded assumptions.

Here's how to do it:

**Compare notes as a team.** Have each person share what they captured, either from written notes, the meeting transcript, or memory. Don't start by debating what the employer meant. Start by establishing what was actually said. Where do your notes agree? Where do they diverge?

**Separate the confirmed from the assumed.** Some things the employer stated clearly: "The deliverable is a competitive analysis report." Other things you may be inferring: "They seemed to want us to focus on the North American market." The first is confirmed information. The second is an assumption that needs verification. Getting comfortable with this distinction is one of the most valuable habits you'll build this semester.

**Flag what needs follow-up.** If something important is unclear or your team disagrees on what was said, write it down as an open question. You'll include these in your Kickoff Summary and can raise them in your next employer interaction.

### Identifying gaps and open questions

After you've compared notes and processed what you heard, take stock of where you stand. Some things are now confirmed. Others are assumptions you haven't tested yet. And some are genuine unknowns that neither the project brief nor the kickoff addressed.

The ability to distinguish between these three categories, confirmed, assumed, and unknown, is critical for the Kickoff Summary and for the research you'll do next. Confirmed information goes into your summary as fact. Assumptions need to be flagged and eventually verified. Unknowns become the starting point for your post-kickoff research.

Ask your team: What do we know for certain? What are we filling in based on context or expectation? And what are we completely missing? Writing this down, even informally, prevents your team from treating assumptions as facts as the project progresses.

## Deliverable: Kickoff Summary

### What the Kickoff Summary is for

The Kickoff Summary is a factual record, not an analysis. It documents what the employer shared during the kickoff meeting: what they stated, what they confirmed, and what remains open. The purpose is to create a reference document your team can rely on as you move into deeper project work.

This is a completion-based checkpoint. You're not being graded on the quality of your analysis (that comes later in the Context Analysis & Project Plan). You're being evaluated on whether your summary accurately and completely captures what was discussed. A strong Kickoff Summary gives your team a solid foundation to build on. A vague one forces you to work from memory and assumptions when the stakes get higher.

The reason this deliverable is completion-based rather than graded is that accuracy matters more than polish at this stage. The course is checking whether your team can capture and organize real information from a professional interaction, not whether you can write a polished report. That skill is coming, but the foundation has to be accurate first.

## Post-kickoff research

### Targeted landscape research

Earlier in the course, your research on the employer's organization was broad and orienting. You were building general familiarity without knowing the specific project scope. Now that the kickoff has confirmed what you're actually working on, your research can be targeted.

This is the second stage of a three-part progression: your initial reconnaissance gave you a foundation, this stage focuses that foundation on the confirmed project scope, and the next stage will be analytical synthesis where you draw conclusions from what you've found. Right now, you're gathering and organizing, not yet analyzing.

Focus your research on:

- **The competitive or sector landscape.** Who else operates in this space? What are the prevailing trends, challenges, or opportunities? Understanding the broader context helps you see how your employer's project fits into a bigger picture.
- **Comparable approaches.** Has anyone tackled a similar problem or opportunity? Case studies, industry reports, or examples of parallel work can inform your team's approach without reinventing what already exists.
- **Stakeholder context.** If the employer mentioned specific audiences, customers, or communities, learn about them. Understanding who your work ultimately affects makes your analysis sharper.

As you research, organize your findings by theme rather than by source. Grouping information by topic (market trends, competitor activity, audience demographics) makes it far easier to synthesize later than a chronological list of everything you read. You don't need to analyze yet. You need to build a well-organized evidence base that you'll draw on when you write the Context Analysis & Project Plan.

AI is a strong tool for understanding unfamiliar domains at this stage. If you're researching an industry you've never encountered, use AI to build your baseline knowledge: ask it to explain how the sector works, what the key terminology means, or what the major trends are. For specific facts about the employer's competitors, market data, or recent developments, go to primary sources. AI's training data has a cutoff, and in a research context where recency matters, you need current information.

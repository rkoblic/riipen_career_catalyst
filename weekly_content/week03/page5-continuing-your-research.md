# Continuing your research

The research you do this week has a specific purpose: it feeds directly into the Context Analysis & Project Plan, your first graded team deliverable. That assignment asks you to analyze the landscape your project exists in and propose a clear direction based on evidence. The stronger your research base coming out of this week, the less backfilling you'll need to do when the deliverable is due.

## Targeted landscape research

[VIDEO: How to research a landscape you know nothing about]

Earlier in the course, your research on the employer's organization was broad and orienting. You were building general familiarity without knowing the specific project scope. Now that the kickoff has confirmed what you're actually working on, your research can be targeted.

This is the second stage of a three-part progression: your initial reconnaissance gave you a foundation, this stage focuses that foundation on the confirmed project scope, and the next stage will be analytical synthesis where you draw conclusions for the Context Analysis. Right now, you're gathering and organizing, not yet analyzing.

Focus your research on:

- **The broader landscape.** What's happening in the space your project lives in? Depending on the project, this might mean a market or industry, a policy area, a community, or a field of practice. The goal is to understand the environment your employer operates in well enough to make informed recommendations. For example, if your employer is a nonprofit expanding a youth mentorship program, you'd research how other organizations structure similar programs and what outcomes they report. If the project is an internal process improvement, you'd look at how comparable organizations handle the same function.
- **Similar initiatives.** Has anyone tackled a similar problem or opportunity? Look for case studies, published reports, program evaluations, or examples of parallel work. If the employer asked you to develop a community engagement strategy, look at how other organizations in the same sector have approached outreach — what worked, what didn't, and what you can learn from their experience. If the project is market-facing, look at how competitors or peers position themselves.
- **The people your work affects.** If the employer mentioned specific audiences, users, clients, or communities, learn about them. Understanding who your work ultimately serves makes your analysis sharper and your recommendations more grounded. If the project involves designing resources for first-generation college students, research that population — what barriers they face, what support structures exist, what language and framing resonates. If you're working on something customer-facing, understand the customer.

As you research, organize your findings by theme rather than by source. Grouping information by topic — trends, similar initiatives, audience characteristics, constraints — makes it far easier to synthesize later than a chronological list of everything you read. You don't need to analyze yet. You need to build a well-organized evidence base that you'll draw on when you write the Context Analysis & Project Plan.

AI is a strong tool for understanding unfamiliar domains at this stage. If you're researching a sector or field you've never encountered, use AI to build your baseline knowledge: ask it to explain how the space works, what the key terminology means, or what the major trends are. For specific facts — organizations, data, recent developments — go to primary sources. AI's training data has a cutoff, and in a research context where recency matters, you need current information.

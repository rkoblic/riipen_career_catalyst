# Video Script: How to write a meeting agenda that actually works

Most meeting agendas are useless. They list topics — "introductions, project overview, questions" — and nobody looks at them again after they're sent. That's not what we're going for here.

A good meeting agenda does three things. First, it tells the other person what you want to get out of the meeting. Not just the topics, but what you need to walk away knowing. If your agenda says "project scope discussion," the employer has no idea what to prepare. If it says "we'd like to confirm the deliverable format, timeline, and key contacts," they know exactly what you need and can come ready to give you clear answers.

Second, it sets expectations for how the time will be used. A sixty-minute meeting where nobody knows what comes after introductions tends to meander. A meeting where the first ten minutes are introductions, the next thirty are the employer's overview, and the last twenty are your team's questions gives everyone a structure to work within. You don't need to time it to the minute. You just need to signal that you've thought about how the hour should flow.

Third, it gives the employer a chance to redirect you. Maybe your team's questions are focused on the wrong area. Maybe the employer wants to spend more time on something you didn't anticipate. When they see the agenda twenty-four hours in advance, they can tell you. That's better than finding out mid-meeting that you prepared for the wrong conversation.

Here's what a good kickoff agenda actually looks like. Start with brief introductions — name, program, one sentence each. Then the employer's overview of the project: context, scope, success criteria, timeline, key contacts. Don't script this part. It's their time to share what they think you need to know. After that, your team's questions, organized by priority, not by topic. Lead with the things you genuinely can't start working without. End with next steps: when's the next meeting, what should each side do before then.

Put it in a shared document your whole team can access during the meeting. Send it to the employer with your confirmation message. And keep it short — one page, max. An agenda that takes five minutes to read defeats its own purpose.

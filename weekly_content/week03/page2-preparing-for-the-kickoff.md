# Preparing for the kickoff

In any client-facing role, the kickoff meeting is where the real work begins. It's the conversation that turns a project brief into a shared understanding between your team and the people you're working for. How well you prepare, listen, and follow up directly shapes the quality of everything that comes after.

The next few pages walk you through the full arc: preparing before the meeting, making the most of the time during it, and processing what you heard afterward.

---

## Professional meeting presence

This is not a class session you're attending. It's a professional client meeting you're running. You sent the agenda. You scheduled the time. The employer is showing up because your team invited them. That means you set the tone — and the employer is forming impressions before your first question. How you show up on screen, how you sound, and whether you look engaged all communicate something about what kind of team you are. Teams that present well get more trust and autonomy. Teams that don't get managed more tightly.

### Camera

Your camera is on. This is non-negotiable in a professional meeting. Camera-off communicates that you're disengaged or that you have something better to do. The employer cannot build a working relationship with a black rectangle.

Use a laptop or desktop, not a phone. A shaking, handheld camera is distracting and signals that you're doing this on the go rather than treating it as a real meeting. If a laptop isn't available, prop your phone on a stable surface at eye level.

Frame yourself from roughly the chest up, centered in the frame. The camera should be at eye level or slightly above — not angled up from your lap or down from a high shelf. Check your background: it doesn't need to be a home office, but it should be reasonably clean and not distracting. A virtual background is fine if your device supports it without visual glitching. Face a window or light source — if the light is behind you, the employer sees a silhouette.

### Audio

Find a quiet space. If you can't fully control background noise, use headphones with a built-in microphone — they reduce ambient sound significantly.

Default to muted when you're not speaking. Unmute when it's your turn to talk. This is standard practice in every professional video call. If something unexpected happens — a roommate walks in, a dog barks — mute immediately, handle it, and unmute when you're ready. Don't narrate the disruption. The less attention you draw to it, the more professional you come across.

Test your audio before the meeting. If you've never used the meeting platform before, join a test call. Discovering your microphone doesn't work in the first minute of the kickoff is exactly the kind of fumble that sets a bad first impression.

### Body language and engagement

Look at the camera when you're speaking, not at yourself or the other participants' video feeds. This creates the effect of eye contact and makes you appear confident and direct.

When others are speaking, show that you're listening. Nod occasionally. Maintain an attentive expression. The employer can see you even when you're not the one talking — and they notice when someone looks checked out.

Do not multitask. Close other tabs, put your phone away or face down. The employer can tell when someone is reading something else. The eyes give it away every time. Sit up. Posture communicates energy and attention. If you're slouched or leaning back, you look like you'd rather be somewhere else.

### Voice

When you speak, project. A quiet, tentative voice signals uncertainty even when what you're saying is perfectly good. You don't need to be loud — you need to be clear and audible.

Speak at a steady pace. Rushing through your introduction or your questions makes you sound nervous. Pausing briefly before you respond is fine and actually reads as thoughtful.

Work on minimizing filler words: "um," "so," "like," "I guess." You won't eliminate them entirely, and that's fine. But noticing them and practicing without them makes a real difference. The team huddle is a good place to do one round.

Sound like you want to be there. This isn't about forced enthusiasm — it's about tone. A flat, monotone delivery communicates that you're going through the motions. A conversational, engaged tone communicates that you care about the project. The difference is audible and it matters.

If your living situation means you can't meet every one of these standards — no quiet space, no laptop, limited internet — do what you can and communicate with your team. The point is to signal professionalism within your circumstances, not to have a perfect home office.

## Setting up AI note capture

Taking detailed notes while staying engaged in a conversation is difficult, especially in a meeting where you also need to be listening carefully and asking questions. AI transcription and summarization tools can help bridge that gap.

Before the meeting, decide how your team will capture what's discussed. There are a few approaches:

- **Built-in transcription.** Zoom, Teams, and Google Meet all offer built-in transcription. Turn it on if it's available. The raw transcript won't be perfect, but it gives you a searchable record to work from afterward.
- **Dedicated AI note-taking tools.** Tools like [Otter.ai](https://otter.ai/) and [Granola](https://www.granola.ai/) offer free tiers and can join your meeting to transcribe, summarize key points, and flag action items automatically. They're worth exploring if you want more than a raw transcript.
- **Manual notes + AI cleanup.** If you can't use a transcription tool, designate one team member to take rough notes during the meeting. Immediately afterward, feed those notes into an AI tool and ask it to organize them by topic. The sooner you do this, the easier it is to fill gaps while the conversation is still fresh.
- **Prompting for structure.** When you ask AI to help organize your notes, give it context. Share the raw notes, the project brief, and the format you want the output in (for example, the Kickoff Summary template on the After the Kickoff page). The more context AI has, the more useful its output will be. This is the "give AI enough context" strategy you practiced earlier in the course, applied here for the first time with real meeting content.

**A warning about AI-generated summaries.** Whether you're using a dedicated tool or cleaning up notes after the fact, AI summaries frequently get things wrong — misattributing who said what, inventing details that sound plausible but weren't discussed, or flattening nuance into oversimplified bullet points. Always review AI-generated summaries against your own memory and notes. Treat them as a starting draft, not a finished record.

**Set up and test before the meeting.** Whatever tool you choose, don't figure it out for the first time during the kickoff. Install it, run a test, and make sure it works with your meeting platform. A few minutes of setup now prevents scrambling during a meeting that matters.

**Always get permission to record.** If your note-taking tool records audio or video, you must ask the employer's permission before turning it on. This is both a professional courtesy and, in many jurisdictions, a legal requirement. A simple ask at the start of the meeting is all it takes: "Would it be okay if we record this meeting so we can review our notes afterward?"

## Pre-meeting prep

The research you've already done on the employer's organization is your foundation. Now your team needs to turn that individual knowledge into a shared plan for the meeting.

### Pre-meeting checklist
- [ ] Review the organizational research you completed earlier in the course. Identify two or three findings that are most relevant to the project.
- [ ] Consolidate your team's questions into a single prioritized list. Start with the most important unknowns, the things you genuinely need answers to before you can begin working.
- [ ] Assign roles for the meeting: who will ask the primary questions, who will take notes, and who will manage time. If your team has more than three members, double up on note-taking so you have multiple perspectives to compare afterward.
- [ ] Identify any assumptions your team is making about the project. Write them down. The meeting is your chance to confirm or correct them. For example, you might be assuming the deliverable is a written report when the employer hasn't specified a format, or that the project focuses on a particular market or audience based on your research rather than anything the employer has said.
- [ ] Test your technology. Make sure your camera, microphone, and meeting platform are working. This sounds basic, but fumbling with tech in the first two minutes of a professional meeting is a poor first impression.

## Prepare a kickoff agenda

[VIDEO: How to write a meeting agenda that actually works]

Put together a simple agenda for the meeting and share it with your employer contact at least 24 hours in advance. This does two things: it shows the employer your team is organized and prepared, and it gives them a chance to adjust the conversation if your priorities don't match theirs.

A kickoff agenda doesn't need to be elaborate. A shared document (Google Doc, or whatever your team is using for collaboration) with the following is enough:

- Brief team introductions (1–2 minutes)
- Employer overview of the project: context, scope, success criteria, timeline, key contacts (led by the employer)
- Team questions (your prioritized list from the pre-meeting prep)
- Next steps and follow-up

Keep the agenda in the same shared workspace your team is using for project documents so everyone can reference it during the meeting. Include it in your confirmation message to the employer so they can review and flag anything they'd like to add or adjust.

## Team huddle

Before the employer meeting, have a brief sync as a team. This is separate from the prep work above, which can be done individually. The huddle is about alignment: making sure everyone is on the same page about what you're walking into.

Use this time to confirm your question list, walk through who's asking what, and practice your introductions. Each person should be able to introduce themselves in about 15 seconds — name, program, and what you're looking forward to. Do a quick verbal run-through if it helps settle nerves. Keep the huddle short. The goal is confidence, not rehearsal.

While you're together, do a quick presence check: is everyone's camera on and framed well? Can you hear each other clearly? Is anyone's background distracting? Give each other honest feedback — it's easier to hear "your camera angle is off" from a teammate now than to realize it after the meeting. Practice your introduction once and ask your team: did I sound confident? Could you hear me clearly? Was I speaking too fast?

If your team is nervous, that's normal. This is likely your first professional meeting with someone outside the academic context, and it's reasonable to feel the stakes. The preparation you've already done is what separates a team that stumbles from one that comes across as capable and ready.

## Confirmation message to employer

At least 24 hours before the meeting, send a message to your employer contact through the Riipen platform confirming logistics: the date, time, and meeting link. Attach or link to the kickoff agenda so they can review it in advance. If there's anything your team needs before the meeting (access to documents, clarification on who's attending from the employer's side), this is the place to ask.

## Final check before you join

Run through this in the ten minutes before the meeting.

- [ ] Camera is on, at eye level, and stable (laptop or propped device — not handheld)
- [ ] You are framed from the chest up, centered, with your face well-lit
- [ ] Background is clean or you're using a virtual background that works without glitching
- [ ] Audio is tested and you can be heard clearly
- [ ] You are in a quiet space, or you have headphones with a microphone
- [ ] Other tabs and applications are closed — phone is put away or face down
- [ ] You have your team's question list and agenda accessible (not buried in tabs you'll need to hunt for)
- [ ] You know your introduction: name, program, one sentence — practiced and under 15 seconds
- [ ] Your AI note-taking tool is set up and running (if using one)
- [ ] You know your role for the meeting (question lead, note-taker, timekeeper)
- [ ] The person opening the meeting knows their opening line

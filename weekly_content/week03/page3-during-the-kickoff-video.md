# Video Script: What actually happens in a kickoff meeting

If you've never been in a professional kickoff meeting before, here's what to expect — and more importantly, what the people on the other side of the call are expecting from you.

You own this meeting. You made that clear when you sent the agenda and scheduled the time. So you open it. Welcome everyone, thank them for being there, and start with introductions. It's courteous to ask the employer to go first — say something like "We'd love to start by hearing from your team." This lets you hear how they frame the project before you speak. When it's your turn, keep it short. Your name, your program, one sentence about what you're looking forward to on this project. That's it. Fifteen seconds, max. The employer doesn't need your life story, and long introductions eat into the time you need for the actual conversation. If there are four people on your team and each one talks for two minutes, you've burned eight minutes on introductions alone. In a sixty-minute meeting, that matters.

After introductions, ask the employer to walk through the project. Something like: "We've read the brief and are excited to work on this with you — we'd love to hear a bit more about it in your own words." They'll describe their organization, the problem or opportunity they want you to work on, what success looks like from their perspective, the timeline, and who on their side you'll be interacting with. Your job during this part is to listen. Not passively — actively. You're listening for specific things: what they emphasize, what they repeat, what they seem most invested in. You're also listening for the gaps: what they don't mention, where the brief and the conversation don't quite match, what seems unclear or assumed.

Here's the part most teams get wrong: they treat their question list like a script. They wait for the employer to stop talking, then go down the list one by one. The problem is that the employer may have already answered half your questions during their overview. If you ask something they just spent three minutes explaining, it signals you weren't listening.

Instead, track your questions as the employer talks. Cross off the ones they've answered. Adjust the ones that need rephrasing based on what you've heard. When it's your turn to ask, lead with the questions that are still genuinely open — the things you couldn't have figured out from what was said. Follow-up questions are often more valuable than prepared ones, because they show you were actually processing the information in real time.

One more thing: pay attention to tone and emphasis, not just content. When an employer says "this is really important to us" or circles back to a topic multiple times, that's a signal. The things they emphasize are the things they'll evaluate you on, whether the rubric says so or not. And when they hedge or seem uncertain about something, that's a signal too — it might be a genuine unknown on their side, and knowing that early helps you plan.

You won't get everything perfect in your first kickoff. That's fine. The teams that do well aren't the ones who ask perfect questions. They're the ones who listen well enough to understand what the employer actually needs, even when it's not exactly what's written in the brief.

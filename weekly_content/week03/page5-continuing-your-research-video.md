# Video Script: How to research a landscape you know nothing about

You've just come out of the kickoff meeting. You know what the project is. And now you need to understand the world it exists in — the landscape, the context, the other players, the people it affects. Maybe that's an industry you've never worked in. Maybe it's a community you've never encountered. Either way, you're starting from near-zero and you need to get smart fast.

Here's a process that works.

Start by defining what you're actually trying to understand. Not "everything about the industry" — that's a rabbit hole. Think about what your employer told you in the kickoff. What's the problem they're trying to solve? Who are they trying to reach? What does success look like? Those answers should shape your research questions. If the employer is a nonprofit trying to improve health outcomes in immigrant communities, your research questions aren't "what is public health" — they're "what approaches have worked for health behavior change in culturally diverse populations" and "what do other organizations doing similar work look like."

Next, build your baseline. This is where AI is genuinely useful. If you're entering an unfamiliar domain, ask AI to give you the lay of the land: how the sector works, what the key terminology means, who the major players are, what the current trends and debates look like. This gets you oriented fast. But treat it as a starting point, not a source. AI will give you a plausible overview, but it may be outdated, oversimplified, or wrong on specifics. Use it to know what to search for, then go find the real sources.

Now go deep on three things. First, the landscape: who else is operating in this space and what are they doing? You're not writing an exhaustive industry report. You're trying to understand the environment your employer exists in well enough to make recommendations that aren't naive. Second, similar initiatives: has anyone tried to solve a similar problem? Look for case studies, program evaluations, published reports. What worked? What didn't? What can you learn from their experience without repeating their mistakes? Third, the people affected by the work. If there's a specific audience, community, or user group, understand them. What do they need? What barriers do they face? What have others gotten wrong when trying to serve them?

As you go, organize by theme, not by source. Don't build a list of "here's what article one said, here's what article two said." Build categories: landscape trends, comparable initiatives, audience characteristics, constraints. When you sit down to write the Context Analysis, you want to pull from organized evidence, not scroll through a pile of bookmarks trying to remember why you saved them.

One last thing: know when to stop. Research expands to fill whatever time you give it. You're not writing a literature review. You're building enough of an evidence base to make informed recommendations. When you can explain the landscape, point to relevant examples, and describe the people your work affects with specificity and confidence — that's enough. Go write your analysis.

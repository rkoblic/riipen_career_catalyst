# Reflection #1

This is your first graded reflection — an individual submission worth approximately 5% of your final grade as part of the Reflection Portfolio. You'll write five of these across the semester, each at a transition point where you have enough experience to reflect on substantively.

You learned the DEAL framework earlier in the course. This page won't re-teach it — go back to the Introduction to Reflective Practice page if you need a refresher on the model or the worked example. What this page does is give you the specific prompt, the rubric, and practical guidance for writing a strong first reflection.

---

## The prompt

Choose one specific moment from the past three weeks where something surprised you, challenged you, or didn't go the way you expected. Using the DEAL framework, describe what happened, examine what that experience reveals about how you enter new professional situations, and articulate what you're taking forward into the next phase of the project.

The kickoff meeting is a natural anchor experience given its recency and significance, but you're not limited to it. Team formation, early research, a conversation that shifted your understanding, a moment where you noticed something about how you work — any of these are valid material.

---

## Connecting to your goals

Look back at your answers on the Your Starting Point questionnaire — specifically what you said about your learning goals and the professional challenge you want to work on. How does the experience you just described connect to those goals? Did anything in the first three weeks confirm, complicate, or shift what you thought you wanted to get out of this course?

You don't need to have answers. Naming the connection — or the tension — is the point.

---

## Choosing an anchor experience

Not every experience makes good reflection material. The best anchor experiences share a few qualities:

- **Something actually happened.** A specific conversation, decision, interaction, or moment — not a general feeling about the week. "The kickoff meeting" is too broad. "The moment the employer said our assumptions about the deliverable format were wrong" is specific enough to examine.
- **It provoked a reaction.** Surprise, discomfort, confusion, unexpected satisfaction — anything that disrupted your expectations. Experiences that went exactly as planned rarely produce the most interesting reflections.
- **You were involved, not just present.** You made a choice, held back when you could have spoken up, prepared in a way that shaped the outcome, or failed to prepare in a way that mattered. If you were a passive observer, it's harder to examine your own role.

If you're stuck choosing, ask yourself: what moment from the past three weeks keeps coming back to you? The thing you're still thinking about is usually the thing worth examining.

---

## Rubric dimensions

Your reflection will be evaluated on four dimensions, equally weighted at 25% each. The rubric is the same across all five reflections in the portfolio — what changes is the depth expected as you gain fluency with the model. For this first attempt, depth and honesty matter more than polish.

**Descriptive specificity.** Can the reader tell what actually happened? A strong description is concrete enough that someone who wasn't there could understand the situation — who was involved, what was said or done, what the context was. Focus on one experience rather than summarizing the whole phase. "We had a meeting and it went fine" gives you nothing to examine. "The employer spent ten minutes describing their priorities, and I realized halfway through that our team's questions were about the wrong things" gives you everything.

**Critical examination.** Does your examination produce insight — something you didn't fully see while the experience was happening? This is where most of the intellectual work lives. The standard is not "I learned a lot" or "this was challenging." It's identifying assumptions you were operating under, surfacing patterns you notice, and connecting the experience to something broader about how you work. The test: if you could delete your Examine section and the Describe section would still convey the same understanding, you haven't examined — you've summarized.

**Personal accountability.** This is the dimension most first reflections struggle with, and it's worth understanding why it's weighted equally with the others. The question is whether you place yourself inside the experience as an actor — examining your own assumptions, decisions, and reactions — or whether you remain outside it as a narrator describing events that happened around you. "The employer's feedback was confusing" is narration. "I realized I'd been so invested in our original approach that I heard the feedback as criticism rather than redirection" is accountability. You don't need to confess to failure. You need to examine your own role in what happened, including the choices you made and why you made them.

**Articulation of learning.** Is your takeaway specific enough to actually apply in a future situation? "I learned that communication is important" doesn't meet the bar — it could have been written before you had the experience. "I learned that prioritizing questions by what we genuinely don't know, rather than listing everything we're curious about, leads to more focused meetings — going forward, I'll sort my team's question list by urgency before any client interaction" does. Strong articulated learning emerges visibly from your examination. If it reads like a pre-formed lesson you're attaching to the experience, push deeper.

## Common pitfalls in first reflections

These are the patterns that keep first reflections at the surface. Knowing them in advance won't guarantee you avoid them, but it helps to recognize them in your own draft.

**Staying external.** You describe what the team did, what the employer said, how the meeting went — but never examine your own thinking, choices, or reactions. The reflection reads like a project update rather than a personal examination. Push yourself to answer: what did *I* do, think, or assume — and why?

**Evaluative language masquerading as analysis.** "This was challenging," "I learned a lot," "communication is key" — these are judgments, not examinations. They tell the reader what you concluded without showing how you got there. If your Examine section is mostly adjectives and conclusions, go back and ask *why* it was challenging, *what specifically* you learned, and *how* you know communication was the issue.

**Describing too broadly.** Trying to reflect on all three weeks at once rather than anchoring in one specific moment. A reflection that covers everything covers nothing. Pick one experience and go deep.

**Generic articulated learning.** "I learned that teamwork is important" could be written by any student in any course. Your articulated learning should only make sense in the context of the specific experience you described and examined. If someone else could have written it without having your experience, it's too general.

## Using AI as a thinking partner

AI can be a useful sounding board for reflection, but it can't reflect for you. The experience is yours, the examination has to come from your own thinking, and the learning has to be something you actually articulate.

Where AI can help: if you're stuck, try describing your experience to an AI tool and asking it to push back. "What assumptions might I be making here?" or "What's another way to interpret what happened?" can help you think more critically. You can also ask AI to read your draft Examine section and tell you where you're still on the surface. Use AI to challenge your analysis, not to generate it.

Where AI can't help: AI wasn't in the room. It doesn't know what happened, what surprised you, or what you felt. Any reflection where the substance reads like it could have been written by someone who wasn't there will be obvious — both to your instructor and to you.

## Format and submission

**Length:** 800–1200 words. The range is a guide, not a hard boundary. A shorter reflection that is specific, honest, and insightful is stronger than a longer one that is vague and performative. That said, reflections under 700 words rarely achieve sufficient depth in the Examine stage, and reflections over 1400 words usually indicate you're describing too broadly rather than examining deeply.

**Structure:** Use the three DEAL stages as organizing sections. Headers are encouraged but not required — what matters is that a reader can tell where description ends and examination begins, and where examination gives way to articulated learning.

**Submission:** Submit individually through the Riipen platform.

**AI disclosure:** If you used AI at any point in your reflection process — as a thinking partner, for feedback on a draft, or to help structure your thoughts — include a brief disclosure describing what tools you used and how. If you did not use AI, a one-sentence statement to that effect is sufficient. Disclosure is required regardless of the extent of use.

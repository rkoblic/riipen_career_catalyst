# Video Script: Working with Data and Visuals

Data doesn't speak for itself. The format you choose either helps the reader see your point or forces them to hunt for it.

The same information can work in three different formats, and the right choice depends on what you're arguing. An environmental sustainability audit team has data on energy consumption across twelve facility types. If they want to show which facilities are the most inefficient (a comparison), a table serves best. Twelve rows, facility name and consumption figure, sorted from highest to lowest. The reader scans it in thirty seconds and the pattern is immediately clear.

If they want to show that energy costs have been trending upward over five years (a change over time), a line chart does what a table can't. The visual slope makes the trend impossible to miss. The same five data points in a table would require the reader to do the arithmetic.

But if the finding is simpler (three facilities account for 70% of total energy use), neither a table nor a chart earns its space. A sentence does it better: "Three facilities account for 70% of total energy spend: the downtown warehouse, the regional distribution center, and the main production floor." One sentence. A chart for three numbers is usually more visual noise than signal.

Same logic for a customer onboarding project. Comparing drop-off rates across five stages? Table or bar chart. Showing that completion rates improved after a redesign? Line chart, before and after. The single most important number? Put it in a sentence.

Pick the format that makes your argument clearest to a reader who isn't already convinced.

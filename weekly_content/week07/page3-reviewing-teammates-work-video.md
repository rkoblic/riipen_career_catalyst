# Video Script: Reviewing Your Teammates' Work

When you read a colleague's draft section, the first instinct is to fix the sentences. Awkward phrasing, a word choice you'd change, a comma that's off. That's line editing, and it's the least useful thing you can do at this stage.

Structural feedback asks a different question: does this section do what it's supposed to do? Does it have a clear claim? Does the evidence actually support it? Would a reader who doesn't know the project follow the logic?

Here's the difference in practice. A teammate's section on market positioning in a customer onboarding redesign opens with three paragraphs of company background, then makes a single claim about positioning in paragraph four. A line editor would fix the grammar. A structural reviewer would flag the real issue: the section buries its main point under setup the reader doesn't need. That's not a prose problem; it's a structure problem, and polishing the sentences wouldn't fix it.

Or consider a section in a digital skills program evaluation that cites five statistics but never says what they mean for the recommendation. The writing is clean. But the reader has to do the analytical work themselves, inferring why any of this matters. That gap (the missing "this means") is structural. Adding a concluding interpretive sentence to each evidence block would do more for that section than any amount of line editing.

Structural feedback comes first. Once the logic holds and the sections do their job, that's when you read for language.

# Before Check-In #1

Check-In #1 is the first employer meeting in the Development phase, and a different kind of employer interaction than the Direction Check. You're not presenting a polished summary of progress. You're bringing specific questions and decision points to someone who can help you move the work forward. The preparation, the meeting itself, and the debrief that follows are all part of the same professional cycle.

---

## Receiving feedback well

In professional work, receiving feedback on something you've spent weeks building is genuinely difficult. The instinct when someone evaluates your work is to explain, defend, or deflect: to manage how you're being perceived rather than to hear what's being said. That instinct makes sense. It's also counterproductive.

The problem with a defensive response isn't that it's rude. It's that it closes off the information you need. An employer who says "this framing doesn't quite match what we were looking for" is giving you a course correction. If your response is to justify the framing you chose, you may leave the meeting having technically heard the feedback but not actually having received it.

The protocol below won't eliminate the discomfort, but it creates a structure that keeps you listening when the instinct is to argue.

**The reception protocol: Listen → Clarify → Acknowledge → Decide Later**

**Listen.** Hear the feedback before you respond to it. Don't start formulating your reply while the employer is still talking. If you catch yourself thinking "but we chose that because..." — pause, and listen to the end.

**Clarify.** Before reacting, make sure you understand what's being said. "Can you say more about what you mean by framing?" or "Is the issue with the scope, the approach, or how we've communicated it?" — these questions show you're taking the feedback seriously and give you actual information to work with. Clarifying isn't arguing. It's understanding.

**Acknowledge.** Name what you heard. "So the concern is that our recommendation doesn't address the operational constraints you mentioned in the kickoff" — this tells the employer you understood them and gives them the chance to confirm or correct. Acknowledgment doesn't mean agreement. It means the message landed.

**Decide Later.** Don't commit to a response or a course of action in the moment. You don't have to agree with every piece of feedback, and you don't have to decide what to do with it during the meeting. Your job in the meeting is to hear and understand. The decision about what to do with it happens in the debrief, with your team.

---

**Feedback Protocol Card**

Save this for use during and after Check-In #1.

- [ ] **Listen.** Hear the full feedback before responding. Pause the internal reply.
- [ ] **Clarify.** Ask one question to confirm you understand what's being said. ("Can you say more about...?" "Is the concern with X or Y?")
- [ ] **Acknowledge.** Name what you heard. ("So the issue is..." "You're saying that...") Let them confirm or correct.
- [ ] **Decide Later.** Capture the feedback. Don't commit to a response in the meeting. Decide what to do with it in the debrief.

## The Work-in-Progress Summary

### What it is and why it exists

The Work-in-Progress Summary is a preparation tool for your team, not a document the employer reads. You submit it before Check-In #1 as a checkpoint — but its primary purpose is to force a clear-eyed look at where the project actually stands before you walk into the meeting. Teams that try to draft the WIP Summary and realize they can't answer the questions clearly are discovering a problem they needed to find. That discomfort is the point.

The submission requirement exists because teams that haven't articulated their status in writing walk into employer meetings unprepared in ways that aren't obvious until the meeting goes badly. Writing it down (actually writing it, not just thinking through it) is what surfaces the vague answers and unresolved questions. Those are exactly what you want to bring to Check-In #1, not hide from it.

### What goes in the summary

The WIP Summary has four sections. Each exists for a specific reason, and understanding that reason will help you fill it in honestly.

**Completed work.** Name specifically what has been produced to date — sections drafted, research completed, decisions made. This section answers the employer's first unspoken question: is this team actually working? Be specific. "We've completed" is more credible than "we've been working on."

**Current status.** Where is the project right now? What's drafted, what's in progress, what hasn't been started yet? This section creates the shared baseline for the meeting. An employer who knows your section on competitive positioning is drafted but your recommendations aren't started can engage with you much more usefully than one who doesn't know what state the work is in.

**Challenges encountered.** An honest account of blockers, gaps, and unexpected complications. This is the section most teams are tempted to soften, and it's the most valuable one. The employer is in the meeting to help you. They can't help with problems you haven't told them about. A team with real challenges and specific questions gets far more out of a progress meeting than a team that reports everything is going well.

**Specific questions or feedback needs.** What does your team need from the employer to move forward? Name the questions precisely. "We need feedback on our research" is not specific enough to generate a useful response. "We've drafted the competitive landscape section but we're unsure whether to include [Competitor X] — can you tell us whether they're a direct competitor in your view?" gives the employer something to engage with. The quality of your questions determines the quality of the meeting.

### What the completion criteria require

A WIP Summary that says everything is progressing smoothly and offers no specific questions does not meet the completion criteria. Not because there's a requirement to report problems, but because every project at this stage has open questions and unresolved decisions. A summary without them is either not honest or not looking closely enough.

The criteria are: an honest assessment of current progress (including what isn't done), at least one specific challenge or uncertainty, and at least one targeted question for the employer. A one-paragraph summary that covers these three things genuinely is more than enough. Length is not the standard.

### AI guidance for the WIP Summary

AI can help you organize and clarify the WIP Summary after your team has agreed on its substance — restructuring for clarity, tightening language, ensuring the four sections flow logically. That's a legitimate use of AI as a production partner.

AI cannot generate the substance of the summary. It doesn't know what your team has built, where you're stuck, or what questions you actually need answered. Those must come from honest team conversation. Using AI to write the challenges section without having the conversation first produces a document that sounds plausible but doesn't reflect your real situation — and your real situation is exactly what the employer needs to engage with.

## Preparing for Check-In #1

### What a questions-based meeting is

Check-In #1 is structured differently from the Direction Check. At the Direction Check, you were presenting a plan and receiving employer approval. Here, you're bringing the work you've built so far and asking the employer to help you move it forward. The employer isn't evaluating a presentation. They're acting as a resource.

This means the meeting works best when your team drives the agenda. You know what the project needs. The brief status update at the start (five minutes, what's done and what's in progress) gives the employer context, but the bulk of the meeting belongs to your questions. A team that comes with three specific, well-considered questions will have a more useful hour than a team that comes with a detailed progress update and nothing to ask.

### Developing specific questions and decision points

The most useful questions for Check-In #1 have two qualities: they're things your team genuinely can't resolve without the employer's input, and they're specific enough that the employer can actually answer them.

Questions worth asking:
- "We've defined the target audience as [X], but our research suggests [Y] might also be relevant. How do you think about that boundary?"
- "Our draft recommendation on [topic] depends on whether [assumption] is accurate — can you confirm?"
- "We've encountered conflicting information about [issue] in our research. What's your read on the current situation?"

Questions that won't generate useful responses:
- "What do you think of our work so far?" (Too broad — employer doesn't know what to respond to)
- "Is our approach going in the right direction?" (Requires the employer to evaluate unseen work)
- "Do you have any feedback for us?" (Delegates the agenda entirely)

Walk through your draft deliverable section by section with your team before the prep session. Every place where your team isn't sure about a decision, an assumption, or a direction is a candidate for a Check-In #1 question.

### Anticipating what the employer might ask

Employers at progress meetings often ask questions the team hasn't thought to prepare for. The most common patterns: asking you to explain your reasoning on a specific decision, asking why you didn't pursue a particular direction, or probing assumptions embedded in your research or recommendations.

You can use AI to stress-test your preparation. After you've developed your questions and know the current state of your draft, try this prompt: "Here's a brief summary of our project and where we are: [paste summary]. What questions is an employer likely to ask about this in a progress meeting?" Review the questions AI generates — not to script your answers, but to identify any places where your team's reasoning is thin or where you haven't made an explicit decision. If AI surfaces a question your team can't answer, that's something to resolve before the meeting, either by deciding or by adding it to your own question list for the employer.

## Team prep session

Use the prep session to make the meeting concrete, not to rehearse a presentation. Rehearsing implies a script; Check-In #1 is a conversation.

Three things to accomplish in the prep session:

1. **Finalize three to five specific questions.** If your list is longer, prioritize — you likely won't get to everything, and the most important questions should come first. Assign one team member to lead each question, meaning they introduce it and drive it forward. The rest of the team listens and adds context.

2. **Draft the status update.** The opening five minutes of the meeting covers what's done, what's in progress, and what's stuck — keep it brief and factual. This isn't a highlight reel. It's context for the questions that follow.

3. **Assign a note-taker.** Someone needs to capture employer answers, decisions made, and any new information surfaced during the meeting — in real time. Decide who this is before the meeting starts. Review the Feedback Protocol Card as a team before you go in.

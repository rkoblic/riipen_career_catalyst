# After Check-In #1

## Post-meeting debrief

Debrief immediately after the meeting while the conversation is fresh. The debrief has one job: convert what was heard into a written record the whole team can act on.

Structure your debrief notes around four questions:
- What did the employer say in response to each of our questions?
- What decisions were made — either explicitly by the employer or implicitly by your team's response to the feedback?
- What new information did the employer surface that changes how you're thinking about the work?
- What action items follow from this, and who owns each one?

A debrief that produces a prioritized list of action items with owners means the meeting worked. A debrief that produces general impressions ("it went well, they seemed positive") means the meeting happened but the learning didn't.

## Weekly Progress Check-In

Starting this week and continuing through the end of the course, your team has a standing prompt to close out each week. Use it after your post-meeting debrief and at the end of every subsequent week:

**What got done this week?** (Be specific — name the sections drafted, the decisions made, the work completed.)

**What moved since last week?** (What progressed, even if not completed?)

**What's blocked?** (What can't move forward until something else happens? Who or what is the blocker?)

**Who owns what this week?** (Name specific tasks and owners for the coming week.)

The Weekly Progress Check-In keeps your team's project plan a living document rather than a plan you made during the Discovery phase and haven't looked at since. It also builds the contribution record that's directly relevant to peer evaluation — teams that check in consistently have clearer evidence of each member's contribution than teams that don't.

---

## AI guidance for the debrief

AI can help you organize and structure debrief notes after the meeting — converting raw notes into a structured action item list, grouping related feedback, or identifying patterns across multiple pieces of input. This is useful when your notes are fragmented or when the meeting covered a lot of ground quickly.

AI cannot generate the content of the debrief. It wasn't in the room. Using AI to write up what the employer said based on a vague prompt produces a document that sounds organized but doesn't capture what actually happened. The debrief must reflect the real conversation.

If you're using AI to transcribe audio from the meeting, treat the transcript as a raw draft — review it for accuracy, especially for names, numbers, and any specific commitments made. AI transcription tools miss context and sometimes introduce errors.

## Submission

**Work-in-Progress Summary** — team submission, due before Check-In #1.

Submit as a team before the Check-In #1 meeting begins. The submission is a completion-based checkpoint — it is verified but not graded for quality. Late submission (after the meeting) does not meet the standard: the summary's purpose is preparation, and a summary submitted after the meeting has already happened serves neither purpose.

**AI disclosure:** Required. If AI was used to assist with drafting or organizing the WIP Summary, include a one-sentence disclosure describing how. If AI was not used, include a one-sentence statement to that effect. The substance of the summary must come from your team's actual project status regardless of whether AI assisted with language or structure.

# Video Script: From Plan to Product — What professional deliverables look like

The difference between a draft that impresses a professor and a draft that works for a client comes down to one thing: a client needs to act on it.

Here's the same research finding written two ways. An environmental sustainability audit team writes: "Based on an extensive review of the academic literature and industry benchmarks, it has been determined that the organization's current energy consumption patterns reflect systemic inefficiencies with broad implications for long-term operational sustainability." That sentence is complete. It's also unusable. A client reading it can't do anything with "broad implications for long-term operational sustainability."

The professional version of the same finding: "Your energy costs are running 23% above industry benchmarks, driven by aging HVAC systems in two facilities. Upgrading those systems would reduce operating costs by an estimated $85,000 annually." Same data. Same underlying analysis. But now the client knows what the problem is, what's causing it, and what to do about it.

The pattern holds across project types. A digital skills training program team documenting a community need doesn't write "it is evident that there exist significant gaps in digital literacy competencies among target demographics." They write "67% of program participants have never used a spreadsheet in a professional context. Basic data entry is the most common barrier to employment in the sector."

The move is the same in both cases: strip the hedging, name the specific problem, connect it to a concrete consequence. Your reader has limited time and other documents open. Write for that reader.

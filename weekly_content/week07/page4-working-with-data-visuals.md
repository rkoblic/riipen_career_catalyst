# Working with data and visuals

In professional work, how you present data affects whether it lands as evidence or as noise. The same numbers that support a clear recommendation in one format can leave a reader confused — or unconvinced — in another. Choosing the right format isn't a design question; it's an argument question.

[VIDEO: Working with Data and Visuals]

The video above walks through the same data presented as a table, a chart, and a sentence — showing when each format serves the argument best. The decision guide below is the companion to those examples.

**When to use each format:**

- Use a **table** when you're comparing multiple items across the same attributes and the reader needs to look up specific values or see all the options at once. Tables are for comparison and reference.
- Use a **chart** when you're showing a trend over time, a distribution, or a proportion — patterns that are easier to see visually than to read in rows. Charts are for shape and movement.
- Use a **sentence or short paragraph** when you have a single key number or finding. If your most important data point can be stated in one sentence, state it in one sentence. A chart for one or two numbers adds visual weight without adding clarity.
- When in doubt, ask: does this visual make the reader's job easier, or does it require a paragraph of explanation to interpret? If it requires explanation, the explanation is probably the better format.

## Presenting numbers to support an argument

There's a difference between reporting data and using data to make a point. A table of survey results reports data. A sentence that says "only 12% of respondents rated current onboarding as effective — the lowest score across all program dimensions" uses data to make a point.

The move that separates reporting from arguing is interpretation: what does this number mean, and why does it matter to the recommendation? Numbers don't carry meaning on their own. You have to supply it. "Our research found that 43% of employees have not received feedback in the past six months" is a finding. "43% of employees have not received feedback in the past six months, which our interviews suggest is the primary driver of low engagement scores in the operations division" is an argument.

Before including any data point in your deliverable, ask: have I told the reader what this means and why it matters? If you haven't, the data is decoration, not evidence.

## Using AI for data visualization

AI tools can help you explore how to visualize data — generating chart types, suggesting formats for specific data structures, or producing a rough visual you can refine. This is a legitimate and useful application, especially when you're working with data formats you haven't visualized before.

Two things to keep in mind. First, AI-generated charts need to be verified against your source data. Check that the numbers match, the labels are accurate, and the chart type actually fits the argument you're making — AI will produce something plausible-looking even when the format choice isn't right. Second, you should be able to explain and defend any visual in your deliverable. If an employer asks why you chose a bar chart over a table, "the AI suggested it" is not a sufficient answer. Know what the visual is showing and why that format serves the argument.

A useful starting prompt: "Here's my data: [paste data]. I want to show [specific comparison, trend, or proportion]. What chart types would work for this, and what are the trade-offs of each?" Review the options, choose based on the argument, and verify the output before it goes into the document.

# Employer Outreach Message

In professional work, your first message to a client sets the tone for the entire relationship. This message is your team's introduction. It tells your employer contact who you are, that you've done your homework, and that you're ready to get started. You'll send it through the Riipen platform, but the same principles of professional correspondence apply: clear structure, purposeful tone, and attention to detail.

---

## What This Message Needs to Accomplish

Your outreach message has three jobs:

1. **Introduce your team.** Keep it brief. One or two sentences about who you are as a group. The employer doesn't need full bios; they need to know the names and faces they'll be working with.

2. **Demonstrate awareness of the project.** Show that you've read the project brief and have a working understanding of what's being asked. This doesn't mean restating the brief back to them. Reference the project naturally, the way you would if someone asked "So what are you working on?"

3. **Request the kickoff meeting.** Propose a 60-minute meeting, suggest a few time options if possible, and ask what platform they prefer (Zoom, Teams, etc.). Make it easy for them to say yes.

---

## Tone and Professional Context

This person is a professional contact, not a professor or a TA. Write the way you'd write to a new colleague at work: respectful, warm, and specific. Avoid being overly formal ("Dear Sir/Madam, I hope this finds you well") or too casual ("Hey! We're super excited to work with you!").

You covered the fundamentals of professional correspondence earlier in the course. This is your first time applying them with a real professional audience. The same principles apply: clear purpose, purposeful paragraphs, and a professional tone.

## Before You Send

**Everyone reviews, everyone agrees.** All team members should read the final version before it goes out. Make sure the message represents the whole team, not just the person who drafted it. One person drafts, everyone reviews, and you send it when the team is aligned.

**After you send it.** Don't just send and wait. If you haven't heard back within two to three business days, send a brief, polite follow-up. Employer contacts are busy professionals, and a gentle nudge is expected and appropriate. You may also need to handle logistics like confirming the meeting platform, sharing calendar invites, or coordinating time zones.

**AI use.** AI is permitted here. We recommend drafting the message yourself first, then using AI to refine your tone, tighten your language, or catch anything you missed. That's how most professionals use AI for correspondence: they write, then polish. It keeps your voice in the message and ensures the content is accurate to your actual situation.

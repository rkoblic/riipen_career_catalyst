# Researching an Organization

Walking into a meeting with a professional contact without knowing anything about their organization is like showing up to a job interview without reading the job description. It signals that you didn't prepare, and it makes the meeting less productive for everyone. The research you do now helps you ask better questions, understand context faster, and make a stronger first impression at the kickoff.

---

## What to Look For

At this stage, your research should be broad and orienting, not deep and analytical. You're trying to build working knowledge, not produce a report. Focus on these areas:

- **What the organization does.** Their core products, services, or mission. If someone asked you "So what does this company do?" you should be able to answer in two or three sentences.
- **Their sector and market context.** What industry are they in? Who are their main competitors or peers? Is the sector growing, shrinking, or shifting? You don't need a full competitive analysis yet. Just enough to understand the landscape they operate in.
- **Recent news or developments.** Has anything significant happened recently? A new product launch, a leadership change, a strategic pivot, a funding round? Recent context helps you understand what the employer might be focused on right now.
- **The people you'll be working with.** Look up your employer contact and any other names mentioned in the project brief on LinkedIn. What's their role? What's their background? Understanding who you're talking to helps you calibrate your questions and shows that you took the time to prepare.
- **How the project connects.** Based on the project brief and what you're learning about the organization, start forming a picture of why this project matters to them. What problem are they trying to solve? Where does your work fit in their bigger picture?

---

## Where to Look

Start with the organization's own website: their "About" page, press releases, and any public reports or case studies. Then broaden:

- **News sources.** Google News, industry publications, or trade media for their sector.
- **Professional networks.** LinkedIn company pages can show you team size, recent hires, and how the organization describes itself.
- **Industry reports.** If the organization operates in a well-documented sector, look for publicly available reports from industry associations or research firms.
- **The project brief itself.** Reread it with fresh eyes after you've done some research. Details that seemed abstract before the kickoff meeting often make more sense once you understand the organizational context.

---

## Using AI for Research

AI is useful for *learning*, not for getting facts. If your employer operates in a field you've never encountered (healthcare technology, sustainable agriculture, financial services), AI is a great way to build your understanding. Have a conversation with it. Ask it to explain the industry in plain language, walk you through how the sector works, or clarify terminology you're seeing in your research. Think of it as a knowledgeable colleague you can ask basic questions without feeling embarrassed.

AI can surface facts too, but its training data has a cutoff date, so anything recent (leadership changes, new products, recent news) may be missing or outdated. And since you'd need to verify what it tells you anyway, you're often better off just going to the company's website, LinkedIn, or a news search directly. Save AI for the learning part, and do your factual research at the source.

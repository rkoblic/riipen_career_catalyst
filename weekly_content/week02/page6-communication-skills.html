Every employer meeting in this course is a professional interaction with a real person who has real expectations. How you communicate in those meetings shapes how the employer perceives your team and the quality of information you walk away with.

This page covers three skills you'll use at every employer touchpoint throughout the course: how to run the meeting itself, how to ask good questions, and how to listen well.

Kickoff Best Practices

The kickoff meeting is your team's first direct interaction with the employer. Here's how to make it count.

Assign Roles Before You Walk In

Before the meeting starts, your team should agree on who does what:

  • Who asks the questions. This doesn't mean one person dominates. It means one person is responsible for making sure the key questions get asked and the conversation stays on track. Others can and should jump in, but having a lead prevents the awkward silence where everyone assumes someone else will speak.
  • Who takes notes. Dedicated note-taking is critical. The note-taker's job is to capture what was said, not interpret or summarize it. You'll have time to process after the meeting.
  • Who manages time. Someone should be quietly tracking the clock. If you have 60 minutes and ten questions, you don't want to spend 40 minutes on the first two. A gentle "We have about 20 minutes left, should we move to our remaining questions?" keeps things moving without being rude.

What You're Trying to Walk Away With

Keep these goals in mind during the meeting:

  • Confirmed project scope. What exactly does the employer want you to deliver? Not what you think they want, but what they've actually said.
  • Success criteria. How will the employer judge whether your work was useful? What does "good" look like to them?
  • Key contacts and constraints. Who else should you talk to? What are the deadlines, budgets, or limitations you need to know about?
  • Open questions. What's still unclear after the meeting? Identifying gaps is just as important as filling them.

When Things Go Off Track

Sometimes the employer will go on a tangent, or the conversation will drift away from your prepared questions. That's normal. It often means they're sharing something they care about, even if it wasn't on your list.

If it's genuinely useful context, let it run and adjust your plan. If it's pulling you away from critical questions you need answered, a polite redirect works:

Example Redirect

"That's really helpful context. I want to make sure we get to a few more questions before our time is up. Could we come back to [topic] if we have time at the end?"

Prioritizing When Time Is Limited

You'll never have enough time to ask everything. Before any employer meeting, rank your questions:

  • Must-ask: Questions where the answer changes what you do next. These are about scope, success criteria, and constraints. If you leave without these, you're guessing.
  • Should-ask: Questions that deepen your understanding but won't block your progress. These cover context, preferences, and the employer's perspective on the industry or problem.
  • Nice-to-ask: Questions that would be interesting but aren't essential. These go at the bottom of the list and get asked if time allows.
Tip

Start with the must-asks. If you get through them early, work your way down. If you run short, you've covered what matters most.

Follow-Up Questions That Go Deeper

The most valuable information often comes from follow-up questions, not your original list. When the employer says something interesting or surprising, push deeper:

  • "Can you say more about that?"
  • "What's driving that priority?"
  • "When you say [term], what does that look like in practice?"
  • "What's been tried before?"

These questions move you past surface-level answers into the thinking behind them, which is where the real insight lives.

Active Listening in Client Meetings

Hearing someone talk and actually listening to what they're saying are two different things. In a professional meeting, active listening is what separates teams that understand their client from teams that think they do.

Note-Taking Without Checking Out

The best way to be present in a meeting is to not be buried in your notes. Consider using an AI transcription or note-taking tool to capture the conversation so your team can focus on listening, asking questions, and reading the room. You'll cover this in more detail before the kickoff meeting, but it's worth thinking about now: the more you can automate the capture, the more you can invest in the conversation itself.

If you do take manual notes, keep them light:

  • Capture keywords and phrases, not full sentences. You'll expand your notes after the meeting.
  • Note what surprised you. If something doesn't match what you expected, flag it. Those moments often point to the most important insights.
  • Use shorthand for follow-up. A simple "?" next to a note means "I need to come back to this." Don't interrupt the flow to chase every thread in real time.

Clarifying Without Interrupting

There's a moment in most meetings where you're not sure what the employer meant. The instinct is to either interrupt immediately or let it go and hope it becomes clear later. Neither is great.

Instead, wait for a natural pause and use a clarifying question: "I want to make sure I understood that correctly. Are you saying [your interpretation]?" This shows you're paying attention and gives the employer a chance to correct any misunderstanding.

If the employer is in the middle of an important thought, jot down your question and come back to it. A quick "Could I circle back to something you mentioned earlier?" works well once there's a break in the conversation.

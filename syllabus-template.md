## Course Description

`[INSTITUTION: You may use or adapt the following catalog description.]`

Students work in teams on real projects for employer partners, developing professional competencies in communication, critical thinking, teamwork, and self-development through a structured 15-week experiential learning cycle. Course activities include employer meetings, collaborative project work, structured reflection, and a formal final presentation. No prior professional experience required.

---

## About This Course

Riipen Career Catalyst is a 15-week experiential learning course where you work on a real project for a real employer — not a simulation, not a case study, a genuine professional engagement. You'll be assigned to a team, matched with an employer partner, and tasked with producing professional-quality deliverables that the employer has identified as meaningful to their organization.

This is not a traditional lecture course. There are no exams. Instead, you'll develop professional competencies — communication, critical thinking, teamwork, professionalism, and career awareness — by doing the kind of work professionals do: researching a problem, developing a strategy, producing deliverables, incorporating feedback, and presenting your work to a real audience.

The course is built on Kolb's experiential learning theory, which holds that lasting learning comes from a cycle of experience, reflection, conceptualization, and experimentation. The structure you'll see in the weekly schedule — module content, project work, employer meetings, reflections — is designed to move you through that cycle repeatedly. Module content gives you frameworks and skills; the project gives you experience; reflections ask you to examine what that experience taught you.

**What makes this course different:**

- Your employer partner is real. They have genuine business needs, and your work matters to them.
- Your team is your primary working unit. Individual accountability happens through reflections, peer evaluations, and professional engagement — not separate individual assignments.
- Feedback is continuous. You'll receive input from your employer, your peers, and your instructor throughout the course — not just a grade at the end.
- Reflection is weighted heavily. This course values what you learn about how you work and think, not just what you produce.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of this course, you will be able to:

1. **Produce professional-quality work for an external client**, including research, analysis, and deliverables that meet employer-defined success criteria.
2. **Collaborate effectively in a team**, navigating the real dynamics of shared work — establishing norms, dividing labor, managing conflict, and holding each other accountable.
3. **Communicate professionally** in written, oral, and interpersonal contexts, including correspondence with an employer partner, formal presentations, and peer feedback.
4. **Think critically about complex, ambiguous problems**, gathering evidence, framing issues, and making defensible recommendations rather than jumping to solutions.
5. **Reflect on your own professional development**, identifying what you've learned about how you work, how you learn, and how you engage with others — and articulating that growth to future employers.

These outcomes align with five NACE (National Association of Colleges and Employers) Career Readiness Competencies: Communication, Critical Thinking, Teamwork, Professionalism, and Career & Self-Development. These are the competencies employers consistently identify as essential for workplace success.

## Course Structure

The course follows a five-phase arc that mirrors how professional projects actually unfold:

| Phase | Weeks | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Foundation** | 1–3 | Orientation, team formation, employer kickoff |
| **Discovery** | 4–6 | Context research, strategic planning, first graded deliverable |
| **Development** | 7–10 | Core project work, feedback cycles, iteration |
| **Refinement** | 11–13 | Final production, quality review, deliverable completion |
| **Integration** | 14–15 | Final presentation, synthesis reflection, transfer |

This structure is consistent across all Riipen Career Catalyst sections — regardless of your specific project, you'll move through the same phases on the same timeline. This is by design: the learning structure carries the course, not the project content.

### Weekly Schedule

Each week combines module content (frameworks and skills), project work (applying what you're learning), and — at key points — employer meetings, reflections, and deliverable submissions.

| Week | Phase | Topic | Deliverable | Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Foundation | Getting Started | Your Starting Point Questionnaire | Checkpoint |
| 2 | Foundation | Building Your Team | Team Charter; Employer Outreach Message | Checkpoint |
| 3 | Foundation | Into the Project | Kickoff Summary; **Reflection #1** | Checkpoint; **Graded** |
| 4 | Discovery | From Research to Strategy | **Context Analysis & Project Plan** | **Graded** |
| 5 | Discovery | Professional Communication | Direction Check Summary | Checkpoint |
| 6 | Discovery | Working Through Ambiguity | **Reflection #2** | **Graded** |
| 7 | Development | Giving and Receiving Feedback | Work-in-Progress Summary | Checkpoint |
| 8 | Development | Making Feedback Useful | **Peer Evaluation #1** | **Graded** |
| 9 | Development | Strengthening Your Work | Draft Deliverable; **Reflection #3** | Checkpoint; **Graded** |
| 10 | Development | Connecting Theory and Practice | — | — |
| 11 | Refinement | Quality and Craft | Near-Final Package | Checkpoint |
| 12 | Refinement | From Feedback to Final | Presentation Outline; **Reflection #4** | Checkpoint; **Graded** |
| 13 | Refinement | Finalizing Deliverables | **Final Deliverables** | **Graded** |
| 14 | Integration | Presenting Your Work | Presentation Prep | Checkpoint |
| 15 | Integration | Transfer and What's Next | **Final Presentation; Peer Eval #2; Reflection #5** | **Graded** |

**Graded** deliverables contribute to your final grade. **Checkpoints** are required for completion but are not quality-graded — they ensure you're keeping pace with the project arc. Detailed instructions for each deliverable are provided in the weekly modules on the course platform.

### Employer Touchpoints

Your employer partner participates at seven points during the course:

| Touchpoint | Week | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Kickoff Meeting | 3 | 60 min |
| Direction Check | 5 | 60 min |
| Progress Review #1 | 7 | 60 min |
| Progress Review #2 | 9 | 60 min |
| Progress Review #3 | 11 | 60 min |
| Progress Review #4 (Final Review) | 13 | 60 min |
| Final Presentation | 15 | 60 min |

All employer meetings are scheduled for 60 minutes. Some weeks you may not need the full time — if the conversation wraps early, the employer can release the remaining time. But hold the full 60 minutes on your calendar for every touchpoint.

These meetings are scheduled on a biweekly rhythm starting in Week 3. Your team is responsible for preparing for each meeting — agendas, materials, and questions — as detailed in the weekly modules. Treat these as professional client meetings: be on time, be prepared, and follow up promptly.

## Assessment and Grading

### How Your Grade Is Calculated

Your final grade is based on four types of evidence, each capturing a different dimension of your learning:

| Component | Weight | What It Demonstrates |
|---|---|---|
| **Project Deliverables** | 40% | Applied skill, work quality, ability to produce professional output |
| **Reflection Portfolio** | 25% | Learning process, self-awareness, ability to extract transferable insight |
| **Professional Engagement** | 20% | Workplace behaviors — reliability, communication, collaboration, responsiveness |
| **Final Presentation** | 15% | Synthesis, communication, ability to present and defend your work |

**Why these weights?** Project deliverables matter, but they don't tell the whole story — a strong deliverable might reflect prior skills rather than learning during the course. Reflection is weighted at 25% because the ability to examine your own experience critically and extract transferable principles is what distinguishes experiential learning from simply completing tasks. Professional engagement captures how you work, not just what you produce. The presentation requires you to synthesize and communicate your work to a real audience — and to answer their questions from your own understanding.

### What's Included in Each Component

**Project Deliverables (40%)** include the Context Analysis & Project Plan (Week 4) and the Final Deliverables (Week 13). These are team submissions assessed using detailed rubrics provided with each assignment.

**Reflection Portfolio (25%)** includes five individual reflections (Weeks 3, 6, 9, 12, and 15), each using the DEAL framework (Describe, Examine, Articulate Learning). All five reflections use the same rubric; what changes is the depth you can bring as you gain experience.

**Professional Engagement (20%)** is assessed through a combination of peer evaluation results, employer feedback, and instructor observation. It covers your reliability, contribution quality, communication, and professional judgment across the full semester.

**Final Presentation (15%)** is a team presentation to your employer and instructor in Week 15. You present your completed work, explain your process, and respond to questions. This is where the "defend your work" standard is most visible.

### Checkpoint Completion Requirement

In addition to the graded components, you must complete course checkpoints. **You cannot pass the course with more than two incomplete checkpoints.** Checkpoints are the scaffolding that keeps you on track — skipping them undermines the structure that supports your success.

## Course Policies

### Time Commitment

This is a 3-credit course. You should expect to spend approximately **9 hours per week** on course activities, including module content, team meetings, project work, employer meetings, and deliverable preparation. The total course commitment is approximately 135 hours.

Early weeks are weighted more toward orientation and module content. As the course progresses, module time decreases and project work time increases — reflecting the natural rhythm of a professional project.

### Attendance and Participation

`[INSTITUTION: Insert your attendance policy. Note that this course includes scheduled employer meetings and regular team meetings — institutions should specify expectations for these synchronous commitments.]`

Employer meetings are professional commitments. Missing a scheduled employer meeting without advance notice to both your team and your instructor is a significant professional engagement concern and will be reflected in your Professional Engagement assessment.

Weekly team meetings are strongly recommended and are an important part of how teams stay coordinated, make decisions, and move the project forward. Teams that meet regularly perform better — teams that don't tend to discover problems too late to fix them.

### Team Expectations

You will work in an assigned team for the duration of the course. In Week 2, your team will develop a Team Charter that establishes your shared agreements on communication, contribution, decision-making, and conflict resolution.

If a team experiences persistent dysfunction that the Team Charter and peer conversation cannot resolve, bring the issue to your instructor promptly. Early intervention works better than suffering in silence.

Peer evaluations at Weeks 8 and 15 assess individual contribution within the team context. These evaluations factor into your teammates' grades — take them seriously and provide honest, specific, constructive feedback. Vague or inflated evaluations don't help anyone and undermine the assessment process. The Week 8 evaluation is formative — it provides mid-course feedback so you and your teammates can adjust before the final evaluation. The Week 15 evaluation is summative and contributes directly to each team member's Professional Engagement grade.

After each peer evaluation, your team is expected to discuss the feedback together and identify concrete adjustments. This is not optional — it's how teams get better. The ability to receive feedback without defensiveness and adapt your behavior accordingly is one of the core professional competencies this course develops.

## AI Use Policy

Artificial intelligence tools are part of the professional landscape you are entering. This course doesn't prohibit AI use — it requires that the thinking is yours.

### The Core Principle

**AI as thinking partner, not ghostwriter.** You may use AI tools to support your work. You may not use AI tools to produce work that you submit as your own thinking, research, or judgment. The test: could you explain and defend every claim, recommendation, and decision in your submission without referring back to what an AI told you? If yes, your use is appropriate. If no, you've crossed the line.

You will be asked to do exactly this at your Final Presentation — the employer and instructor will ask questions about your findings and recommendations, and your answers need to come from your own understanding.

### What's Encouraged

AI tools can be used productively for getting oriented in an unfamiliar domain, brainstorming options, improving the clarity of writing you've already drafted, explaining unfamiliar concepts, getting feedback on drafts, and organizing information you've developed. These are good professional practices.

### What's Not Permitted

AI tools may not be used to generate reflections (reflections must come from your actual experience), fabricate meeting content (summaries must capture what actually happened), or substitute AI judgment for yours through uncritical pass-through of generated content.

### AI Disclosure

Every submission includes a brief AI Disclosure — a short paragraph at the end of your work stating which AI tools you used (if any), what you used them for, and what you contributed yourself. This is a professional transparency norm, not a punishment. If you didn't use AI, a one-sentence statement is sufficient.

## How to Succeed in This Course

This course rewards engagement, not perfection. Here's what students who do well tend to have in common:

**Start early and stay current.** The weekly structure builds on itself. Falling behind in Week 4 creates problems in Week 7 that compound through Week 13. The checkpoints exist to keep you on pace — treat them as your minimum, not your target.

**Take the employer relationship seriously.** Your employer partner is investing their time because they believe your work can be valuable to their organization. Show up prepared. Follow up promptly. Ask good questions. This is a professional relationship, and how you conduct yourself matters.

**Do the reflection work honestly.** It's tempting to treat reflections as a writing exercise to get through. The students who get the most from this course are the ones who use reflections to actually examine their experience — including the uncomfortable parts. Reflection is where learning becomes durable.

**Talk to your team — especially when things are hard.** Every team hits rough patches. The teams that succeed talk about problems early instead of letting resentment build. Use your Team Charter. Use your peer evaluations constructively. Ask your instructor for help before a small issue becomes a big one.

**Use your instructor.** `[INSTITUTION: Adapt this to your support model — office hours, advising, check-ins, etc.]` Your instructor has seen how these projects unfold and can help you navigate ambiguity, team dynamics, employer communication, and the moments where the project feels stuck. Reaching out is a sign of professionalism, not weakness.

## Learning Objectives

By the end of Week 7, students will be able to:

## Weekly Activity Breakdown

Week 7 marks the transition into the Development phase and is built around two priorities: equipping students with production skills for the client-facing work they are now building, and preparing them for Check-In #1, a questions-based employer meeting where students bring specific questions and decision points rather than presenting a formal progress report. The module content has been rebalanced from the original feedback-only design to serve both needs. Students get a compressed but complete feedback reception protocol (just-in-time for Check-In #1) alongside new production skill content — professional document conventions, peer draft review, and data/visual presentation — that they need as they enter the building phase. The "Why Feedback Feels Hard" framing is folded into the receiving protocol as a brief intro rather than a standalone section; students are about to experience real feedback from a real employer, and the lived experience will teach this lesson more effectively than a module. The WIP Summary assignment was not previewed in Week 6, so this week includes time for students to read the assignment description and completion criteria before drafting. The production block remains substantial despite the meeting load and expanded module because Week 7 is still a core production week — teams need to continue building their deliverables alongside the feedback cycle. This week also introduces the Weekly Progress Check-In as a standing prompt embedded in the async communication activity, continuing through Week 13.

## Deliverable Connection

**Work-in-Progress Summary → Communication (Written Clarity) + Professionalism (Feedback Reception)**

Checkpoint; completion-based. Team submission. The WIP Summary serves a dual function: it is the checkpoint deliverable for Week 7 and the preparation artifact for Check-In #1. Teams produce a 1–2 page document covering completed work, current status, challenges encountered, and specific questions or feedback needs for the employer. The assignment was not previewed in Week 6, so activity 7.07 allocates time for students to read the assignment description and completion criteria before drafting.

The WIP Summary is a student preparation tool, not an employer pre-read — the employer does not receive or review the document. Students use the summary to organize their thinking and drive their live presentation in the meeting. The submission requirement ensures teams cannot walk into the meeting unprepared. The completion criteria require that the summary provides an honest assessment of progress and identifies specific feedback needs — vague or performative summaries ("everything is going well, no questions") do not meet the standard. A team whose summary does not match what they present will surface the discrepancy in the live conversation.

Employers do not provide written feedback on draft deliverables. The feedback mechanism for Check-In #1 is the live meeting itself — students present their work, receive verbal feedback in real time, and capture it in the post-meeting debrief (7.10). This keeps the employer touchpoint lightweight and realistic. The debrief is the critical accountability point: teams that listened well will have specific, actionable notes; teams that didn't will have vague ones.

AI-assisted drafting of the summary is permitted with disclosure. The substance must come from the team's actual project status — AI cannot generate an honest assessment of what the team has and has not accomplished. AI can help organize and clarify the summary after the team has agreed on its content.

## AI in This Week

**Permitted and encouraged:**

Using AI to help organize and clarify the Work-in-Progress Summary after the team has agreed on its substance is appropriate — restructuring a draft for clarity, tightening prose, or ensuring the document flows logically are legitimate uses that mirror professional practice. Using AI to prepare for the Check-In #1 by generating potential questions the employer might ask about the draft work is encouraged: this is a form of stress-testing that helps students anticipate feedback rather than being caught off guard. Using AI to help process and organize feedback notes after the Check-In #1 debrief is permitted — converting raw meeting notes into a structured action item list or identifying patterns across multiple pieces of feedback are reasonable applications. Module 7.05 explicitly teaches using AI to help visualize data while maintaining accuracy — students may use AI to generate chart options or suggest data presentation formats, provided they verify the output and can defend their choices. Continued use of AI for production support in activity 7.12 follows the same boundaries established in Week 6: AI can help with research, drafting, structural review, and gap identification.

**Off-limits this week:**

The Work-in-Progress Summary must reflect the team's actual project status. AI cannot generate an honest assessment of what has been completed, what challenges exist, or what questions the team needs answered — those must come from the team's genuine experience with the project. The post-meeting debrief notes (7.10) must capture what was actually said in the Check-In #1; AI can help organize notes after the fact but cannot fabricate the content of the conversation. AI-generated content in draft deliverables that students cannot verify or defend remains off-limits — the Check-In #1 is a live accountability mechanism, and students who cannot explain their work under employer questioning will have a poor experience.

**Disclosure required:** Yes — on the Work-in-Progress Summary submission. If AI was not used, a one-sentence statement to that effect is sufficient.

## Resolved Decisions

**1. WIP Summary submission timing relative to the Check-In #1.** ✅ RESOLVED. The WIP Summary is submitted before the Check-In #1 meeting as a checkpoint (completion-based, verified but not graded for quality). It is a student preparation tool, not an employer pre-read — the employer does not receive or review the document. Students use the summary to organize their thinking and drive their live presentation in the meeting. The submission requirement ensures teams cannot walk into the meeting unprepared.

**2. Employer written feedback.** ✅ RESOLVED. Employers do not provide written feedback on draft deliverables. The feedback mechanism for Check-In #1 is the live meeting itself — students present their work, receive verbal feedback in real time, and capture it in the post-meeting debrief (7.10). This keeps the employer touchpoint lightweight and realistic, and it makes the debrief activity the critical accountability point.

**3. Feedback Protocol Card as a resource.** The Feedback Protocol Card is a reference resource introduced alongside module 7.02. Not listed as a separate timed activity. Affects: resource library configuration, module content packaging.

Original feedback-only module (60 min across 4 sections) replaced with production skill + feedback hybrid (75 min across 6 sections). "Why Feedback Feels Hard" folded into "Receiving Feedback Well" as brief framing. "Giving Useful Feedback" reframed as "Reviewing Your Teammates' Work."

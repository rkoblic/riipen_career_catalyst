## Weekly Activity Breakdown

Week 15 is the final week of the course and the densest in terms of consequential work: three graded submissions (Final Presentation, Reflection #5, Peer Evaluation #2), the last employer touchpoint, and the team's formal closing. The module content is light (35 minutes) and entirely practical — a brief presentation-day prep module and a career articulation module that helps students translate the experience into professional self-presentation. The anchor event is the Final Presentation, one of only two formal student presentations in the course (the other was the Direction Check in Week 5). The presentation arc that began conceptually in Week 12 and became operational in Week 14 reaches its conclusion here. Reflection #5 and Peer Evaluation #2 were both started in Week 14; this week, students incorporate insights from the presentation and team closing before finalizing both. The post-presentation debrief serves double duty: capturing employer feedback while fresh and giving the team a structured ending.

## Deliverable Connection

**Final Presentation → Communication (Oral Presentation, Active Listening) + Professionalism (Quality & Attention to Detail)**

Graded; rubric-based. Team submission. Worth 15% of the course grade. The Final Presentation is a 60-minute formal event: students present their completed work to the employer and course supervisor, respond to questions, and receive feedback. The presentation rubric assesses the team's ability to communicate their work to a professional audience through narrative structure, visual support, speaker coordination, and Q&A performance.

The presentation arc spans three weeks: Presentation Fundamentals in Week 12 (conceptual), Planning Your Presentation and Handling Q&A in Week 14 (operational), deck build and rehearsal in Week 14 (production), and delivery in Week 15. The polish session (15.04) is not a rehearsal but a final-pass preparation — practicing opening and closing, confirming timing, and reviewing anticipated questions.

AI-assisted slide design and speaker notes are permitted (with disclosure). The defend-your-work standard applies to the Q&A: students must respond from genuine understanding of the research, analysis, and recommendations. Answers that reveal the student cannot explain their own team's work will be visible to the employer, the course supervisor, and the grading rubric.

**Reflection #5 → Career & Self-Development (Reflection, Growth Orientation, Career Articulation)**

Graded; rubric-based. Individual submission. Contributes ~5% of the final grade as part of the Reflection Portfolio (25% total, five reflections). The fifth and final DEAL reflection. Students examine the full 15-week course arc and identify the single most important thing they learned about how they work, learn, or engage professionally — not about the project's subject matter but about themselves. The experiential pool is the entire course: from the kickoff meeting in Week 3 through the Final Presentation in Week 15.

The same rubric applies as for all reflections: Descriptive Specificity (25%), Critical Examination (25%), Personal Accountability (25%), Articulation of Learning (25%). The calibration expectation is the highest of any reflection: students should demonstrate precise description, probing examination, and articulated learning that is both specific enough to be actionable and broad enough to transfer to future professional contexts. The distance between Reflection #5 and Reflection #1 should be evident in every dimension.

Drafting began in Week 14 (14.10). The Final Presentation, employer feedback, and team closing (15.06) may surface final insights to incorporate. AI-as-Socratic-partner prompt is embedded in the assignment. AI-assisted drafting is off-limits. AI disclosure required on submission via Riipen.

**Peer Evaluation #2 → Teamwork (Reliability, Collaborative Contribution, Constructive Feedback, Shared Accountability)**

Contributes to Professional Engagement grade (not graded as a standalone deliverable). Peer Evaluation #2 is the summative end-of-project evaluation, covering the full arc of collaboration from Week 1 through Week 15. The evaluation carries 70% of the overall peer evaluation score (the mid-project evaluation in Week 8 carries 30%), which is 50% of the Professional Engagement component (20% of course grade).

The instrument includes: (A) BARS ratings on four dimensions (Reliability, Contribution, Constructive Engagement, Accountability) with behavioral evidence for each rating; (B) contribution allocation (100 points distributed among teammates, unequal distribution required); (C) self-evaluation on the same four dimensions; (D) closure questions visible only to the instructor. Students evaluate the full arc, not just the final weeks — a teammate who started strong but disengaged in the Refinement phase is assessed on the full pattern, not just the ending.

Started in Week 14 (14.11). AI cannot generate the evaluation — observations and ratings must reflect the student's genuine assessment of their teammate's behavior. AI disclosure required.

## AI in This Week

**Permitted and encouraged:**

For the Final Presentation, AI-assisted slide polish and speaker note refinement continues from Week 14. At this stage, the slides should already be built — AI use is most appropriate for final-pass work: checking that slides are visually consistent, tightening speaker notes, and preparing concise answers to anticipated questions. Using AI to simulate Q&A is encouraged as final rehearsal preparation — prompting AI with the presentation content and asking it to generate challenging questions the employer might ask, then practicing answers out loud (not in writing).

Module 15.03 (Articulating Your Experience) introduces a new AI application: using AI to refine professional self-presentation. This includes prompting AI to strengthen resume bullets, practice interview responses, and identify which accomplishments to lead with. This is distinct from AI-assisted reflection because the task is packaging and communicating learning, not generating or examining it. A student who uses AI to turn "I helped with a project for a credit union" into "Led market research synthesis for a regional financial institution, translating customer data into a strategic recommendations deck that informed the client's membership strategy" is using AI to articulate what they actually did — not to fabricate what they didn't.

**Off-limits this week:**

AI-assisted drafting is off-limits for Reflection #5. This is the final and most important reflection in the portfolio — the one that requires students to look across the full course arc and identify what changed in how they work professionally. Only the student can do this because only the student was there for all 15 weeks. AI as a Socratic thinking partner is permitted and encouraged: asking AI to push back on "I learned about teamwork" and challenge the student to articulate what specifically changed about how they collaborate, communicate, or approach ambiguity.

AI cannot generate the peer evaluation. The qualitative feedback, BARS ratings, contribution allocation, and self-evaluation must all reflect the student's genuine observations and assessment. The closure questions (Part D) are especially sensitive — these are instructor-only signals about team dynamics that AI-generated responses would undermine.

**Disclosure required:** AI disclosure required on the Peer Evaluation #2 submission and the Reflection #5 submission. Presentation slides should include an AI disclosure if AI was used in their creation.

**Prompt strategy reinforced:** Three AI strategies conclude this week. The production partner role (Week 8+) ends with final slide polish. The Socratic partner strategy for reflection (introduced Week 1, applied in every reflection) reaches its most demanding application in Reflection #5. The new application — AI for professional self-presentation (Module 15.03) — introduces the last AI use case in the course and is explicitly about what comes after: using AI to articulate professional experience is a skill students will use beyond this course.

## Design Decisions

**1. Three graded submissions in one week.** Week 15 is the most submission-heavy week in the course. This is by design: the Final Presentation, Reflection #5, and Peer Evaluation #2 must all occur at the end of the course because they each depend on the full experience. The workload is manageable because both the reflection and peer evaluation were started in Week 14 — students are finalizing, not starting from scratch. The total time (490 min / ~8 hrs 10 min) is within the 9-hour ceiling and comparable to other heavy weeks (Week 3 at 565 min, Week 4 at 585 min).

**2. Peer Evaluation #2 time allocation reflects scope.** At 150 minutes, Peer Evaluation #2 is the single largest activity this week. This is not padding — evaluating three teammates across the full project arc with specific behavioral evidence for each BARS dimension, completing qualitative feedback, distributing contribution points, completing a self-evaluation, and answering closure questions takes real time if done thoughtfully. The approximately 45–50 minutes per teammate (plus self-evaluation and closure questions) is what the instrument demands. Students who rush through it produce thin evidence that undermines the evaluation's utility for both grading and teammate development.

**3. Sequencing: present, close, then reflect and evaluate.** The recommended completion order — present first, debrief and close as a team, then finalize Reflection #5 and Peer Evaluation #2 — is deliberate. The presentation experience and the team closing both generate material that strengthens the reflection and informs the evaluation. A student who writes Reflection #5 before presenting is missing the culminating experience of the course. A student who completes Peer Evaluation #2 before the team closing is evaluating an incomplete picture. The sequencing maximizes the quality of both individual submissions.

**4. Team closing as a designed activity, not an afterthought.** Activity 15.06 includes a structured team closing alongside the post-presentation debrief. This is intentional: experiential learning literature emphasizes that endings matter. Teams that disband without acknowledging what they built together miss a developmental opportunity. The structured format — what we accomplished, what we learned from each other, individual acknowledgments — gives students a framework for closing an experience that many have never had before. It also surfaces insights and observations that feed both Reflection #5 and Peer Evaluation #2.

**5. Articulating Your Experience as the final module.** Module 15.03 is the last instructional content in the course, and it's forward-facing: it helps students translate the project experience into professional self-presentation for contexts beyond the course. This is a deliberate choice about what the course's final teaching moment should be. The course has spent 14 weeks building professional competencies; the final module helps students learn to communicate what they've built. It also introduces the last AI use case — using AI to refine professional framing — which is a skill students will use in job searches, applications, and professional networking beyond this course.

**6. Module content is light and practical.** At 35 minutes total across two modules, Week 15 has among the lightest module content in the course. Presentation Day Prep (15.02) is not instruction — it's a brief mindset module for students who have already learned the concepts (Week 12) and practiced the execution (Week 14). Articulating Your Experience (15.03) teaches a genuinely new skill but one that requires concrete application rather than extensive instruction. The rest of the week's time is allocated to doing: presenting, closing, reflecting, and evaluating.

**7. The presentation tests the full arc, not just presentation skills.** The Final Presentation is graded as a communication competency assessment (15% of course grade), but it also functions as an implicit accountability check on the entire project. Students who outsourced thinking to AI, disengaged from the research, or let teammates carry the analysis will be exposed during Q&A — the defend-your-work standard from Module 14.03 applies. The employer and course supervisor will ask questions, and the answers must come from genuine understanding of the work. This is not a gotcha — it is the professional standard that the entire course has been building toward.

**8. No Open Questions section.** Week 15 is the course's conclusion. The design is settled, the activities are specified, and the assessment framework is in place. Any remaining implementation questions (platform logistics for peer evaluation submission, presentation scheduling, grade calculation procedures) are operational matters addressed in facilitator materials, not design decisions that affect the weekly structure.

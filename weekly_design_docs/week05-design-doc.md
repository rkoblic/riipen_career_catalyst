By the end of Week 5, students will be able to:

## Weekly Activity Breakdown

Week 5 is organized around a single anchor event — the Direction Check — and everything else in the week either prepares for it or follows from it. The module content is sequenced as just-in-time skill instruction: students complete all four sections before they prepare their talking points, so they arrive at the meeting having just learned what good professional communication in this context looks like. The post-meeting arc (debrief → Summary → plan revision) is kept as four distinct line items rather than collapsed, because each step represents a different cognitive task: capturing what was heard, writing it up formally, and then deciding what to do about it. The week closes with forward-looking production work that sets up Week 6 production.

| Competency Area | Subskill | How This Week Builds It |
| :---- | :---- | :---- |

## Deliverable Connection

**Direction Check Summary → Communication (Written Clarity) + Professionalism (Feedback Reception)**

Checkpoint; completion-based. Team submission. Not graded on a rubric — assessed on whether the summary documents specific feedback received, identifies concrete adjustments or confirms the approach, and captures next steps. This checkpoint exists because the Direction Check is a verbal interaction: what the employer said, what the team heard, and what they decided to do about it can diverge significantly if not written down promptly. Pivots documented in the Summary are not failures — they are evidence of responsiveness, which is a professional competency in its own right.

The Summary has one page and three components: feedback received (what the employer said, as specifically as possible), plan adjustments (what changes, and why), and confirmed approach (what stays the same, and why). The distinction between the debrief (5.10) and the Summary (5.11) is intentional: the debrief is a team conversation about what was heard, the Summary is the formal written record of what was decided. Both have standalone value and cannot be collapsed without losing the thinking that happens in between.

**AI guidance specific to this deliverable:** AI can help organize and clarify notes into the Summary format after the team has captured what was actually said. AI cannot generate the substance of what the employer communicated. A Summary that reads as though it was produced before the meeting happened — generic, unspecific, lacking any reference to what Sarah Chen actually said — has not met the completion criteria, regardless of how it was produced. AI disclosure is required on submission.

## AI in This Week

**Permitted and encouraged:**

Using AI transcription or summarization tools during or immediately after the Direction Check meeting is permitted — this is the same practice established in Week 3 at the kickoff, and it reflects how professionals routinely use AI in client-facing contexts. Using AI to help organize and structure talking points before the meeting is appropriate: the findings themselves come from the team's own work in Week 4, and AI can help make the presentation of those findings clear and logically sequenced. Using AI to orient to unfamiliar formats, domain concepts, or skill gaps — whatever independent learning each student identifies as needed — is the primary use case for activity 5.15 and is explicitly encouraged. Using AI to help draft the Direction Check Summary after the team has captured what was actually said supports the writing task without substituting for the listening that precedes it.

**Off-limits this week:**

The Direction Check Summary must reflect the actual conversation with the employer. AI cannot generate what the employer said; it can only help the team organize and clarify notes they have already taken. A Summary produced primarily from AI rather than from meeting notes fails the completion criteria for a straightforward reason: it cannot document something AI was not present for. No new prompt strategy is introduced this week — the domain orientation strategy introduced in Week 4 continues to apply to the independent learning activity (5.15).

**Disclosure required:** Yes — on the Direction Check Summary submission, regardless of the extent of AI use. If AI was not used, a one-sentence statement is sufficient.

## Open Questions / Flags

**1. Employer touchpoint timing and course flexibility guidance.** The week is designed with the Direction Check as the anchor event, but the course cannot fully control when standing employer meetings occur — scheduling constraints, employer availability, and institutional calendars will all create variation. This is a structural reality across all employer touchpoint weeks, not just Week 5. Students need explicit guidance early in the course (likely Week 1 or the course syllabus) that weeks should be treated flexibly: when a meeting falls late in a week, the post-meeting work carries into the following week; when students have lighter weeks, they should use that time to get ahead on upcoming tasks. Without this framing established upfront, students who encounter a late Direction Check will experience the week as broken rather than as a normal feature of real-world project work. Affects: Week 1 course orientation content, syllabus language, facilitator materials for all employer touchpoint weeks.

**2. Employer pre-read of the Context Analysis & Project Plan.** The Direction Check is most productive when the employer has reviewed the Week 4 submission before the meeting. Contingency guidance for students when the employer arrives unprepared is addressed in module 5.04. The underlying handoff question — how the document reaches the employer and when — remains unresolved from Week 4. Affects: employer onboarding expectations, platform or LMS configuration.

**3. Graded feedback turnaround time.** Activity 5.06 asks students to review graded feedback on their Context Analysis & Project Plan as part of Direction Check prep — the "if available" qualifier acknowledges that feedback may not yet be returned when students need it. The realistic turnaround time for graded submissions needs to be established and communicated clearly: if the standard is 5–7 days and the Direction Check happens in the first half of Week 5, feedback will likely be available; if the meeting falls early and grading is slow, students may prep for the Direction Check without it. This affects both student expectations and the design of 5.06 — if feedback is reliably unavailable by the Direction Check, the activity should be reframed accordingly. Affects: grading timeline expectations, faculty/facilitator guidelines, student-facing course policies.

## Weekly Activity Breakdown

Week 4 marks the first full production week of the Discovery phase and the first graded deliverable of the course. Students arrive with meaningful prior scaffolding: they have been introduced to what a project plan is and why it exists (Week 3, activity 3.16), have progressed through two prior stages of the research skills sequence (organizational reconnaissance in Week 2, targeted landscape orientation in Week 3), and reviewed the Context Analysis & Project Plan assignment and rubric in Week 2. The module content this week is therefore not starting from zero — it is teaching the final analytical layer that prior weeks deliberately deferred. The six module sections address the skills required to move from information-gathering to analytical argument: synthesizing findings, reasoning from evidence, drawing strategic implications, prioritizing, identifying risk, and using AI appropriately for research tasks. This content is heavier than surrounding Discovery weeks precisely because every section maps directly to a rubric criterion; students cannot be assessed on evidence-based reasoning or risk identification if those concepts have not yet been explicitly taught. The project work structure — individual research, team synthesis, individual drafting, async cross-review, integration pass — is designed to produce a document that functions as a genuine team product rather than four separate pieces stitched together.

## Deliverable Connection

**Context Analysis & Project Plan → Critical Thinking (Research & Analysis, Evidence-Based Reasoning, Problem Framing, Prioritization) + Communication (Written Clarity)**

Graded; rubric-based. Team submission. Worth 10% of the final course grade. This is the first graded deliverable in the course and the one that establishes whether students have done the analytical work that will make their project valuable rather than simply executed on instructions received at the kickoff. All team members are accountable for the whole document; it is submitted once per team.

The deliverable has four parts: Organizational & Project Context (~1 page), Research Findings (~1.5–2 pages), Strategic Implications (~1 page), and Project Plan (~1.5–2 pages). The rubric has five dimensions: Communication Quality (20%), Analytical Rigor (25%), Actionability (20%), Professionalism (15%), and a Project-Specific Generated Dimension (20%). The project-specific dimension is derived from the employer's project brief and is intended to capture analytical work particular to that team's context — a dimension that would look different for a team researching youth banking trends than for a team examining supply chain logistics.

The deliverable is assessed on the quality of research and reasoning, not on whether the employer validates the approach. The Direction Check (Week 5) is explicitly the mechanism for alignment; pivots based on employer feedback are expected and do not reflect poorly on the Week 4 submission.

**AI guidance specific to this deliverable:** AI-assisted research is permitted — using AI to generate an initial competitive landscape overview or to identify patterns across gathered findings is appropriate. The accountability mechanism is the Direction Check: students who cannot explain their findings under employer questioning will have a poor experience, which naturally incentivizes genuine engagement rather than uncritical pass-through. AI disclosure is required on submission. The specific boundary is this: AI-generated content the team cannot verify or defend should not be in the deliverable.

**Submission:** Via LMS, end of Week 4 (activity 4.15, 10 min).

## AI in This Week

**Permitted and encouraged:**

Using AI to generate an initial competitive landscape or industry overview as a starting point is explicitly permitted — the expectation is that students read the output critically, verify key claims against real sources, push back on what seems wrong or incomplete, and can walk the employer through the findings confidently at the Direction Check. Using AI to identify patterns across research findings gathered from multiple team members is appropriate synthesis support. Using AI to understand unfamiliar task formats — what does a competitive analysis typically include, what goes in a risk register — is encouraged and supports activity 4.09 directly. Using AI to support the integration pass (4.13) is a legitimate use case: asking AI to identify where the document is inconsistent, where transitions are unclear, or where sections contradict each other mirrors how professionals use AI as a document editing tool.

**Off-limits this week:**

AI-generated research that students cannot verify or defend is not permitted. This is not primarily a policy enforcement issue — it is a downstream consequence issue. The Direction Check in Week 5 is a direct accountability mechanism, and students who cannot explain their findings under employer questioning will have a poor experience. The test stated in the course AI policy applies here: could you explain and defend every claim in your submission without referring back to what an AI told you? If no, it should not be in the document.

**Disclosure required:** Yes — on the Context Analysis & Project Plan submission, regardless of the extent of AI use. If AI was not used, a one-sentence statement to that effect is sufficient.

**Prompt strategy introduced this week — Using AI for Research and Synthesis:**

This week introduces a new prompt strategy explicitly taught in module 4.07. Two anchor prompts:

- For competitive landscape: *"I am researching [industry/sector] for a student consulting project. Generate an overview of the key players, what they offer, and how they differentiate. Flag any claims I should verify independently."*
- For synthesis: *"Here are research findings from four team members on [topic]. Identify the three to five most important patterns or tensions across these findings, and explain what each one might mean for how we approach the project."*

Both prompts are designed to position AI as a starting point that requires student judgment to evaluate, not a finishing point that can be submitted without engagement.

## Open Questions / Flags

**1. Employer access to the Context Analysis & Project Plan.** The Direction Check agenda assumes the employer has read the document before the meeting — the Week 5 scenario describes the employer having "reviewed it" in advance. Two questions are unresolved: (a) how does the document get to the employer — does submission to the LMS automatically share it, does the team send it directly, or does Riipen facilitate the handoff? and (b) when should it be shared — at the moment of submission at the end of Week 4, or a specified number of days before the Direction Check meeting to give the employer adequate review time? The answer affects student instructions at the end of Week 4, employer onboarding expectations, and whether a platform workflow needs to be built to support the handoff. Affects: student-facing submission instructions, employer touchpoint prep, platform or LMS configuration.

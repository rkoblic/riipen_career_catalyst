## Weekly Activity Breakdown

Week 11 opens the Refinement phase. The shift from Development is deliberate: students are no longer building new content but polishing, integrating, and defending what they have. The module content is light (50 min across four segments) and focused entirely on the quality standard students need to meet — what "polished" means in a professional context, how to self-review before a client sees the work, and how to seek the right kind of feedback at this late stage. The anchor is the Near-Final Review, where teams share their deliverable package with the employer for review and bring targeted questions — this is a document-sharing meeting, not a formal presentation. Only Weeks 5 and 15 involve formal student presentations. Production time is substantial (210 min) but scoped to refinement rather than generation. The Near-Final Package checkpoint submission creates accountability for having the work in reviewable condition.

## Deliverable Connection

**Near-Final Package → Professionalism (Quality & Attention to Detail) + Communication (Written Clarity)**

Checkpoint; completion-based. Team submission. The Near-Final Package is a forcing function — it requires teams to assemble all deliverable components into a coherent package and submit it for review before the employer sees it in the Near-Final Review. The checkpoint is not quality-graded, but the completion standard is substantive: the package should represent work that is close to final, not a rough draft relabeled. Teams that submit incomplete or disorganized packages will find the Near-Final Review less productive because employer feedback will address fundamentals rather than quality.

The Near-Final Package is not a separate deliverable from the Final Deliverables due in Week 13 — it is the same work at a near-final stage. The checkpoint creates accountability for having the work in reviewable condition by Week 11, ensuring that Weeks 12–13 are spent on targeted revision rather than first-draft production. The Final Deliverables rubric (five dimensions: Deliverable Quality 30%, Analytical Rigor 25%, Actionability 20%, Communication 15%, Responsiveness to Feedback 10%) applies to the Week 13 submission, not this checkpoint.

AI-assisted polish and editing is permitted and encouraged at this stage — tightening prose, improving consistency, catching errors. AI disclosure required on submission. The substance of the deliverable package should reflect the team's own research, analysis, and recommendations built over the preceding weeks.

## AI in This Week

**Permitted and encouraged:**

Production-oriented AI use from Week 8 onward continues and intensifies during Refinement. The AI as Quality Reviewer strategy introduced in Week 10 (10.05) is the primary new application this week. Using AI to evaluate the deliverable against the self-review protocol (11.03) — identifying formatting inconsistencies, catching grammatical errors, flagging sections where the argument is weak or the evidence thin — is encouraged and mirrors professional practice. Using AI to help prepare the Change Log for the Near-Final Review is appropriate: organizing feedback received across multiple touchpoints into a clear narrative of what was addressed and why. Using AI to generate likely employer questions about the near-final work is a continuation of the stress-testing strategy introduced in Week 7 — useful for the prep session (11.08) even though the meeting itself is document-sharing rather than presentation-based.

**Off-limits this week:**

No specific prohibitions beyond the standing course policy. The near-final stage makes uncritical pass-through particularly risky — AI-polished prose that the team cannot explain or defend will surface in the Near-Final Review conversation and again at the Final Presentation in Week 15.

**Disclosure required:** AI disclosure required on the Near-Final Package checkpoint submission.

**Prompt strategy reinforced:** The quality review application extends the production partner role (Week 8+) into a new domain. Rather than using AI to generate content, students use it to evaluate their own work against professional standards — a shift from creation to critical assessment. Module 11.03 embeds this by suggesting students prompt AI with their deliverable and ask it to identify the three weakest sections and explain why, then compare that assessment to their own internal review gate findings.

## Design Decisions

**1. Refinement phase opens with a quality orientation.** The module content for Week 11 is entirely about quality — what it means (11.02), how to self-review for it (11.03), and how to seek feedback on it (11.04). This is not a coincidence. The shift from Development to Refinement is the shift from "Is it done?" to "Is it good?" Every module addresses a different facet of that question, and every project activity applies the answer.

**2. Near-Final Review is a document-sharing meeting, not a presentation.** The design consistently distinguishes this touchpoint from the formal presentations in Weeks 5 and 15. This is intentional: the document should be able to stand on its own. Students who need to "walk the employer through" every section have a document problem, not a meeting-format problem. The prep session (11.08) focuses on preparing the document and developing questions, not rehearsing a presentation.

**3. Change Log walkthrough as a new employer communication practice.** For the first time, students present their engagement with prior feedback as a structured narrative. The Change Log (introduced 10.04) makes this possible — teams can walk through what feedback they received, what they did about it, and why they chose not to act on certain items. This is a professional skill that doesn't exist in most academic contexts, where feedback is received and acted on (or not) invisibly.

**4. Internal review gate serves a different purpose than in Week 10.** The Week 10 gate (10.08) focused on identifying gaps before Refinement. The Week 11 gate (11.07) focuses on quality and presentation before the employer sees the work. Same practice, different lens — and the shift mirrors the phase transition from Development to Refinement.

**5. Production block sized for refinement, not generation.** The 210-minute production block is substantial but smaller than Week 10's 300-minute block. This reflects the design assumption that by Week 11, the deliverable content is largely complete and the remaining work is refinement — tightening, polishing, ensuring consistency. The block is sized for revision work, not first-draft production. Teams that are still generating new content in Week 11 have a timeline problem that the Near-Final Review will make visible.

**6. Twelve activities reflects genuine structural complexity.** Week 11 has more activities than most weeks because the Near-Final Review creates a three-part sequence (prep → meeting → debrief) that doesn't compress well, and the checkpoint submission and internal review gate add distinct steps. Each activity serves a specific function; none is padding.

**7. No graded deliverable — but the checkpoint matters.** The Near-Final Package is completion-based, not quality-graded. But the completion standard is substantive: the package should be close enough to final that employer feedback addresses quality, not fundamentals. This is a design choice about where to place assessment pressure. Quality-grading the near-final package would duplicate the Final Deliverables rubric; completion-grading it creates a forcing function without the risk that students treat an intermediate submission as summative.

## Weekly Activity Breakdown

Week 9 carries triple demands: students are processing feedback from multiple sources (peer evaluation results, employer mid-project survey, and the upcoming Check-In #2), pushing their draft deliverable toward a submission-ready state, and completing their third DEAL reflection. The module content teaches production skills that directly serve the draft: multi-author integration, the actionability test, and scope management — the practical problems students encounter as they assemble individually drafted sections into a coherent document for an external audience. The peer evaluation conversation module is tightly scoped to a practical protocol rather than theory, since students have been collaborating for eight weeks and don't need abstract teamwork concepts. The Team Processing Session gives teams a structured hour to synthesize feedback from three sources (peer eval, employer survey, Check-In #2) and agree on specific behavioral changes. The internal review gate introduces a recurring professional practice — reading assembled work as a complete document before an external audience sees it — that will recur before each subsequent checkpoint and graded submission.

## Deliverable Connection

**Draft Deliverable (with team processing agreement) → Communication (Written Clarity) + Critical Thinking (Prioritization, Evidence-Based Reasoning)**

Checkpoint; completion-based. Team submission. The Draft Deliverable is the second-to-last checkpoint before the Final Deliverables submission in Week 13. It serves two functions: first, it creates a forcing function for teams to assemble their separate sections into a coherent whole — not just a collection of individually drafted parts, but a document that reads as a unified product. Second, it gives the employer substantive work to respond to in Check-In #2, enabling more specific and useful feedback than they could provide on the WIP Summary in Week 7.

The checkpoint does not assess quality — it verifies that teams have made substantive progress and are producing genuine work product rather than outlines or placeholders. However, the module content this week (multi-author integration, actionability, scope management) is designed to raise the quality of what gets submitted. Teams that engage with the module content will produce a more coherent draft; teams that skip it will submit work that reads like "separate papers stapled together" and will receive exactly that feedback from the employer in Check-In #2.

The team processing agreement is appended to the Draft Deliverable submission rather than submitted as a separate artifact. This collapses two potential submissions into one and frames the agreement as part of the team's working process rather than a standalone bureaucratic requirement. The agreement documents specific behavioral changes the team has committed to based on peer evaluation and employer feedback, creating a reference point for Peer Evaluation #2 in Week 15 — if the same issues surface in the summative evaluation, there is documented evidence that the behavior was named and not corrected.

AI disclosure is required on the Draft Deliverable submission and on Reflection #3. AI-assisted drafting, structural review, and integration work are all permitted on the deliverable — the production-oriented AI guidance from Week 8 (module 8.05) applies. AI cannot substitute for the team's own judgment about what to include, how to prioritize recommendations, or what scope decisions to make.

**Reflection #3 → Career & Self-Development (Reflection)**

Graded; rubric-based. Individual submission. Contributes ~5% of the final grade as part of the Reflection Portfolio (25% total, five reflections). Students draw from the most intensive feedback and production period (Weeks 7–9) — Check-In #1 feedback, peer evaluation, team dynamics under production pressure, or any moment of learning involving feedback or collaboration. Module 9.14 (DEAL Reflection — Going Deeper) provides scaffolding focused on the Examine stage. The expectation for depth is higher than Reflections #1 and #2; the framework is no longer new, and the material from this period is rich. AI-as-Socratic-partner prompt embedded in assignment. AI disclosure required. Submitted via Riipen.

## AI in This Week

**Permitted and encouraged:**

The production-oriented AI guidance introduced in Week 8 (module 8.05) continues to apply. Using AI for structural review of the assembled draft is especially appropriate this week — asking AI to identify where sections don't connect, where the argument flow breaks, or where different authorial voices create inconsistency mirrors the internal review gate activity (9.07) and extends students' ability to self-edit at scale. Using AI to stress-test recommendations for actionability is a powerful application of module 9.03: prompting AI with "read these recommendations and tell me which ones are too vague for a client to act on" is exactly how a professional might pressure-test their own work. Using AI to help organize the Check-In #2 debrief notes (9.10) into a prioritized action plan is consistent with the feedback processing guidance from Week 8 — converting raw notes into structured next steps. Using AI to check for consistency across sections during the production block (9.12) is encouraged, particularly for teams whose members have been drafting independently.

**Off-limits this week:**

AI cannot generate the team processing agreement (9.06). The agreement must reflect the team's genuine discussion of their peer evaluation and employer feedback — what patterns they identified, what they agreed to change, and what specific behaviors they committed to. An AI-generated agreement defeats both the learning purpose (the conversation is the learning) and the accountability purpose (the agreement must reflect real commitments). AI cannot generate the substance of the Draft Deliverable — the research, analysis, findings, and recommendations must represent the team's genuine intellectual work. The test from the course AI policy applies: could you explain and defend every claim in your submission without referring back to what an AI told you? AI-generated content that students cannot verify or defend remains off-limits, and Check-In #2 is a live accountability mechanism where the employer will ask questions about the work. Reflection #3 (9.15) must reflect the student's own experience — AI cannot supply the experience, the examination, or the articulated learning; AI-as-Socratic-partner use is encouraged for deepening examination.

**Disclosure required:** Yes — on both the Draft Deliverable and Reflection #3 submissions. If AI was not used, a one-sentence statement to that effect is sufficient.

**Prompt strategy reinforced this week — AI for Actionability Testing:**

No new AI concept is introduced this week — the production-oriented AI skills from Week 8 (module 8.05) find their most direct application here. Module 9.03 includes a specific prompt pattern for stress-testing draft recommendations: *"Read these recommendations and tell me which ones are too vague for a client to act on. For each one, explain what's missing — is it specificity, feasibility, or prioritization?"* This extends the Week 8 structural review concept into evaluative territory: students are not just asking AI to check their writing, but to pressure-test whether their thinking meets a professional standard.

## Open Questions / Flags

**1. Peer evaluation and employer survey release timing.** The design assumes anonymized peer eval results (qualitative and aggregated BARS) and employer mid-project survey results are available to students before the Week 9 Team Processing Session. The mechanism for processing, anonymizing, and delivering this data needs to be defined. If the course runs at scale, this may require tooling rather than manual instructor processing. If results are delayed, the Team Processing Session loses its anchor data and becomes a less structured conversation. Affects: 9.06 activity design, instructor workload, platform requirements.

**2. Team processing agreement format.** The agreement is described as "a brief written agreement documenting specific behavioral changes." The format is intentionally unspecified — it could be a paragraph, a bulleted list, or a simple table of "What we agreed to change / Who owns it / How we'll know." Providing a lightweight template might reduce variance without over-prescribing. Alternatively, leaving it open tests whether teams can produce a useful working document without scaffolding. Affects: student instructions, submission review.

**3. Internal review gate duration in future weeks.** This week allocates 45 minutes for the first internal review gate because it's the first time teams read their assembled work end-to-end. Future instances (before Week 11 Near-Final Package and Week 13 Final Deliverables) may need less time if teams have established the habit, or more time if the deliverable has grown in complexity. The appropriate duration should be calibrated after the pilot. Affects: Weeks 11 and 13 time budgets.

**4. Draft Deliverable completion criteria.** "Substantive progress" and "genuine work product" are intentionally subjective completion criteria for a checkpoint. The question is whether to provide more specific guidance (e.g., "all major sections present in draft form, even if some are incomplete") or leave the standard open to accommodate variation across project types. More specific criteria reduce ambiguity for students and instructors but risk being wrong for certain project formats. Affects: student instructions, facilitator guidance.

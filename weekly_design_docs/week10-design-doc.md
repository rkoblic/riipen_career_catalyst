## Weekly Activity Breakdown

Week 10 is the final week of the Development phase, and it is intentionally lighter than Week 9 (~8.5 hrs vs. ~9 hrs). After the most intensive week in the course — Progress Review #2, Team Processing Session, Draft Deliverable submission, and Reflection #3 — students need room to regroup and focus on integration work. The lighter load is recovery by design, not a scheduling gap. The module content is heavier than recent weeks (65 min across four segments) because this week serves a distinct conceptual function: helping students step back from the daily production grind and connect what they're doing to what they're learning. This is the "making sense of your experience" week, and the conceptual modules (10.02, 10.03) prepare students for Reflection #4 in Week 12 and Reflection #5 in Week 15 — both of which require students to articulate transferable learning, not just describe events. The Change Log introduction (10.04) and AI as Quality Reviewer module (10.05) are practical tools that students will use through the remainder of the course. No employer touchpoint this week — the last was Progress Review #2 in Week 9 and the next is the Near-Final Review in Week 11.

## AI in This Week

**Permitted and encouraged:**

Module 10.05 introduces the final AI skill-building application: using AI as a quality reviewer. This is the capstone of the AI skill progression that has developed across the course — from policy orientation (Week 1) to research prompting (Weeks 2–4) to Socratic partner for reflection (Week 3+) to production partner (Week 8+) to quality reviewer (Week 10+). The quality reviewer application asks students to use AI differently than in production: instead of generating or editing content, students prompt AI to read their draft as an unfamiliar reader and identify where the document is confusing, inconsistent, or logically weak. This mirrors the professional practice of having a colleague review your work before it goes to a client.

Production-oriented AI use from Week 8 continues for the integration work (10.07). Using AI to help identify inconsistencies across sections written by different teammates, check that terminology is used consistently, and flag transitions that don't work is particularly appropriate during integration. Using AI to help set up and organize the Change Log (10.04) is permitted — structuring the log, categorizing feedback by source and type, and drafting initial entries are reasonable applications.

**Off-limits this week:**

No specific prohibitions beyond the standing course policy. The standing caution applies with particular force as integration work intensifies: AI-generated content that students cannot explain or defend remains off-limits, and the Near-Final Review in Week 11 is the next accountability check.

**Disclosure required:** AI disclosure will be required on the Near-Final Package checkpoint submission in Week 11. Teams should be tracking their AI use as part of the emerging Change Log habit.

**Prompt strategy introduced — AI as Quality Reviewer:** Module 10.05 introduces the quality reviewer strategy. Students prompt AI with their draft and ask it to read as an unfamiliar reader:
- "Read this section as someone who has never heard of this project. Where do you get lost or confused?"
- "Does this document read like one coherent piece, or does it feel like sections written by different people? Where are the seams?"
- "What are the three weakest points in this argument? Why?"

This strategy extends through Weeks 11–15 and is referenced in the self-review protocol (11.03) and the final submission checklist (13.02).

## Design Decisions

**1. Conceptual modules serve the reflection arc, not just this week.** Modules 10.02 and 10.03 are not about Week 10 activities — they're about preparing students for Reflections #4 and #5. The "What Are You Actually Learning?" and "From Experience to Concept" modules teach the analytical move that both prompts require: connecting specific project experiences to transferable professional principles. Placing them here — two weeks before Reflection #4 and five weeks before Reflection #5 — gives students time to absorb the concepts and begin noticing their own learning before they're asked to write about it.

**2. Change Log introduced with three weeks of runway.** The Change Log could have been introduced in Week 7 alongside the first employer feedback or in Week 11 when it becomes immediately relevant to the Near-Final Review. Week 10 is the deliberate middle ground: students have enough feedback history to populate initial entries (from Progress Reviews #1 and #2), but there are still three production weeks before the Final Deliverables submission where the Change Log is assessed. This gives teams time to develop the habit without the pressure of an imminent submission.

**3. The 300-minute production block is the largest in the course.** Moving Reflection #3 to Week 9 freed substantial time in Week 10 for integration work. The 300-minute block reflects a specific design judgment: this is the last week where teams have uninterrupted production time before Refinement begins. Week 11 introduces the Near-Final Review (which consumes synchronous time and shifts focus to quality), and Weeks 12–13 are revision and submission weeks. Teams need to emerge from Week 10 with a substantially complete package. The time allocation makes this expectation explicit.

**4. AI skill progression reaches its final stage.** Module 10.05 is the last AI skill-building module in the course. After this week, students apply the full toolkit — research prompting, Socratic partner, production partner, and quality reviewer — independently. The quality reviewer application is positioned here because it maps to what students need at this stage: they have draft content that needs critical assessment, not generation. The progression from "AI helps me make things" to "AI helps me evaluate things" is a deliberate developmental arc.

**5. Recovery by design.** The week is intentionally lighter than Week 9 (505 min vs. ~540 min) and has no graded deliverable. After the most intensive week in the course — which included Progress Review #2, the Team Processing Session, Draft Deliverable submission, and Reflection #3 — students need a week where they can focus on the work itself rather than on checkpoints and submissions. The module content is reflective and practical rather than high-pressure, and the production block is large but self-directed. This is the breathing room before the Refinement phase tightens.

**6. Forward prep creates a two-week lead on Reflection #4.** Activity 10.09 previews the Reflection #4 prompt (due Week 12) two weeks early. The prompt asks students to examine the gap between the plan they made in Week 4 and the work they've actually produced — a gap that is most visible while students are still producing. Reading the prompt now, while the gap is live, helps students begin the noticing that makes a strong reflection possible.

**7. No employer touchpoint.** Week 10 has no employer meeting. The last was Progress Review #2 in Week 9; the next is the Near-Final Review in Week 11. This creates an uninterrupted work week, which is what teams need to get the package to a state that's ready for the Refinement phase.

## Weekly Activity Breakdown

Week 8 is built around a dual challenge: processing the employer feedback students just received in Check-In #1, and completing their first peer evaluation — a graded individual deliverable that requires them to assess each teammate's contribution with specific behavioral evidence. There is no employer touchpoint this week, which frees synchronous time for the largest production block in the course so far. The module content serves both challenges directly: feedback processing and iteration skills apply immediately to the revision work, while the peer evaluation module — including a calibration exercise using the Maple & Main scenario — prepares students for the evidence-based evaluation they must complete. The AI module shifts from the research-oriented AI guidance of Weeks 2–4 to production-oriented guidance appropriate for the Development phase. This week also introduces the Status Update to Employer as a standing prompt in the async communication activity — a brief between-reviews update that maintains employer engagement without requiring a synchronous meeting.

## Deliverable Connection

**Peer Evaluation #1 → Teamwork (all subskills) + Communication (Active Listening)**

Graded; BARS-based + qualitative. Individual submission. Peer Evaluation #1 is the mid-project formative evaluation, contributing 30% of the overall peer evaluation score (which is 50% of the Professional Engagement component, worth 20% of the final course grade). The evaluation is formative in purpose — its primary function is diagnostic and corrective, surfacing team dynamics while there is still time to change behavior. But it also generates a grade signal that incentivizes honest, evidence-based assessment.

The evaluation instrument has four parts completed for each teammate: (A) qualitative feedback using the Continue/Start/Adjust framework, with a two-sentence minimum per prompt; (B) BARS ratings on four dimensions (Reliability, Contribution, Constructive Engagement, Accountability) using behavioral anchors, with a 1–2 sentence specific evidence example for each rating; (C) self-evaluation on the same four BARS dimensions; and (D) two team pulse questions visible only to the instructor. The BARS dimensions map directly to the four Teamwork subskills in the NACE competency framework.

Module 8.04 prepares students for this deliverable through the Continue/Start/Adjust framework, the BARS anchors, examples of weak vs. strong evidence, and a calibration exercise using the Maple & Main scenario (rating a fictional teammate "Jordan" and norming expectations against the behavioral descriptors). Activity 8.06 provides additional time to review the assignment description and evidence expectations before students draft and submit.

Results are processed between Weeks 8 and 9: anonymized qualitative feedback and aggregated BARS averages are shared back to students before the Week 9 Team Processing Session, where teams discuss the feedback and agree on specific behavioral changes. Instructor review flags include any rating of 1 from multiple teammates, high standard deviation within a team, self/peer calibration gaps ≥ 1.5, and low team pulse confidence scores.

AI-assisted drafting of qualitative feedback is permitted with disclosure — students may use AI to help articulate an observation more clearly or check specificity. AI cannot generate the evaluation itself; the learning happens in the act of evaluating, and the data must reflect real team dynamics.

## AI in This Week

**Permitted and encouraged:**

Module 8.05 explicitly shifts AI guidance from the research context of Weeks 2–4 to the production context of the Development phase. Using AI for structural review of draft deliverable sections is appropriate — asking AI to identify where arguments are weak, where transitions are unclear, or where evidence does not support claims mirrors professional editing practice. Using AI for self-editing during revision work is encouraged: asking AI to flag repetition, check for consistency across sections written by different teammates, or suggest where data visualization might be more effective than narrative. Using AI to help organize the feedback response plan (8.08) is permitted — converting raw debrief notes into a structured priority list or identifying which pieces of feedback affect multiple deliverable sections are reasonable applications. Using AI to help articulate peer evaluation feedback more clearly or specifically is permitted with disclosure, provided the observations themselves come from the student's genuine assessment of their teammate's behavior.

**Off-limits this week:**

AI cannot generate the Peer Evaluation. The qualitative feedback (Continue/Start/Adjust) must reflect the evaluator's actual observations of their teammate's behavior, and the BARS ratings must reflect the evaluator's genuine assessment. AI-generated peer evaluations defeat both the pedagogical purpose (the learning happens in the act of evaluating) and the administrative purpose (the data must reflect real team dynamics, not plausible fabrications). The team pulse questions (Part D) are especially sensitive — these are instructor-only triage signals, and AI-generated responses would undermine the early warning system they are designed to provide. AI-generated content in draft deliverables that students cannot verify or defend remains off-limits — the Week 9 Check-In #2 is approaching.

**Disclosure required:** Yes — on the Peer Evaluation #1 submission. If AI was used to help draft or refine qualitative responses, this must be disclosed. If AI was not used, a one-sentence statement to that effect is sufficient.

## Open Questions / Flags

**1. Peer Evaluation platform.** The evaluation instrument described here (four-part structure with BARS, qualitative prompts, self-evaluation, and team pulse) is more complex than a standard LMS peer review tool can typically support. The platform for administering Peer Evaluation #1 needs to be confirmed — options include building a custom form in Riipen, using a specialized tool like CATME or FeedbackFruits, or creating a structured template that students complete and submit as a document. The choice affects student experience and instructor workflow. Affects: assignment configuration, student instructions, instructor review process.

**2. Anonymized feedback release timing.** The design assumes anonymized qualitative feedback and aggregated BARS averages are shared back to students between Week 8 submission and the Week 9 Team Processing Session. The mechanism for this release needs to be defined — who processes and anonymizes the data, how it is delivered to students, and what the turnaround time is. If the course is facilitated at scale, this may require tooling. Affects: Week 9 activity sequencing, instructor workload.

**3. Employer mid-project survey.** The professional engagement mechanics specify that employers complete a brief mid-project survey at Week 8, assessing team responsiveness, communication quality, and professionalism. This survey is not a student-facing activity and is not included in the activity list, but its timing should be coordinated — ideally administered shortly after Check-In #1 while the employer's impressions are fresh. Affects: employer communication, Professional Engagement scoring.

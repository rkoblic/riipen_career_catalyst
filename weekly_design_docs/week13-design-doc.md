## Weekly Activity Breakdown

Week 13 is a submission week. The module content is deliberately minimal (20 minutes total) because students don't need instruction at this point — they need time to finish. The week's anchor is the Final Deliverables submission, worth 30% of the course grade and representing the culmination of the project work that began with the Context Analysis in Week 4. The only employer touchpoint is the Final Check-In, held as a 60-minute block that teams should cancel if they don't have substantive remaining questions. Whether or not the meeting is held, teams must communicate with the employer about Week 15 presentation logistics — format, timing, and attendees. The forward prep activity (13.09) previews both Reflection #5 and Peer Evaluation #2, giving students time to begin noticing what they'll write about and evaluate before the pressure of Week 15.

## Deliverable Connection

**Final Deliverables → Critical Thinking (Problem Framing, Research & Analysis, Evidence-Based Reasoning) + Communication (Written Clarity) + Professionalism (Quality & Attention to Detail, Feedback Reception)**

Graded; rubric-based. Team submission. Worth 30% of the course grade — the single highest-weighted submission. The Final Deliverables package includes all deliverable components specified in the project brief, a Change Log documenting the feedback received across employer touchpoints and draft reviews along with the team's response to each item, and an AI disclosure statement.

The rubric assesses five dimensions: Deliverable Quality (30%) evaluates whether the work product meets the employer's needs and demonstrates understanding of the project scope. Analytical Rigor (25%) evaluates the quality of research, evidence, and reasoning that supports the deliverable's claims and recommendations. Actionability (20%) evaluates whether the recommendations are specific, feasible, and useful to the employer. Communication (15%) evaluates the clarity, organization, and professionalism of the written document. Responsiveness to Feedback (10%) evaluates the team's documented engagement with feedback across the project arc, assessed through the Change Log.

The Change Log is required. Without it, the Responsiveness to Feedback dimension scores as "Not Submitted" (0%). The Change Log is assessed based on its documentation of the feedback cycle, not the evaluator's memory of check-ins.

AI-assisted polish, editing, and quality review is permitted and encouraged at this stage — the substance of the deliverable package should reflect the team's own research, analysis, and recommendations built over the preceding weeks. AI disclosure required on submission.

## AI in This Week

**Permitted and encouraged:**

Production-oriented AI use from Week 8 onward continues through the final submission. At this stage, AI is most useful for the tasks that the Final Deliverable Checklist (13.02) describes: verifying formatting consistency across sections, checking that the document reads as a coherent whole rather than separately authored pieces, tightening prose, and catching errors that hide in plain sight after weeks of looking at the same material. Using AI to review the Change Log for completeness — checking that all substantive feedback items from employer touchpoints are documented with clear responses — is appropriate and encouraged.

If the Final Check-In is held (13.05), using AI to quickly assess how last-minute feedback maps to the existing deliverable — where it aligns with work already done, where it suggests a targeted change, and where it represents scope that can't be addressed before submission — continues the triage framework from Week 12. This is a professional judgment aid, not a substitute for the judgment itself.

**Off-limits this week:**

No specific prohibitions beyond the standing course policy. The standing caution remains: AI-polished content that the team cannot explain or defend will surface in the Week 15 Final Presentation. Teams submitting AI-generated analysis or recommendations they haven't verified are creating a problem for themselves two weeks from now.

**Disclosure required:** AI disclosure required on the Final Deliverables submission.

**Prompt strategy reinforced:** The quality review application from Weeks 11–12 continues. Students use AI to evaluate their own work against the rubric dimensions — a final-pass review tool, not a content generator. The specific application is narrower than in previous weeks: students are checking, not creating.

## Design Decisions

**1. Module content is deliberately minimal.** Twenty minutes of module content is the lightest in the course. This reflects the design principle that students at this stage need time, not instruction. The checklist (13.02) is practical rather than conceptual — a tool students use, not a lesson students absorb. The mindset module (13.03) is included because the impulse to overhaul at the last minute is a real and predictable failure mode, and naming it explicitly is more useful than assuming students will resist it on their own.

**2. The Final Check-In is optional by design.** Unlike every other employer touchpoint in the course, the Final Check-In is structured as a hold that teams can and should cancel if they don't have substantive remaining questions. This is a deliberate design choice: by Week 13, teams that have engaged in the feedback cycle through Progress Reviews #1–3 and the Near-Final Review should have the information they need. Making the meeting optional tests whether teams can distinguish between needing input and needing reassurance — a professional judgment skill. The 60-minute hold ensures the time is available if needed without creating a default expectation that the meeting must happen.

**3. Async presentation logistics communication regardless of meeting status.** Whether or not the Final Check-In is held, teams must communicate with the employer about Week 15 presentation logistics. This ensures that the presentation planning in Week 14 has the information it needs (format, timing, attendees) and that the employer is not left waiting for coordination that doesn't come. Embedding this in both the team sync (13.04) and the async block (13.10) creates redundancy — the communication happens one way or another.

**4. Production block sized for assembly, not generation.** The 210-minute production block is substantial but scoped to final assembly and polish. Teams at this stage should be integrating completed components, running through the checklist, and making targeted fixes — not writing new sections or producing new analysis. The design doc names this explicitly: teams that are still producing new content at this stage have a problem that more time won't solve. The 210 minutes accounts for the reality that even "just polish" takes longer than expected when the package has been through 12 weeks of development.

**5. Internal review gate maintained for the fourth time.** The standing internal review gate pattern (introduced 9.07, repeated 10.07, 11.07) appears here in its final instance. By now the practice should be automatic — teams know what the gate is and how to use it. The note in the activity description reflects this: the gate at Week 13 should confirm what the team already knows rather than surfacing surprises. If it does surface surprises, that's a signal the earlier gates weren't used effectively.

**6. Forward prep creates a bridge to Weeks 14–15.** Previewing both the Reflection #5 prompt and Peer Evaluation #2 instructions in Week 13 — before the submission pressure of Week 15 — gives students lead time to begin the noticing that both tasks require. Reflection #5 asks students to look back over the full course arc, which is easier to do if they've been paying attention to what they're learning for the past two weeks rather than trying to reconstruct it under deadline pressure. Peer Evaluation #2 requires behavioral evidence about teammates, which students accumulate more effectively when they know what they'll be asked about. This is consistent with the course's just-in-time principle: giving students information at the point when it becomes useful.

**7. No Open Questions section.** Week 13 is a convergence week — the design decisions are settled, the submission structure is clear, and the remaining work is execution. Unlike earlier design docs where open questions flagged unresolved design or logistical decisions, there are no outstanding issues that would affect how students or instructors approach this week's activities.

## Weekly Activity Breakdown

Week 6 marks the transition from planning to production. Students have completed the Direction Check, revised their project plan, and divided deliverable sections — now they have to actually write. The module content is sequenced as just-in-time instruction: three segments address the conditions students are about to encounter in production (working without certainty, moving from notes to draft, handling disagreement). DEAL was introduced in Week 1 and students have one reflection attempt behind them from Week 3, so Reflection #2 here is a second application of a familiar framework rather than a first encounter — it is sequenced at the end of the week so students draw from the fullest possible pool of experience before sitting down to write. The production block is deliberately front-loaded and sized generously because Week 7 will require significant time on Work-in-Progress preparation, leaving less time for independent drafting; students should take full advantage of the production space this week while it is available.

## Deliverable Connection

**Reflection #2 → Career & Self-Development (Reflection, Self-Awareness)**

Graded; rubric-based. Individual submission. Contributes ~5% of the final grade as part of the Reflection Portfolio (25% total, five reflections). Students draw from their Discovery phase experience — the research process, the Direction Check, the transition into production, team dynamics under pressure, or any moment of ambiguity or learning that emerged in Weeks 4–6 — and examine it using the DEAL model across three stages: Describe (what happened, factually and specifically), Examine (what assumptions were operating, what patterns emerge, what the experience reveals), and Articulate Learning (what transferable insight can be extracted that connects to professional practice beyond this project).

This is the second DEAL reflection in the course; the first was in Week 3 following the kickoff. The framework is familiar — the expectation here is not that students demonstrate they can use DEAL, but that they use it with more depth and honesty than they did in Week 3. The rubric dimensions are Accurate Description, Critical Examination, Honest Self-Assessment, and Articulation of Learning. Growth across the five reflections in the portfolio is itself evidence of learning.

The reflection is sequenced at the end of Week 6 so students have the fullest possible view of the Discovery phase before sitting down to write. The Direction Check and the first week of production are both live in memory, giving students rich, specific material to draw from rather than reaching back to earlier, more distant experiences.

AI disclosure is required on submission via the Riipen platform. The core boundary is that AI cannot supply the experience, the examination, or the articulated learning — a reflection that could apply to any student in any project has not met the standard regardless of what tools were used to produce it.

## AI in This Week

**Permitted and encouraged:** Using AI as a Socratic thinking partner during the reflection writing process is encouraged — the prompt strategy for this was introduced in Week 1 alongside the DEAL framework and applied in Week 3. Students may use AI to deepen their examination of an experience by asking it to identify assumptions they haven't named, push back on surface-level observations, or ask follow-up questions that help surface genuine learning. AI can also support the notes-to-outline-to-draft process introduced in Module 6.03: using AI to help cluster and organize raw research notes, stress-test an outline structure before committing to drafting, or identify gaps in a section plan before prose production begins. Using AI to fill knowledge gaps that surface during drafting and to check draft sections for logical coherence and clarity are also permitted.

**Off-limits this week:** The reflection (6.10) must reflect the student's own experience and genuine learning from the Discovery phase. AI cannot supply the experience being described, generate the examination, or produce the articulated learning statement. A reflection that reads as though it could apply to any student in any project — lacking the specific details of what actually happened and what the student actually felt or thought — has not met the standard regardless of whether AI was used. On the production side, AI-generated content that students cannot verify or defend should not be in deliverable drafts: the Work-in-Progress presentation in Week 7 is a live accountability mechanism.

**Disclosure required:** Yes — on Reflection #2 submission. If AI was not used, a one-sentence statement to that effect is sufficient.

**Prompt strategy introduced — From Research to Draft:** Module 6.03 introduces AI as a tool for moving from notes to outline to prose. Students use AI to organize and cluster raw notes rather than generate content, keeping the intellectual work their own while using AI to reduce the structural overhead of synthesis.

- *"Here are my raw research notes on [topic]: [notes]. Help me identify the three to five main themes or clusters across these notes. Don't summarize — just group them and label each cluster."*
- *"Here is my outline for my assigned section: [outline]. What gaps do you see? What questions would a reader have after reading this section that the outline doesn't seem to answer?"*
- *"I'm about to start drafting from this outline. Is there anything in the structure that seems out of order or that would be confusing to a reader?"*

# Riipen Experience: Course Structure

*The fixed 15-week framework that makes diverse projects comparable*

*Updated March 2026*

---

## Overview

The 15-week course is organized into five phases that mirror the natural arc of professional project work: orientation and launch, research and planning, core production, refinement, and synthesis. This structure follows the logic of how projects actually unfold — not an abstract pedagogical sequence, but the rhythm that experienced professionals recognize.

The phase structure is fixed across all projects and institutions. This consistency enables diverse projects to be comparable — the learning structure remains constant even as project content varies. Faculty can supervise multiple projects simultaneously because the pedagogical scaffolding doesn't change.

**Key insight:** The course structure carries the learning — not instructor performance. Content is delivered through self-paced modules; the fixed structure ensures every student experiences the same pedagogical arc regardless of their specific project.

---

## How the Structure Supports Learning

Kolb's experiential learning theory (1984) describes learning as a cycle: concrete experience → reflective observation → abstract conceptualization → active experimentation. The insight isn't that these stages should happen sequentially, but that all four modes must be present and that learners often skip reflection and conceptualization when left to their own devices.

The course structure addresses this by creating repeated opportunities to cycle through all four modes:

1. **Concrete experience:** The employer project itself. Students encounter real ambiguity, real stakeholder needs, and real constraints throughout the course.
2. **Reflective observation:** Five structured reflections using the DEAL model explicitly prompt students to step back and examine their experience. Without this deliberate intervention, many students would skip reflection entirely.
3. **Abstract conceptualization:** Weekly modules provide frameworks, concepts, and principles that help students make sense of their experience.
4. **Active experimentation:** Feedback cycles (employer check-ins, draft submissions, peer feedback) create opportunities to apply insights. Students don't just think about what they've learned — they test it in the next iteration.

Each feedback loop (i.e., draft → feedback → revision) is a mini-cycle through all four modes. Students experience this cycle multiple times at different scales: within a single week, across a phase, and across the full course.

## How the Structure Implements Design Principles

Each element of the course structure connects to specific principles from the Design Principles document. The following table shows how the structure operationalizes the pedagogical logic:

| Design Principle | How the Structure Implements It |
|---|---|
| **Structured Reflection** | Five reflections are placed at phase transitions and key junctures (Weeks 3, 6, 9, 12, 15) when students have enough experience to reflect meaningfully but before they've moved on mentally. |
| **Scaffolded Progression** | No gap longer than two weeks between deliverables. Each phase has 2–4 checkpoints or graded submissions, preventing procrastination and enabling early feedback. |
| **Multiple Evidence Sources** | Evidence distributed across phases: Context Analysis & Project Plan (Week 4), Final Deliverables (Week 13), five Reflections, two Peer Evaluations, two Employer Evaluations, Final Presentation (Week 15). |
| **Layered Accountability** | Team Charter (Week 2) establishes norms early. Mid-project peer evaluation (Week 8) creates course-correction opportunity. Final peer evaluation (Week 15) provides summative assessment. |
| **Consistent Framework** | Five-phase structure is constant across all projects. Same competency framework, same reflection model, same evidence architecture — regardless of project content. |
| **Intentional Engagement** | Student intake establishes personal learning goals. Employer touchpoints at seven points create external accountability. Professionalism is explicitly assessed. |

## Phase Overview

| Phase | Weeks | Focus | Employer Touchpoint |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Foundation** | 1–3 | Orientation, team formation, project launch, employer kickoff | Kickoff Meeting (Week 3) |
| **Discovery** | 4–6 | Context research, strategic planning, early deliverable work | Direction Check (Week 5) |
| **Development** | 7–10 | Core project work, client feedback cycles, iteration | Progress Review #1 (Week 7); Progress Review #2 (Week 9) |

## Phase 1: Foundation (Weeks 1–3)

**Purpose:** Orient students to experiential learning, form functioning teams, and launch the project with a shared understanding of expectations.

This phase establishes the conditions for effective learning. Students learn what experiential learning means and how this course differs from traditional coursework. Teams form and establish working norms before project pressure hits. The employer kickoff creates shared understanding and real-world accountability from the start.

**Primary competencies developed:** Teamwork (team formation, establishing norms), Professionalism (first employer impression, dependability)

| Week | Module Topic | Project Work | Deliverable | Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | What Is Experiential Learning? | Review assigned project and complete intake. Team assignment and first meeting. Review Team Charter template. | Student Intake Questionnaire | *Checkpoint* |
| 2 | Preparing for Your Client | Negotiate and draft Team Charter. Research organization and context. Develop kickoff questions. Prepare team introduction. | Team Charter + Employer Outreach Email | *Checkpoint* |
| 3 | Understanding Your Client | **EMPLOYER KICKOFF.** Meet with employer contact. Clarify project scope, deliverables, and success criteria. Begin initial research. Draft and submit Kickoff Summary. Write Reflection #1. | Kickoff Summary + **Reflection #1** | Checkpoint + **GRADED** |

**Reflection #1 focus:** Students examine a specific moment from the Foundation phase — most commonly the kickoff meeting — exploring what it revealed about how they enter new professional situations. This reflection targets Career & Self-Development (self-awareness, reflection).

**Phase outcome:** Teams are formed with clear working agreements, students understand what's expected of them, and everyone has a shared understanding of the project from the employer kickoff.

---

## Phase 2: Discovery (Weeks 4–6)

**Purpose:** Research the project context, develop a strategic approach, and begin early deliverable work while there's still time to course-correct.

This phase is where students do the thinking that will make their project work valuable. The Context Analysis & Project Plan forces structured research before diving into production. The Direction Check with the employer catches misalignment before students invest significant effort in the wrong direction. Reflection #2 at the end of the phase asks students to examine their approach to ambiguity and initiative.

**Primary competencies developed:** Critical Thinking (problem framing, research and analysis, evidence-based reasoning)

| Week | Module Topic | Project Work | Deliverable | Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | From Research to Strategy | Complete context research. Analyze stakeholder needs, competitive landscape, or relevant background. Develop initial strategic approach. | **Context Analysis & Project Plan** | **GRADED** |
| 5 | Direction Check Preparation | **DIRECTION CHECK.** Present research findings and proposed approach to employer. Finalize project plan based on feedback. | Direction Check Summary | *Checkpoint* |
| 6 | Working Through Ambiguity | Continue deliverable development. Team check-in on progress and challenges. Write Reflection #2. | **Reflection #2** | **GRADED** |

**Reflection #2 focus:** Students examine their approach to ambiguity, how they've taken initiative, and how they've navigated uncertainty across the Discovery phase. This reflection targets Career & Self-Development (self-awareness, reflection) and connects to the Professionalism competency (initiative, feedback reception).

**Phase outcome:** Students have completed substantive research (Context Analysis), have employer-validated direction (Direction Check), and have reflected on their approach — all before the intensive production phase begins.

---

## Phase 3: Development (Weeks 7–10)

**Purpose:** Execute the core project work through feedback cycles and iteration, with external accountability from employer check-ins and peer evaluation.

This is the most intensive phase — where the real work happens. The design deliberately builds in multiple feedback loops: Progress Reviews #1 and #2 with the employer (Weeks 7 and 9), peer feedback throughout, and draft submissions that receive formative feedback before final deliverables. The mid-project peer evaluation creates accountability and enables course-correction on team dynamics before it's too late.

**Primary competencies developed:** Communication (incorporating feedback, professional correspondence), Teamwork (peer evaluation, navigating team dynamics under pressure)

| Week | Module Topic | Project Work | Deliverable | Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 7 | Giving and Receiving Feedback | **PROGRESS REVIEW #1.** Present work-in-progress. Receive and document employer feedback. Conduct team peer feedback session. | Work-in-Progress Summary | *Checkpoint* |
| 8 | Making Feedback Useful | Apply feedback from Progress Review #1. Document how feedback was incorporated. Complete mid-project peer evaluation. | **Peer Evaluation #1** | **GRADED** |
| 9 | Teamwork Under Pressure | **PROGRESS REVIEW #2.** Present updated work. Core production continues. Address any team dynamics issues surfaced in peer evaluation. | Draft Deliverable | *Checkpoint* |
| 10 | Connecting Theory and Practice | Continue production. Begin integrating components into coherent deliverable package. Write Reflection #3. | **Reflection #3** | **GRADED** |

**Reflection #3 focus:** Students examine a specific moment involving feedback or collaboration — receiving difficult feedback, giving feedback to a peer, or navigating team tension under pressure. This reflection targets Career & Self-Development (reflection, growth orientation) and connects to Teamwork (constructive feedback).

**Phase outcome:** Students have received substantive feedback from the employer, addressed team dynamics through peer evaluation, and have draft deliverables approaching completion.

---

## Phase 4: Refinement (Weeks 11–13)

**Purpose:** Transform draft work into polished final deliverables through systematic review, client feedback, and attention to quality and craft.

This phase is about the difference between "done" and "done well." The Near-Final Package creates a forcing function for getting work to a reviewable state. Progress Reviews #3 and #4 (Weeks 11 and 13) provide continued employer input as deliverables are finalized. Week 13's final review ensures the employer sees completed work before the formal presentation.

**Primary competencies developed:** Professionalism (quality and attention to detail, meeting deadlines)

| Week | Module Topic | Project Work | Deliverable | Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 11 | Quality and Craft | **PROGRESS REVIEW #3.** Assemble near-final versions of all deliverables. Present to employer for feedback. Identify remaining gaps. | Near-Final Package | *Checkpoint* |
| 12 | From Feedback to Final | Apply feedback from Progress Review #3. Finalize all deliverables. Prepare presentation outline. Write Reflection #4. | Presentation Outline + **Reflection #4** | Checkpoint + **GRADED** |
| 13 | Finalizing Deliverables | **PROGRESS REVIEW #4 (Final Review).** Present completed deliverables. Receive final employer feedback. Submit final package. | **Final Deliverables** | **GRADED** |

**Reflection #4 focus:** Students examine the gap between what was planned and what was produced — what changed in their understanding of the problem, the deliverables, or their own capabilities. This reflection targets Career & Self-Development (reflection, growth orientation) and connects to Critical Thinking (evidence-based reasoning).

**Phase outcome:** All project deliverables are complete and submitted. Students have had four rounds of substantive employer feedback throughout Development and Refinement, and are prepared for their formal presentation.

---

## Phase 5: Integration (Weeks 14–15)

**Purpose:** Synthesize the experience through reflection and final presentation, extracting transferable learning that extends beyond this specific project.

This phase matters because experience alone doesn't produce lasting learning — synthesis and articulation do. The Week 15 presentation requires students to communicate their work coherently to an external audience, and Reflection #5 asks students to step back over the full arc of the course and articulate what they've learned about how they work as a professional. The final peer evaluation captures summative assessment of collaboration.

**Primary competencies developed:** Communication (oral presentation), Career & Self-Development (reflection, articulating professional growth)

| Week | Module Topic | Project Work | Deliverable | Type |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 14 | Transfer and What's Next | Finalize presentation. Compile ePortfolio materials. | Presentation Prep | *Checkpoint* |
| 15 | Presenting Your Work | **FINAL PRESENTATION.** Present completed work to employer and supervisor. Respond to questions. Receive final employer feedback. Complete final peer evaluation. Write Reflection #5. | **Final Presentation + Peer Eval #2 + Reflection #5** | **GRADED** |

**Reflection #5 focus:** Students step back over the full course arc and identify the single most important thing they learned — not about the project's subject matter, but about how they work, how they learn, or how they engage professionally. This reflection targets Career & Self-Development (career articulation, growth orientation) and synthesizes learning across all competencies.

**Phase outcome:** Students have communicated their work professionally, reflected on what they've learned and how they'll apply it, and provided final assessment of their teammates' contributions.

## Reflection Design

Five structured reflections are placed at phase transitions and key junctures throughout the course. Each reflection uses the DEAL framework (Describe, Examine, Articulate Learning) and is timed to capture learning at a meaningful juncture — when students have enough experience to reflect substantively but before they've mentally moved on. All five use fixed prompts consistent across all students.

| Reflection | Timing | Focus | Competency Connection |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Reflection #1** | **Week 3 (end of Foundation)** | Entering a new professional situation; what the kickoff revealed about how the student approaches new environments | Career & Self-Development (self-awareness, reflection) |
| **Reflection #2** | **Week 6 (end of Discovery)** | Approach to ambiguity, initiative, navigating uncertainty | Career & Self-Development (self-awareness, reflection); Professionalism (initiative) |
| **Reflection #3** | **Week 9 (mid-Development)** | Receiving and giving feedback; navigating team tension under pressure | Career & Self-Development (reflection, growth orientation); Teamwork (constructive feedback) |
| **Reflection #4** | **Week 12 (end of Refinement)** | The gap between plan and reality; what doing the work revealed about prior assumptions | Career & Self-Development (reflection, growth orientation); Critical Thinking (evidence-based reasoning) |
| **Reflection #5** | **Week 15 (end of Integration)** | Transferable principles, professional growth narrative, applying learning beyond this course | Career & Self-Development (career articulation, growth orientation); synthesis across all competencies |

**Why these timing points:** Reflection #1 follows the kickoff — the first significant professional interaction of the course — giving students a concrete, high-stakes experience to examine. Reflection #2 closes the Discovery phase after students have navigated ambiguity, produced their first graded deliverable, and received employer feedback. Reflection #3 follows the Development phase's most intensive feedback period. Reflection #4 closes Refinement, when the gap between plan and execution is most visible. Reflection #5 closes the full course arc, when students can synthesize across all five phases.

## Employer Touchpoint Integration

Seven employer touchpoints are distributed across the course on a consistent biweekly schedule, aligned with critical decision points in the project arc. This structure ensures students receive input before committing to directions rather than after investments have been made. The predictable "every other week" rhythm makes employer participation easy to schedule and commit to.

| Touchpoint | Timing | Duration | Purpose |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Kickoff Meeting** | Week 3 | 60 min | Establish relationship, clarify scope and success criteria, answer student questions |
| **Direction Check** | Week 5 | 30 min | Review proposed approach before students commit; catch misalignment early |
| **Progress Review #1** | Week 7 | 30 min | Substantive feedback on early draft work; course correction while time remains |
| **Progress Review #2** | Week 9 | 30 min | Continued feedback on work-in-progress; address any emerging issues |
| **Progress Review #3** | Week 11 | 30 min | Near-final review; feedback on assembled deliverable package |
| **Progress Review #4 (Final Review)** | Week 13 | 30 min | Final review of completed deliverables before formal presentation |
| **Final Presentation** | Week 15 | 30 min | Formal presentation of completed work; closure; final feedback |

**Total employer commitment:** Approximately seven to eight hours over 15 weeks, including synchronous touchpoints (4–5 hours), email responsiveness (48-hour turnaround), written feedback on at least one draft deliverable, and brief feedback surveys at mid-point and end of project.

**Why biweekly:** A consistent "every other week starting Week 3" rhythm is easier for employers to remember and schedule than variable gaps. It also ensures students never go more than two weeks without external feedback during the active project phases.

Detailed touchpoint specifications, including agendas and preparation guidance, are provided in the Employer Touchpoints document.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|

| 8 | **Peer Evaluation #1** | Individual | **GRADED** | Professional (20%) | ~10% |
| 9 | Draft Deliverable | Team | *Checkpoint* | — | — |
| 10 | **Reflection #3** | Individual | **GRADED** | Reflection (25%) | ~5% |

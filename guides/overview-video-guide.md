# Overview Video Script Guide

How to write the weekly overview video script (`page1-overview-video.md`) for Riipen Career Catalyst.

Overview videos are short (60-90 seconds, ~120-200 words). They sit on the overview page after the opening paragraphs and before the logistics. Their job is to give learners a reason to care about the week's work before they see what's due.

---

## What overview videos are for

The overview page handles logistics: what's due, in what order. The video handles motivation: why this week's work matters in a professional context.

A good overview video gives the learner a specific, concrete reason to take the week seriously. Not "this is important" but *why* it's important, grounded in how professional work actually operates.

---

## What makes a strong overview video

**Lead with a specific professional insight.** The viewer should learn something in the first 10 seconds that they didn't already know about how professional work functions. Week 3's video works because it opens with "the employer is evaluating you — not formally, but they're forming an impression" — that's a non-obvious, slightly uncomfortable truth that earns the viewer's attention.

**Ground everything in professional practice.** Don't describe the course activity. Describe the professional reality that the activity mirrors. "In any real project, there are gates" (Week 4) is stronger than "This week you'll submit your Context Analysis."

**Make a specific claim, not a vague one.** "Misalignment is expensive — six weeks executing the wrong plan costs the project" is specific. "This is a really important deliverable" is vague. If you can't name a concrete consequence or a specific professional behavior, the video isn't earning its runtime.

**Be honest about stakes without being dramatic.** Learners are smart enough to know when they're being hyped. State the real consequence plainly: "Teams that show up cold may get managed more tightly, sometimes for the rest of the project" is honest and motivating. "This could make or break your entire project!" is not.

---

## Patterns to avoid

These are the most common failure modes in overview video scripts. Most are AI writing tells that make the video sound generic and skippable.

**"Up to this point, you've been X... now you're Y."** This is the single most common AI-ism in overview scripts. It sounds like a transition but says nothing. The viewer already knows what they've been doing. Don't recap the course arc — make a claim about the week.

**"This isn't X, it's Y" constructions.** Limit these. One per script at most, and only if the distinction is genuinely surprising. "This isn't a presentation, it's a conversation" can work if the learner would genuinely expect a presentation. "This isn't busywork, it's real" is defensive and implies the learner was already thinking it's busywork.

**Parallel triplets and list-padding.** "Communication, collaboration, and critical thinking" or "consulting, product development, nonprofits, or startups" — these read as AI filling space. If you need to name examples, pick the one or two most relevant ones. Don't list for coverage.

**Hyperbolic closers.** "That's real." "Take this one seriously." "Make it worth their time." These feel like pep talks. End on your strongest specific point, not on emphasis. If the content is compelling, you don't need to tell the viewer it's important.

**Reassurance and hedging.** "That might sound exciting, or it might sound a little intimidating" — this tries to cover every possible reaction and ends up saying nothing. Pick a lane. If the thing is hard, say it's hard. Don't hedge.

**Fluffy "both outcomes are good" framing.** "Some teams will hear X. Others will hear Y. Both are good outcomes." This is technically true but not useful. Instead, explain *why* a redirect is valuable: what specific problem does it prevent?

**Describing the week's activities.** The page text already does this. The video should not repeat the week's schedule, deliverables, or ordering. If the viewer could get the same information from reading the page, the video isn't adding anything.

## Structure

A strong overview video typically has two or three moves:

1. **Open with a professional insight or claim** (1-2 sentences). Something the learner doesn't already know about how this type of work operates in professional contexts.
2. **Develop the claim with specifics** (3-5 sentences). Concrete consequences, professional behaviors, or examples that make the claim tangible. This is the body of the video and where most of the value lives.
3. **Close on the strongest point** (1 sentence). Don't summarize. Don't add a pep talk. Just land on the most important thing you said.

Total: ~120-200 words. If you're over 200, you're probably padding.

---

## Voice rules

- **Second person** ("you," "your team"). No first person ("I," "me," "let me").
- **No em dashes.** Use commas, periods, or parentheses.
- **Written to be heard.** Short sentences. Conversational rhythm. Natural pauses between paragraphs.
- **American English** throughout.
- **No slang, idioms, or culturally specific expressions.**
- **No visual formatting.** No colons as labels, no bullet lists, no bold text. These don't translate to voiceover. Use paragraph breaks and transitional phrases for structure.
- **Read every script aloud before finalizing.** If a sentence feels unnatural to say, rewrite it.

---

## Quality check

Before finalizing an overview video script, ask:

- [ ] Does the opening sentence teach the viewer something specific about professional work?
- [ ] Could someone get this information by reading the overview page? (If yes, rewrite.)
- [ ] Is there a concrete consequence or professional behavior named? (Not just "this matters.")
- [ ] Does it avoid the "up to this point / now" arc-recap pattern?
- [ ] Is it under 200 words?
- [ ] Does the closing land on a specific point, not a pep talk?
- [ ] Read it aloud: does it sound like a person talking, or a chatbot summarizing?

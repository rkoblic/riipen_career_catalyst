# Employer Touchpoint Arc — Riipen Career Catalyst

Reference document mapping every employer meeting and touchpoint across the 15-week course. Used for content production planning, not student-facing.

**Content status:** Weeks 1-6 have produced content. Weeks 7-15 are design doc only.

---

## The arc at a glance

| Week | Touchpoint | Type | Distinct purpose |
|------|-----------|------|-----------------|
| 2 | Outreach email | Written | Introduce the team, request kickoff |
| 3 | Kickoff meeting | Listening meeting (60 min) | Understand the project from the employer's perspective |
| 3 | Follow-up email | Written | Confirm understanding, thank employer |
| 5 | Direction Check | Presentation (30 min) | "Are we headed in the right direction?" |
| 5 | Pre-meeting email | Written | Confirm logistics + agenda |
| 7 | Check-In #1 | Working session (30 min) | "Help us decide X, Y, Z." |
| 8+ | Status updates | Async written | Brief between-meeting updates (standing practice) |
| 9 | Check-In #2 | Working session (30 min) | "Does this meet the bar? Where are the weak spots?" |
| 11 | Near-Final Review | Document review (30 min) | "What's missing before we finalize?" |
| 13 | Final Check-In | Optional (30 min) | Only if substantive questions; cancel if not needed |
| 15 | Final Presentation | Formal presentation (60 min) | "Here's the finished work and what we learned." |

**Note on meeting duration:** The design docs specify 60-minute holds for most meetings. In practice, many of these will run 30 minutes, especially the working sessions (Weeks 7, 9) and document review (Week 11). The 60-minute hold gives buffer. Content should reference the actual expected duration rather than the calendar hold.

---

## The progression

The employer touchpoints follow a deliberate arc that mirrors how professional client relationships develop:

1. **Listening** (Week 3) — Students are the audience. The employer sets the context.
2. **Presenting strategy** (Week 5) — Students present their thinking for the first time. The employer reacts.
3. **Working sessions** (Weeks 7, 9) — Students drive the agenda with specific questions. The employer is a resource.
4. **Document review** (Week 11) — The work speaks for itself. The employer reviews near-final deliverables.
5. **Delivery** (Week 15) — Students present completed work. The employer receives deliverables.

This progression shifts students from passive (listening) to active (driving the conversation) to confident (presenting finished work). Each step requires more ownership and more professional judgment than the last.

## Week-by-week detail

### Week 2 — Outreach email
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Introduce the team, demonstrate awareness of the project, request the kickoff meeting |
| **Format** | Written message via Riipen platform |
| **What students prepare** | Professional email with team introductions, reference to project brief, meeting request |
| **Employer's role** | Receives introduction, confirms meeting |
| **Deliverable** | Employer Outreach Message (completion-based checkpoint) |
| **Content framing** | This is their first professional correspondence with a real stakeholder. Frame as a professional skill, not a course task. |

### Week 3 — Kickoff meeting
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Understand the project from the employer's perspective. Listen, ask prepared questions, capture what was said. |
| **Format** | Synchronous meeting (60 min hold) |
| **What students prepare** | Research on the organization, prepared questions, meeting agenda, note-taking setup |
| **Employer's role** | Presents organizational context, project scope, success criteria, timeline, key contacts; answers questions |
| **Deliverable** | Kickoff Summary (completion-based checkpoint) |
| **Content framing** | Students are the audience here. Their job is to understand, not to present. Preparation is about asking good questions, not having answers. |

### Week 3 — Follow-up email
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Confirm understanding, thank the employer, flag any open questions |
| **Format** | Written message via Riipen platform |
| **What students prepare** | Summary of key takeaways from the meeting, any clarifying questions |
| **Content framing** | Professional norm — every meeting gets a follow-up. |

### Week 5 — Direction Check
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | "Are we headed in the right direction?" Present findings and proposed approach; get feedback before committing to production. |
| **Format** | Document-based discussion (30 min). Employer has already received the Context Analysis & Project Plan. |
| **What students prepare** | 2-3 key highlights from the document, specific questions for the employer, assigned roles (presenter, notetaker, timekeeper) |
| **Employer's role** | Has read the document; provides feedback on direction; validates or redirects the approach |
| **Deliverable** | Direction Check Summary (completion-based checkpoint) |
| **Content framing** | This is NOT a slide presentation. It's a structured conversation around a document. Students highlight key areas and invite feedback, not walk through the document page by page. Frame as presenting for validation, not approval. |

### Week 7 — Check-In #1
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | "We're building. Help us decide X, Y, Z." Use the employer as a resource to unblock specific production decisions. |
| **Format** | Working session (30 min). Student-driven agenda. |
| **What students prepare** | 2-3 specific questions or decision points where the employer's input would change what they do next. Brief status context (2 min max, not a presentation). |
| **Employer's role** | Responds to specific questions; provides context that helps students make better decisions; offers observations |
| **Deliverable** | Work-in-Progress Summary (completion-based checkpoint — internal document, not sent to employer) |
| **Content framing** | This is a working session, NOT a presentation. Students aren't showing progress to be evaluated. They're using the employer's knowledge to make better decisions about work that's actively underway. The value of this meeting is proportional to how specific the questions are. "Are we on track?" wastes the time. "We're deciding between approach A and approach B for the competitive analysis — which would be more useful to your team?" uses it. |
| **Design doc note** | The design docs use "WIP presentation" language. In student-facing content, soften this to "working session" or "check-in." The WIP Summary is still a required checkpoint, but the meeting itself is a conversation, not a performance. |

### Weeks 8+ — Async status updates
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Maintain employer engagement between meetings |
| **Format** | Brief written update via Riipen platform |
| **Content framing** | Standing practice, not a big deal. One paragraph: what you've done, what's next, any blockers. |

### Week 9 — Check-In #2
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | "Here's what we've built. Does this meet the bar? Where are the weak spots?" Quality feedback on draft work. |
| **Format** | Working session (30 min). Student-driven agenda. |
| **What students prepare** | Draft sections to share (or summarize); specific questions about quality, completeness, and whether the work meets the employer's expectations. Students have been through peer evaluation by this point, so questions should be more refined than Week 7. |
| **Employer's role** | Reviews draft work; provides feedback on quality and direction; identifies weak spots; shares observations |
| **Deliverable** | Debrief notes (not a formal checkpoint, but feeds into Draft Deliverable) |
| **Content framing** | The question has shifted from "are we on track?" (Week 7) to "is this good enough?" Frame as quality feedback, not progress reporting. Students should be specific about what kind of feedback they need: "Is this analysis deep enough for your purposes?" is better than "What do you think of our progress?" |
| **Design doc note** | The employer mid-project survey results are shared to students around this time. Content should acknowledge that students may be processing feedback from multiple directions (peer evaluation + employer survey + this meeting). |

### Week 11 — Near-Final Review
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | "Here's the near-final product. What's missing before we finalize?" The deliverable should speak for itself. |
| **Format** | Document review (30 min). Employer reviews the near-final deliverable package. |
| **What students prepare** | Complete near-final deliverable; Change Log showing how prior feedback was incorporated; targeted questions about completeness and remaining gaps |
| **Employer's role** | Reviews the deliverable package (pre-read or during meeting); discusses quality and completeness; provides final directional feedback |
| **Deliverable** | Near-Final Package (completion-based checkpoint) |
| **Content framing** | This is NOT a presentation. The document should speak for itself. The Change Log is the key artifact — it demonstrates that the team engaged with prior feedback, not just received it. Brief walkthrough of the Change Log, then let the employer react to the work. |

### Week 13 — Final Check-In (optional)
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Only if substantive questions remain. If not, cancel the meeting — that's a professional judgment, not laziness. |
| **Format** | Working session (30 min) OR async communication |
| **What students prepare** | Remaining substantive questions, if any. If no questions: async confirmation of Week 15 presentation logistics. |
| **Employer's role** | Answers remaining questions if meeting is held; receives logistics confirmation |
| **Deliverable** | Final Deliverables (graded, 30% of course grade) — submitted regardless of whether meeting is held |
| **Content framing** | The decision to hold or cancel is itself a professional skill. Frame it as such: "If your team doesn't have substantive questions that would change the final deliverable, cancelling the meeting and using that time for polish is the right call." |

### Week 15 — Final Presentation
| Element | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Purpose** | Present completed work. The culmination of 15 weeks. |
| **Format** | Formal presentation (60 min). The only formal presentation besides the Direction Check. |
| **What students prepare** | Narrative-structured presentation (20-25 min), Q&A preparation, deliverable handoff |
| **Employer's role** | Attends presentation; asks questions; provides feedback and reflections; receives final deliverables |
| **Deliverable** | Final Presentation (graded, 15% of course grade) |
| **Content framing** | This IS a formal presentation — the only one in the production phase. Defend-your-work standard applies to Q&A. Students should present from genuine understanding, not rehearsed talking points. |

## Key principles for content production

1. **Name the distinct purpose.** Every employer touchpoint page should open by stating what this meeting is for and how it's different from the last one. Don't assume students will infer the difference.

2. **Match the framing to the format.** Only Weeks 5 and 15 are presentations. Everything else is a conversation, working session, or document review. Don't use "presentation" language for meetings that aren't presentations.

3. **The quality of a working session depends on the quality of the questions.** For Weeks 7 and 9, the prep guidance should focus on formulating specific, decision-relevant questions, not on preparing to show work.

4. **Progression should be visible.** Students should notice that their role shifts across the course: from listener (Week 3) to presenter (Week 5) to driver (Weeks 7, 9) to reviewer (Week 11) to deliverer (Week 15). Making this progression explicit helps students understand why each meeting feels different.

5. **Don't manufacture formality.** If a meeting is a casual working session, let it be casual. Students who treat every employer interaction like a formal presentation will exhaust themselves and their employer. Professional judgment includes knowing when to be formal and when to be direct.

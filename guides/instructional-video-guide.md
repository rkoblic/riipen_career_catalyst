# Instructional Video Script Guide

How to write non-overview video scripts for Riipen Career Catalyst. These are the videos embedded in content pages (pages 2+), not the overview video on page 1. For overview videos, see `guides/overview-video-guide.md`.

---

## What instructional videos are for

Instructional videos teach something specific. They are the primary teaching vehicle for a concept, skill, or process. The page text that surrounds them becomes a scannable recap, not a parallel explanation.

The core rule: **the video and the page text must not say the same thing.** If a learner could skip the video and get the same understanding from the page, the video isn't pulling its weight. If they could skip the page and get the same understanding from the video, the page is redundant.

The split should be:
- **Video:** Teaches the concept through concrete examples, before/after comparisons, or step-by-step walkthroughs. This is where the learner *understands* it.
- **Page text:** Provides a bulleted recap of the key moves or principles. This is what the learner *references* while doing the work.

Not every content page needs a video. A video earns its place only when it teaches something that's genuinely easier to understand through narrated examples than through written text. If the concept is straightforward enough to explain in a few paragraphs, don't force a video.

---

## What makes a strong instructional video

**Teach through examples, not definitions.** Don't open with "synthesis means identifying patterns across your findings." Open with a concrete example of what summary looks like versus what synthesis looks like. The definition can come after, or the learner can get it from the page recap. The video's job is to make the concept visceral, not to define it.

**Use the week's running project types.** When the week has established running examples (e.g., social media strategy, community outreach, process improvement), walk through the concept using those same projects. This lets learners see how the same skill applies to different kinds of work, and it creates consistency across the week's pages.

**Show the contrast.** The most effective instructional videos show what "wrong" looks like next to what "right" looks like. "Here's a summary of research findings. Now here's the same research, synthesized." The gap between them is what teaches the concept. Before/after, weak/strong, vague/specific: pick the contrast that makes the skill visible.

**Make each example earn its place.** If you're walking through three project-type examples, each one should illustrate something the previous one didn't. Don't just repeat the same point three times with different nouns. The first example might show the basic concept, the second might show a subtler application, and the third might show a common mistake.

**Be specific and actionable.** The learner should walk away knowing how to do something, not just why it matters. "Name your assumptions explicitly" is actionable. "Strategic thinking is important" is not. Every example should model a concrete move the learner can replicate in their own work.

## When to use the video-as-primary-teaching-vehicle pattern

This pattern (video teaches, page recaps) works best when:

- The concept is easier to grasp through narrated examples than written explanation (e.g., synthesis vs. summary, connecting findings to recommendations)
- The page would otherwise be long and dense with examples that work better spoken
- There's a clear before/after or weak/strong contrast to demonstrate

This pattern is not required for every video. Some videos serve a different purpose: a short framing video before a meeting prep section, a brief "here's what to expect" before a new type of activity. Those are closer to overview videos in function. Use the pattern that fits the content.

When you do use this pattern, restructure the page text:
1. Remove the prose explanation that the video now covers
2. Replace it with a short intro line referencing the video ("The video above walks through...")
3. Add a bulleted recap of the key moves (4-6 bullets, each one sentence)
4. Keep any guidance that's distinct from what the video covers (e.g., "What counts as evidence" can stay as page text if the video covers synthesis but not evidence standards)

## Structure

Instructional videos are longer than overview videos. Typical length: 90 seconds to 3 minutes (~150-450 words).

**Opening (2-3 sentences max):**
State the concept or skill plainly. Get to the first example fast. Don't spend a paragraph setting up why the concept matters — if the video is placed correctly on the page, the surrounding context already does that.

**Body (the bulk of the video):**
Walk through 2-3 concrete examples. Each example should follow a pattern:
- Set up the scenario briefly
- Show the weak/wrong version
- Show the strong/right version
- Name what's different and why it matters

If using running project types, cycle through them: first example uses Project A, second uses Project B, third uses Project C.

**Closing (1-2 sentences max):**
Land on the key takeaway or the most important move. Don't summarize what you just said. Don't add a pep talk. If the examples were clear, the closing is almost unnecessary.

## Patterns to avoid

All of the patterns from the overview video guide apply here too, plus:

**Restating the page text.** If the page already explains what synthesis is, the video shouldn't explain it again. The video should *show* it through examples.

**Opening with a definition.** "Synthesis means..." or "Strategic implications are..." — save definitions for the page text. The video should open with a scenario or example that makes the concept concrete before naming it.

**Generic examples.** "For example, if your team is working on a project..." — this is too vague to be instructive. Name a specific project type, a specific finding, a specific recommendation. The more concrete the example, the more the learner can transfer it to their own work.

**Narrating the obvious.** "Notice what happened there" or "See the difference?" — if the example was clear, you don't need to tell the learner to notice it. If it wasn't clear, the solution is a better example, not a meta-commentary.

**Teaching multiple concepts in one video.** Each video should teach one thing well. If you find yourself covering synthesis AND evidence standards AND pitfalls, that's three videos or one video and two page text sections.

## Voice rules

Same as overview videos:
- **Second person** ("you," "your team"). No first person ("I," "me," "let me").
- **No em dashes.** Use commas, periods, or parentheses.
- **Written to be heard.** Short sentences. Conversational rhythm.
- **American English** throughout.
- **No slang, idioms, or culturally specific expressions.**

### Writing for voiceover, not for reading

Scripts will be narrated in Lumen5. Everything must sound natural spoken aloud. A few specific rules:

- **No colon-labeled formats.** "Defensive response: 'We chose this because...'" reads fine on a page but sounds robotic narrated. Instead, flow it as prose: "A natural response is 'we chose this segment because our research showed...' That explains your reasoning, but it doesn't get you anywhere."
- **No bullet lists.** Bullets are for page text recaps. In a script, each point should flow as a sentence or short paragraph.
- **No formatting that depends on visual structure.** Bold, headers, numbered lists — none of these translate to audio. If the script needs structure, use paragraph breaks and transitional phrases ("Same pattern with vague feedback." "Here's another scenario.").
- **Read every script aloud before finalizing.** If any sentence feels unnatural to say, rewrite it. If you stumble on a phrase, the narrator will too.

## Quality check

Before finalizing an instructional video script, ask:

- [ ] Does this video teach something the page text doesn't? (If the page says the same thing, one of them needs to change.)
- [ ] Does it open with an example or scenario, not a definition?
- [ ] Are the examples specific enough that a learner could model their own work on them?
- [ ] Does each example illustrate something the previous one didn't?
- [ ] Is the opening under 3 sentences?
- [ ] Is the closing under 2 sentences, with no summary or pep talk?
- [ ] Is the companion page text structured as a recap (bullets), not a parallel explanation?
- [ ] Read it aloud: does it sound like someone explaining something at a whiteboard, or like a textbook being narrated?
- [ ] No colon-labeled formats, bullet lists, or visual formatting that won't translate to voiceover?

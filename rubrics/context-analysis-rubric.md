# Context Analysis & Project Plan — Assignment & Rubric

*Riipen Experience | Week 4 Deliverable*
*Team submission | 5–8 pages | Due end of Week 4*

---

## The Assignment

The Context Analysis & Project Plan is the first graded deliverable in the course, worth 10% of the final grade. It demonstrates whether students have done the analytical work that will make their project valuable — rather than simply executing on instructions received at the kickoff meeting. All team members are accountable for the whole document; it is submitted once per team.

### Part 1: Organizational & Project Context (~1 page)

Brief overview of the organization — what they do, who they serve, their current situation. The problem or opportunity this project is addressing, in the team's own words. Key things learned from the kickoff that shaped their understanding of the project.

### Part 2: Research Findings (~1.5–2 pages)

Summary of the team's independent research — what they looked at and why. The 3–5 most important findings, with evidence. What the team still doesn't know and would need to find out.

### Part 3: Strategic Implications (~1 page)

What the research means for how the team approaches this project. The assumptions they're making and where they're uncertain. Any significant constraints or considerations the employer should know the team is aware of.

### Part 4: Project Plan (~1.5–2 pages)

Deliverables the team will produce, in what form. Key dates and checkpoints (largely reflecting the course calendar). Team roles and responsibilities. Risks — what could go wrong and how the team will manage it. Open questions for the Direction Check.

## Rubric Design

### Scale

All course rubrics use the same 5-point scale, enabling competency-level reporting and cross-rubric comparability.

| Level | Label | Percentage | Grade Equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|
| 4 | Exemplary | 95% | A |
| 3 | Proficient | 85% | B |
| 2 | Developing | 75% | C |
| 1 | Emerging | 65% | D |
| 0 | Not Acceptable | 0% | F |

The 0 anchor captures submitted work that makes no real attempt, which is distinct from weak work. The 65% floor for Emerging prevents the mathematical penalty of a zero from disproportionately punishing students who submit genuine but struggling work.

### Dimensions

Five fixed dimensions, equally weighted at 20% each. Every dimension represents a genuine and distinct intellectual demand of the deliverable. Students receive the same rubric as the instructor — transparency is a feature.

| Dimension | Weight | Competency → Subskill | Evaluates |
|---|---|---|---|
| Research Quality | 20% | Critical Thinking → Research & Analysis | Part 2 |
| Evidence-Based Reasoning | 20% | Critical Thinking → Evidence-Based Reasoning | Part 2 |
| Strategic Implications | 20% | Critical Thinking → Problem Framing | Part 3 |
| Planning Quality | 20% | Critical Thinking → Prioritization | Part 4 |
| Document Quality | 20% | Communication → Written Clarity | Whole document |

**Why no Professionalism dimension:** Professionalism subskills (dependability, initiative, feedback reception) are not visible in a Week 4 document submission. The only Professionalism subskill assessable from a written deliverable is quality and attention to detail — which is captured by Document Quality. Professionalism is primarily assessed through Professional Engagement, which is the appropriate evidence source.

**Why Evidence-Based Reasoning and Strategic Implications are separate dimensions:** Evidence-Based Reasoning (how you use your research) and Problem Framing (what you conclude from it) are genuinely distinct cognitive tasks. The deliverable separates them structurally — Part 2 is where Evidence-Based Reasoning lives, Part 3 is where Strategic Implications lives. Splitting enables cleaner competency reporting and more useful diagnostic feedback. A team that finds good sources but asserts conclusions without grounding them needs different feedback than a team that reasons carefully from thin research.

## Dimension 1: Research Quality

*Critical Thinking → Research & Analysis*
*Evaluates Part 2: Research Findings*

| Level | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| **Exemplary (4)** | Findings are specific to this project context, grounded in cited sources, and presented with enough detail to be useful. Gaps in what the team doesn't yet know are named and given meaningful consideration. |
| **Proficient (3)** | Findings are broadly relevant and supported by cited sources. Gaps are acknowledged. |
| **Developing (2)** | Findings are present but generic — applicable to almost any project in this space. Sources are thin or uncited. Gaps are not acknowledged. |
| **Emerging (1)** | Findings are vague or largely restate what would be known without investigation. Sources are absent or insufficient to support the findings presented. |
| **Not Acceptable (0)** | No meaningful research is present. The section is missing or contains only information requiring no independent investigation. |

---

## Dimension 2: Evidence-Based Reasoning

*Critical Thinking → Evidence-Based Reasoning*
*Evaluates Part 2: Research Findings — whether claims follow from evidence rather than assertion.*

| Level | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| **Exemplary (4)** | Claims are consistently grounded in the research. The team distinguishes clearly between what the evidence shows and what they are inferring from it. Where evidence runs out, assumptions and uncertainty are named explicitly. |
| **Proficient (3)** | Most claims are grounded in the research. The team generally distinguishes between findings and inferences, even if not always explicitly. Some uncertainty is acknowledged. |
| **Developing (2)** | Claims are frequently asserted without grounding in the research. The team does not distinguish between what they found and what they're concluding. Uncertainty is not acknowledged. |
| **Emerging (1)** | Claims are largely unsupported assertions. There is little evidence that conclusions follow from any research base. |
| **Not Acceptable (0)** | No meaningful attempt to ground claims in evidence. |

---

## Dimension 3: Strategic Implications

*Critical Thinking → Problem Framing*
*Evaluates Part 3: Strategic Implications*

| Level | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| **Exemplary (4)** | Implications are clearly derived from the research and meaningfully shape the direction of the project. They are specific to this context and identify tradeoffs, tensions, or strategic choices the team must navigate. |
| **Proficient (3)** | Implications follow logically from the research and are relevant to this project context. They identify clear priorities or considerations that should shape the team's approach. |
| **Developing (2)** | Implications are present but remain general or loosely connected to the research. They resemble broad observations rather than project-specific direction. |
| **Emerging (1)** | Implications are vague, generic, or disconnected from the research findings. They do not meaningfully inform project direction. |
| **Not Acceptable (0)** | No meaningful strategic implications are articulated. |

---

## Dimension 4: Planning Quality

*Critical Thinking → Prioritization*
*Evaluates Part 4: Project Plan*

| Level | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| **Exemplary (4)** | The plan is realistic, clearly structured, and aligned with the project's strategic direction. Roles are clearly defined. Risks are thoughtfully identified with plausible mitigation strategies. Scope reflects intentional prioritization rather than breadth. |
| **Proficient (3)** | The plan is coherent and generally aligned with the project direction. Roles are defined. Risks are identified. Scope is reasonable, though tradeoffs or scoping decisions are not made explicit. |
| **Developing (2)** | The plan is present but lacks clarity, alignment, or realism. Roles may be unevenly defined. Risks are listed but not meaningfully considered. Scope may appear overly broad or underdeveloped. |
| **Emerging (1)** | The plan is vague, incomplete, or unrealistic. Roles are unclear. Risks are absent or superficial. Scope is not thoughtfully considered. |
| **Not Acceptable (0)** | No meaningful project plan is provided. |

---

## Dimension 5: Document Quality

*Communication → Written Clarity*
*Evaluates the document as a professional artifact*

| Level | Descriptor |
|---|---|
| **Exemplary (4)** | The document is clear, well-organized, and professional. The document reads as a unified whole despite being produced by a team. Ideas flow logically across sections. Writing is concise and precise. Formatting enhances readability. |
| **Proficient (3)** | The document is organized and understandable. Writing is generally clear, though occasional awkward phrasing or minor errors may appear. Formatting supports readability. |
| **Developing (2)** | Organization is uneven or sections lack clarity. The document may read as separately written sections that were not fully integrated. Writing may be wordy, imprecise, or contain noticeable errors. Formatting inconsistencies detract from readability. |
| **Emerging (1)** | The document is difficult to follow. Organization is unclear. Writing contains frequent errors or unclear phrasing. Formatting hinders readability. |
| **Not Acceptable (0)** | The document is incomplete or unreadable. |

## Design Principles

The following principles guided rubric construction and should inform any future refinement.

**Proficient is the anchor.** Every dimension was written Proficient-first. Proficient describes work a reasonable instructor would accept without hesitation but wouldn't single out as exceptional. All other levels calibrate off it.

**Every phrase is observable from the document.** Evaluators do not need to cross-reference other documents (e.g., the Kickoff Summary) to apply the rubric. If a descriptor requires comparison to another source, it was reframed around what's visible on the page.

**Descriptors are tight; the grading guide carries examples.** The rubric is precise but not exhaustive. Concrete illustrations of what descriptors look like in practice belong in the grading guide, not the rubric itself. This keeps the rubric usable for both students and evaluators.

**Dimensions have clean boundaries.** Each dimension assesses one thing and is silent on the others. Research Quality measures the inputs. Evidence-Based Reasoning measures whether claims follow from those inputs. Strategic Implications measures whether the team translates reasoning into project direction. Planning Quality measures deliberate choices about scope, roles, and risk. Document Quality measures clarity, coherence, and professionalism. No dimension double-counts what another already captures.

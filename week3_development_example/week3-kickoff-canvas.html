This prompt walks you through the full arc: preparing for the kickoff meeting, running it well, processing what you heard, and documenting it in your Kickoff Summary.

Before the Meeting

Pull together what you already know

Your team did individual research in Week 2 on the organization, its industry, and the project brief. Now it's time to consolidate that into shared preparation.

As a team, we recommend you:

  • Review each person's research. What did everyone find? Where do your findings overlap, and where do they diverge?
  • Identify what you still don't know. The project brief gave you a starting point, but briefs are always incomplete. What assumptions are you making? What would change your approach if the answer were different than you expect?
  • Prioritize your questions. You won't get to ask everything. Rank your questions by what you most need to learn from the employer specifically — things you can't find out on your own.
  • Assign meeting roles. Decide in advance who will lead the conversation, who will take notes, and who will manage time. Having roles prevents the meeting from becoming five people jumping in unprepared.
Suggested Agenda — Pre-Meeting Team Huddle (~30 min)
  • Quick round-robin: what did each person learn in their Week 2 research? (10 min)
  • What are our top questions for the employer? Prioritize to 5–7. (10 min)
  • Confirm roles: lead, note-taker, timekeeper. Brief walkthrough of the flow. (10 min)

Confirm logistics with your employer

Before the meeting, send a short email to your employer contact confirming the details. This is a professional courtesy — it reduces no-shows, surfaces any last-minute changes, and gives you a chance to set expectations.

Your confirmation email should include:

  • Date, time, and platform (with the meeting link)
  • Who will be attending from your team
  • A brief note on what you're hoping to cover (not a full agenda — just a sentence or two so they know what to expect)
  • An invitation for them to flag anything they'd like to discuss

Keep it short. Three to five sentences. Professional, warm, to the point.

Set up for note capture

Good notes from this meeting are essential — your Kickoff Summary depends on them, and your memory of what was said will fade faster than you think.

You're encouraged to use AI tools to help with note capture. Options include AI-powered meeting transcription that runs during the call, or using an AI assistant to organize your raw notes immediately after. The key is having a plan before the meeting starts — not scrambling to remember details afterward.

A few practical notes on AI-assisted note capture:

  • If using a transcription tool, test it before the meeting and make sure your employer is comfortable with it being active
  • If you prefer manual notes, designate your best note-taker and have at least one other person capturing key points as backup
  • Either way, plan to do a quick AI-assisted cleanup of your notes within an hour of the meeting ending, while context is fresh

Quick Reference: Video Call Professionalism

If this is your first professional video call, a few basics go a long way:

  • Camera on, good lighting (face a window or lamp, don't sit in front of one), clean background
  • Mute when you're not speaking, especially in a group
  • Look at the camera when you're talking, not at yourself
  • If you have technical issues, stay calm — say "I'm having some audio trouble, give me a moment" rather than disappearing silently

During the Meeting

What you're trying to learn

Your kickoff meeting will cover a lot of ground, but there are specific things you need to walk away understanding.

📋 Meeting Checklist

☐ Project scope — what exactly are they asking you to produce? What's in bounds and what's out?

☐ Success criteria — what does "good" look like to them?

☐ Deliverables and format — what do they want handed to them at the end?

☐ Timeline and milestones — are there dates that matter on their end?

☐ Key contacts — who is your primary contact? Anyone else you should know about?

☐ Organizational context — what's happening in their organization that shapes this project?

Reading between the lines

The most important information in a kickoff meeting isn't always what's said directly. Experienced professionals learn to read the conversation — to hear what's emphasized, what's repeated, and what's left unsaid.

After the Meeting

Debrief immediately

We strongly recommend your team debrief within an hour of the meeting ending — ideally right after. Impressions fade fast, and the things that surprised you or felt important in the moment are exactly what you need to capture.

This doesn't need to be formal. A 15–20 minute conversation covering:

  • What surprised us? What did we learn that we didn't expect?
  • What does the employer care most about? Based on what they said and how they said it.
  • What don't we know yet? What questions are still open?
  • Did we hear the same thing? Compare interpretations — if two team members took away different understandings of the scope, surface that now.

Process your notes into shared understanding

Raw meeting notes — whether from a transcription tool or handwritten — aren't useful to anyone yet. The work is in turning them into something your whole team agrees on.

This is where AI can genuinely help. If you have a transcript or detailed notes, you can ask an AI assistant to organize them by topic, flag areas where the information is ambiguous, or draft a structured summary for your team to review. The AI is organizing your information — it's not generating content from nothing.

When prompting AI to help organize your notes, give it context:

  • Share the raw notes or transcript
  • Share the project brief
  • Tell it the format you want (e.g., "organize by: confirmed scope, deliverables, success criteria, timeline, contacts, open questions")
  • Review what it produces critically — AI will sometimes infer things that weren't actually said
Scenario Example

Your note-taker wrote: "They want us to look at their social media."
Your lead heard: "They want a full social media audit with competitor benchmarking."
The employer actually said: "It would be helpful to understand how we're showing up on social compared to others in our space."

These are three different things. The note is too vague. The lead's version adds scope that wasn't requested. The employer's actual words suggest something in between — an informal comparison, not a formal audit. This is why comparing notes matters, and why going back to exact language matters more.

Send a follow-up email

Within 24 hours of the meeting, your team should send a brief follow-up email to your employer contact. This is standard professional practice — it shows you were paying attention, confirms your understanding, and opens the door for clarification.

Your follow-up email should include:

  • A thank-you for their time
  • A brief summary of your understanding of the project scope and next steps (2–3 sentences, not a full recap)
  • Any specific open questions you want to flag for follow-up
  • Your expected timeline for next contact

This email also serves a practical function: if you misunderstood something in the meeting, this is where the employer can catch it early.

The Deliverable: Kickoff Summary

What it is

The Kickoff Summary is a factual record of what your employer communicated in the kickoff meeting. It's not analysis. It's not your interpretation of what the project should be. It's documentation of what was actually said — what's confirmed and what's still open.

Think of it as the foundation your team will build on for the rest of the project. If this document is vague or inaccurate, every decision you make afterward sits on a shaky base. Next week's Context Analysis & Project Plan — your first graded deliverable — depends directly on the quality of this summary.

Vague version "The employer wants us to help with their marketing. They mentioned social media and said the board cares about it. Timeline is soon."
Clear version Confirmed scope: Assess current social media presence across Instagram and LinkedIn relative to three regional competitors.

Success criteria: Actionable recommendations for the June board meeting.

Timeline: Draft by June 1; board presentation June 15.

Open question: Email marketing — in scope or aspirational?

What to include

📋 Kickoff Summary Sections

☐ Confirmed project scope — what the employer asked you to do, reflecting what they actually said

☐ Deliverables and format — what you'll produce and in what form

☐ Success criteria — how the employer will evaluate your work

☐ Timeline and key dates — deadlines, milestones, dependencies

☐ Key contacts — who you'll work with and their roles

☐ Open questions — what wasn't answered, what needs follow-up

Key Principle

The distinction between confirmed information and open questions is the most important thing in this document. Being clear about what you know versus what you're still figuring out is a sign of intellectual honesty and professional maturity.

Format and logistics

  • Team submission: One document, collaboratively produced. One person drafts; all team members review and confirm accuracy before submission.
  • Length: No specific requirement. Be thorough and concise. Most good Kickoff Summaries run 2–4 pages.
  • AI use: You are encouraged to use AI to help organize and structure your notes. You may not use AI to generate the substance. AI disclosure required.
  • Completion criteria: Checkpoint deliverable (completion-based). Must include all sections above and clearly distinguish confirmed info from open questions.

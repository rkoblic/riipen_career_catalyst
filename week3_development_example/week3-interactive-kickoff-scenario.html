Reading Between the Lines

Practice interpreting a client conversation before your real kickoff meeting.

In a kickoff meeting, the most important information isn't always what's said directly. Experienced professionals learn to read the conversation — to hear what's emphasized, what's repeated, and what's left unsaid.

You're about to watch a simulated kickoff meeting with a fictional employer. Pay attention not just to what she says, but how she says it — what she emphasizes, what she downplays, and what she mentions in passing that might actually be critical.

After the meeting, you'll answer a few interpretation questions. Then we'll show you what an experienced consultant would have picked up from the same conversation.

The Scenario

Dana Whitfield, Director of Community Engagement at Lakeview Regional Health System, is meeting with your team for the first time to discuss a community health outreach project.

Time: ~5 minutes to watch, ~5 minutes to answer questions

Watch the Meeting

Watch Dana describe her project. Pay attention to what she emphasizes, what she downplays, and what she mentions in passing. When you're ready, continue to the questions.

The meeting's over. Before you look at your notes or talk to your team — what did you pick up? Answer based on what you heard, not what you'd guess.

The Full Picture

Here's what that conversation revealed — the kind of reading that gets easier with practice.

What she actually wants

Dana wants to understand why outreach isn't reaching underserved communities — not just better marketing materials. She's open to the answer being about trust, channels, or messaging. Your job is to figure out which, not to assume.

The real deadline

Late June, tied to a board presentation. When a client softens a deadline ("not a hard deadline for you"), they're being polite. They've already told you when your work needs to be done to be useful.

The internal politics

The VP of Marketing wants better creative. Dana isn't sure that's the answer. Your deliverable needs to address both perspectives — not to pick sides, but to be usable by Dana in a room where the VP is also present. This is a very common professional situation.

The elephant in the room

The merger. Lakeview used to be two separate hospitals. The eastern communities have a "complicated relationship" with the system. Dana calls this "probably relevant" — which in professional-speak means "this is the context that explains everything, and I'm curious whether you'll pick up on it." Your post-kickoff research should start here.

What would go in your Kickoff Summary

Confirmed scope: assess why preventive care outreach isn't reaching underserved populations in the eastern service area. Deliverable: assessment with recommendations, formatted for leadership/board consumption. Timeline: board presentation late June. Key contact: Dana Whitfield. Open questions: what specific ZIP codes or communities? What data do they already have on screening rates? Can you speak with anyone in the eastern communities directly? What's the VP of Marketing's specific critique of current creative?

The takeaway

In your real kickoff, you'll hear all of this in real time — without the ability to reread or pause. That's why preparation matters, why having a note-taker matters, and why debriefing with your team immediately after matters. The signals are there. You just have to practice noticing them.

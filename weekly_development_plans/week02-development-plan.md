# Week 2 — Building Your Team
## Content Development Plan

---

## Content Architecture

Week 2 is a **Pattern E (orientation/formation)** week with a dual deliverable: the Team Charter and the Employer Outreach Email. The Team Charter is the primary anchor — the first team meeting and charter negotiation organize the week. The outreach email is a lighter secondary prompt. The week also introduces several standalone modules: Using AI Responsibly (the first formal prompt strategy, referenced throughout the course), a research primer (Part 1 of the three-week research progression), and communication skills for client meetings (transferable skills applied at every employer touchpoint). The flow is: Overview → Using AI Responsibly → Team Charter Anchor → Outreach Email Prompt → Research & Communication Primers → What's Next (bridging to the Week 3 kickoff and Week 4 Context Analysis).

---

## Content Production Plan

### 1. Week 2 Overview
**Type:** Orientation text
**Absorbs:** 2.01, 2.04

Welcomes students to their first week with a team. Frames the week around team formation — you've been assigned your team, and by the end of the week you'll have a working agreement and an email in your employer's inbox. Names both deliverables (Team Charter, Employer Outreach Email) and gives suggested order of completion. Prompts students to read their teammates' Week 1 intro posts before the first meeting.

- What's happening this week and why it matters: your team starts now (AI-generated video; ~60–90 sec)
- What's due, when, and suggested completion order (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Before your first meeting: read your teammates' intro posts (AI-generated text; ~75 words)

---

### 2. Using AI Responsibly
**Type:** Standalone module
**Absorbs:** 2.02

First formal introduction of prompt strategy and critical evaluation of AI output. This module establishes the shared frame students will use for the rest of the course — how to prompt well, how to evaluate what AI gives you, and how to use it as a thinking partner rather than an answer machine. Positioned before the first team meeting so students have a shared vocabulary for AI use during team work.

- Why learning to work with AI is a professional skill, not a shortcut (AI-generated video; ~60–90 sec)
- Three prompt principles: give context, ask for options not answers, question your assumptions (AI-generated text; ~300 words)
- Example prompts: good vs. weak prompts for common project tasks (AI-generated text; ~250 words)
- How to evaluate AI output: checking for accuracy, unsupported claims, and hallucinated sources (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- Critical evaluation exercise: spot the problems in this AI-generated research summary (interactive practice; ~5 min)

---

### 3. Team Formation & Charter Anchor Prompt
**Type:** Assignment prompt with embedded guidance
**Absorbs:** 2.03, 2.06, 2.07

The primary anchor. Covers the full arc from preparing for your first team meeting through negotiating and submitting the Team Charter. The module content from 2.03 (Setting Your Team Up for Success) folds directly into the meeting preparation section.

#### Before Your First Meeting
- What the Team Charter is actually for: a reference point for when things get bumpy, not a compliance document (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- Three things to get agreement on: how you'll communicate, how you'll make decisions, what "done" looks like (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- How to come prepared: review the charter template, think about your own working style and preferences (AI-generated text; ~100 words)

#### Running Your First Meeting
- Suggested meeting agenda: introductions → shared brief read-through → initial reactions → charter negotiation (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- Ground rules: no AI during this meeting — this is relationship-building time (AI-generated text; ~75 words)
- What to do if you can't agree: how to navigate disagreement in a brand-new team (AI-generated text; ~150 words)

#### Drafting and Submitting the Charter
- Charter template walkthrough: communication norms, contribution expectations, decision-making process, conflict handling (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- What "done" looks like: all four members agree on the substance before submission (AI-generated text; ~75 words)
- AI disclosure: AI can help format or refine language after the team has agreed, but the substance must come from your discussion (AI-generated text; ~75 words)
- Submission requirements (AI-generated text; ~50 words)

---

### 4. Employer Outreach Email Prompt
**Type:** Assignment prompt with embedded guidance
**Absorbs:** 2.09, 2.10

The team's first professional correspondence with their employer contact. Lighter than the charter anchor — students already have email basics from Week 1 and are applying a known skill in a higher-stakes context.

- What the outreach email needs to accomplish: introduce your team, demonstrate awareness of the project, and request the kickoff meeting (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Outreach email template: structure and key elements (template)
- Tone and professional context: this is a client, not a professor (AI-generated text; ~100 words)
- Collective agreement: all team members review and agree before sending (AI-generated text; ~75 words)
- Following up: what to do after you send it — confirming receipt, handling logistics (AI-generated text; ~75 words)
- AI disclosure: AI-assisted drafting is permitted; disclosure required (AI-generated text; ~50 words)

---

### 5. Researching an Organization
**Type:** Standalone module
**Absorbs:** 2.11

Part 1 of the three-week research progression (Week 2 = organizational reconnaissance, Week 3 = targeted landscape, Week 4 = analytical synthesis). Scoped narrowly: show up to the kickoff informed. Who is this company, what do they do, what's their sector, any recent news.

- Why you research before you meet: arriving informed vs. arriving cold (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- What to look for: organization overview, sector context, recent news, project relevance (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- Where to look: recommended sources for organizational research (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Using AI for research: how to use AI to understand unfamiliar sectors or terminology (AI-generated text; ~100 words)

---

### 6. Communication Skills for Client Meetings
**Type:** Standalone module
**Absorbs:** 2.12, 2.13, 2.14

Combines three closely related modules: kickoff-specific operational guidance (2.12) and two transferable communication skills — asking good questions (2.13) and active listening (2.14) — that students will use at every employer touchpoint throughout the course. Taught together at point of need before the Week 3 kickoff.

#### Kickoff Best Practices (2.12)
- Role assignment: who asks, who takes notes, who manages time (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- How to open the meeting, what to listen for, what you're trying to walk away with (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- What to do if the conversation goes off track (AI-generated text; ~100 words)

#### Asking Good Questions (2.13)
- Open vs. closed questions and when to use each (curated link + framing; source article or video, ~5 min reading/viewing)
- How to prioritize questions when time is limited (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Listening for what's not being said: follow-up questions that go deeper (AI-generated text; ~100 words)

#### Active Listening in Client Meetings (2.14)
- How to listen without waiting to talk (curated link + framing; source article or video, ~5 min reading/viewing)
- Note-taking strategies that don't pull you out of the conversation (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Clarifying without interrupting: when and how to ask for more (AI-generated text; ~100 words)

---

### 7. What's Next
**Type:** Transitional prompt
**Absorbs:** 2.15

Bridges to Week 3 (kickoff meeting) and Week 4 (Context Analysis & Project Plan). Frames the rubric review as a strategic activity — knowing the assessment criteria before beginning work shapes what students prioritize in research and charter conversations.

- What's coming: Week 3 is the employer kickoff meeting (AI-generated text; ~100 words)
- Why you should review the Context Analysis & Project Plan rubric now: knowing where you're headed shapes how you prepare (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Linked resource: Context Analysis & Project Plan assignment description and rubric (students review in advance; ~20–25 min)
- What to look for in the rubric: criteria, evidence required, how this connects to your research (AI-generated text; ~100 words)

# Week 7 — Building Professional Work Products
## Content Development Plan

---

## Content Architecture

Week 7 is a **hybrid of Pattern A and standalone modules**. Check-In #1 is the dominant anchor event — a questions-based employer meeting that organizes the second half of the week around before/during/after structure. Three production skill modules (7.03, 7.04, 7.05) are genuinely distinct from the meeting prep: they teach professional writing, peer review, and data presentation skills students need throughout the Development phase, not just for Check-In #1. These get their own standalone pages following Week 6 precedent. Two modules (7.02, 7.06) serve Check-In #1 directly and absorb into the anchor's "before" section. The flow is: Overview → From Plan to Product → Reviewing Your Teammates' Work → Working with Data and Visuals → Check-In #1 Anchor Prompt → What's Next. **6 content pieces total.**

### 2. From Plan to Product

**Absorbs:** 7.03 (15 min)

Covers conventions of client-facing reports, decks, and memos; the distinction between academic writing and professional writing; what makes a document "polished" vs. "complete." Video is the primary teaching vehicle; page text is a scannable bulleted recap. Follows instructional-video-guide.md rules.

- Instructional video (~90–120 sec): teaches through before/after contrast between an academic-style and professional-style version of the same content — shows concretely what changes and why. Video is primary teaching vehicle; page text is a scannable bulleted recap. Must follow instructional-video-guide.md rules (concrete examples, before/after contrast, no restating page text). (AI-generated video)
- Why this matters framing: professional documents aren't graded for effort — they're evaluated for whether a client can act on them (~100 words, AI-generated text)
- Academic vs. professional writing: key distinctions with concrete examples — bulleted recap structured as companion to the video, not a parallel prose explanation (~250 words, AI-generated text — bulleted recap format)
- What "polished" vs. "complete" means in a client context: polished means readable, navigable, and credible to someone who didn't write it (~200 words, AI-generated text)
- Curated link on professional writing conventions (article or short video, ~5–8 min)

### 3. Reviewing Your Teammates' Work
**Type:** Standalone module
**Absorbs:** 7.04 (15 min)
**Competencies:** Teamwork → Constructive Feedback

Reframed from abstract "giving feedback" to concrete production review skill. Positioned as Peer Evaluation #1 prep coming next week. Teaches how to read a colleague's draft section for clarity, argument structure, evidence quality, and tone; distinguishes line editing from structural feedback. Video is primary teaching vehicle; page text is scannable recap. Follows instructional-video-guide.md rules.

- Instructional video (~90–120 sec): walks through a concrete example of structural vs. line-level feedback on a draft section — shows what each looks like and why structural feedback is more valuable at this stage. Video is primary teaching vehicle; page text is recap. Follows instructional-video-guide.md rules. (AI-generated video)
- Why this matters framing: the ability to give useful written feedback on a colleague's work is a professional skill used throughout careers, not a course exercise (~100 words, AI-generated text)
- Structural feedback vs. line editing: what distinguishes them and why structural feedback is the priority at this stage of production (~200 words, AI-generated text — bulleted recap format as companion to video)
- The four dimensions to review: clarity (can a reader follow the logic?), argument structure (does the section have a clear claim and supporting evidence?), evidence quality (is the evidence specific and credible?), tone (is it professional and appropriate for a client audience?) — what to look for in each dimension (~250 words, AI-generated text)
- What actionable feedback looks like: specific, referenced to the work, focused on what to change rather than what's wrong — with contrasting examples of vague vs. actionable feedback (~150 words, AI-generated text)
- Curated link on giving effective feedback on written work (article, ~5 min)

*Time check: 15 min budget. ~90–120 sec video + ~500–600 words (~3 min) + curated link (~5 min) = ~9–11 min. Remaining ~4–6 min = applying the framework to an actual teammate section. Fits.*

### 4. Working with Data and Visuals
**Type:** Standalone module
**Absorbs:** 7.05 (15 min)
**Competencies:** Communication → Written Clarity; Critical Thinking → Evidence-Based Reasoning

New content this week. Teaches when to use tables, charts, or narrative to present data; how to present numbers so they support an argument rather than report information; using AI to help visualize data while maintaining accuracy. Video is primary teaching vehicle; page text is scannable recap. Follows instructional-video-guide.md rules.

- Instructional video (~90–120 sec): concrete example of the same data presented three ways — table, chart, and narrative — when each serves best and why. Video is primary teaching vehicle; page text is recap. Follows instructional-video-guide.md rules. (AI-generated video)
- Why this matters framing: how you present data affects whether it lands as evidence or noise for a professional reader (~100 words, AI-generated text)
- Table vs. chart vs. narrative: decision guide for when each format serves best — bulleted recap structured as companion to the video's examples (~200 words, AI-generated text — bulleted recap format)
- Presenting numbers to support an argument: the difference between "reporting" data and using data to make a point (~200 words, AI-generated text)
- AI for data visualization: using AI to generate chart options or suggest presentation formats; verify the output and defend the choices (~100 words, AI-generated text)
- Curated link on data presentation in professional documents (article or short video, ~5–8 min)

*Time check: 15 min budget. ~90–120 sec video + ~400–500 words (~2.5 min) + curated link (~5–8 min) = ~9–12 min. Remaining ~3–6 min = applying decision guide to actual data in draft. Fits.*

### 5. Check-In #1 Anchor Prompt
**Type:** Assignment prompt with embedded guidance
**Absorbs:** 7.02 (15 min), 7.06 (10 min), 7.07 (75 min), 7.08 (60 min) — total 160 min

Pattern A: before/during/after structure organized around Check-In #1. Most of the 160 min is doing time — drafting the WIP Summary, running the team prep session. Produced content targets the instructional moments where guidance is needed. Check-In #1 is the first progress check-in meeting of the Development phase — a new type of employer meeting — so an interactive practice scenario is required per Principle 8.

#### Before Check-In #1

**Receiving feedback well (absorbs 7.02)**

- Why feedback feels hard in a professional context: brief framing — the natural defensive response when someone evaluates work you've invested in (~100 words, AI-generated text)
- The reception protocol: Listen → Clarify → Acknowledge → Decide Later — each step explained with concrete professional behaviors, not just labels (~200 words, AI-generated text)
- Feedback Protocol Card: scannable reference card with the four steps and a one-line behavioral guide for each — designed to be used during and immediately after the meeting (checklist/reference card format; ~100 words)

**The Work-in-Progress Summary (absorbs part of 7.07)**

- What the WIP Summary is and why it exists: this is a preparation tool for the team, not an employer pre-read — the employer does not receive it; its job is to force the team to articulate progress clearly before the meeting. Surface the pedagogy: the submission requirement ensures teams cannot walk into the meeting unprepared (~150 words, AI-generated text)
- What goes in the summary — four sections, each with an explanation of what it accomplishes from the employer's perspective (per Principle 11 — explain the "why" behind every deliverable section):
  - Completed work: what has been produced to date, specific and named
  - Current status: where the project is right now — what's drafted, what's in progress, what's not started
  - Challenges encountered: honest account of blockers, gaps, and unexpected complications
  - Specific questions or feedback needs: what the team needs from the employer to move forward
  (~250 words, AI-generated text)
- What the completion criteria require: honest assessment, specific questions (not vague or performative summaries); what "everything is going well, no questions" signals about a team (~150 words, AI-generated text)
- AI guidance for WIP Summary: AI can help organize and clarify after the team has agreed on substance; AI cannot generate an honest project status assessment (~100 words, AI-generated text)

**Preparing for Check-In #1 (absorbs 7.06)**

- What a questions-based meeting is: how this meeting differs from a formal presentation — you're not presenting to impress, you're bringing problems the employer can help solve (~150 words, AI-generated text)
- How to develop specific questions and decision points that make the meeting productive: what makes a question useful vs. generic (~150 words, AI-generated text)
- Anticipating what the employer might ask: how to stress-test your own preparation by anticipating reactions to draft work (~100 words, AI-generated text)

**Team prep session (absorbs 7.08)**

- How to use the prep time: identify 3–5 specific questions, assign who leads each topic, prepare the brief status update (~5 min), plan for note-taking during the meeting (~150 words, AI-generated text)

**Interactive practice: a simulated Check-In #1 (Principle 8)**

- Simulated scenario: the team receives unexpected feedback from the employer on their draft direction — the employer says the framing of the deliverable doesn't match what they needed. Learners decide how to respond using the reception protocol, what to capture, and how to translate the feedback into a decision. Debrief surfaces the difference between a defensive response and a receptive one. (~8–10 min) (interactive practice)

#### During Check-In #1

- Meeting structure: brief status update (~5 min), student questions and decision points (~35–40 min), employer observations (~10 min), confirming priorities (~5 min) — with brief rationale for why this structure serves the team (~150 words, AI-generated text)
- Capturing decisions in real time: what to write down, how to flag ambiguous answers for follow-up (~100 words, AI-generated text)

#### After Check-In #1

- Post-meeting debrief: what to capture immediately after the meeting — employer answers to questions, decisions made, new information surfaced, action items — and how to structure notes for the whole team to act on (~150 words, AI-generated text)
- Weekly Progress Check-In standing prompt: introduce the new standing practice — at the close of each week, teams check against their project plan: what's done, what moved, what's blocked, who owns what this week. First instance this week; continues through the end of the course. (~100 words, AI-generated text)
- AI guidance for debrief: AI can help organize and structure notes after the meeting; AI cannot fabricate the content of a conversation it wasn't part of (~75 words, AI-generated text)

#### Submission

- WIP Summary completion criteria and AI disclosure requirement: submission is required before Check-In #1; AI-assisted drafting is permitted with disclosure; the substance must come from the team's actual project status; a one-sentence non-use statement is sufficient if AI was not used (~100 words, AI-generated text)

*Time check: 160 min total budget. Produced content: ~1,800 words (~9 min reading) + interactive practice (~8–10 min) + Feedback Protocol Card scan (~2 min) = ~19–21 min of produced content. Remaining ~140 min = doing time (WIP Summary drafting, team prep session, pre-meeting assembly). Appropriate — the bulk of time is doing.*

### 6. What's Next
**Type:** Transitional prompt

Bridges to Week 8 — Peer Evaluation #1. Follows the standardized what's-next page structure from the content-production-guide.md: H1 is "What's next"; first H2 names the next milestone specifically; optional second H2 for actionable preparation. No video.

- Connecting sentences: what was just completed — Check-In #1 marks the first employer accountability point of the Development phase; the feedback received this week becomes the production direction for the weeks ahead (~2 sentences, AI-generated text)
- "Peer Evaluation #1" H2: what it is (the first formal assessment of teamwork and peer feedback skills), what it assesses, and how this week's peer draft review work is direct preparation (~150 words, AI-generated text)
- "How to prepare" H2: review the Peer Evaluation rubric before next week; ensure this week's peer feedback notes are captured in a form you can act on; specific, documented feedback on a teammate's work is the input Peer Evaluation #1 requires (~100 words, AI-generated text)

# Week 5 — Professional Communication
## Content Development Plan

---

## Content Architecture

Week 5 is a **Pattern A (meeting-centered)** week. The entire week revolves around the Direction Check — the first time students present their own work to a professional stakeholder and receive real feedback on their approach. All four module activities (5.02–5.05) serve the Direction Check directly, teaching communication and feedback skills students need immediately before and during the meeting. The anchor prompt organizes as **before / during / after** sections, with the Direction Check Summary (completion-based checkpoint) as the written output on the other side. The flow is: Overview → Direction Check Anchor Prompt → What's Next (bridging to Week 6 production).

---

## Content Production Plan

### 1. Week 5 Overview
**Type:** Orientation text
**Absorbs:** 5.01

Frames the week around the Direction Check as the anchor event. Names the before/during/after structure and orients students to the suggested order of completion: module content first, then prep, then meeting, then debrief and summary. Makes clear that this is the week where the project direction gets confirmed or redirected — both outcomes are expected and professional.

- Why this week matters: your first real presentation to a professional stakeholder — and the feedback that shapes everything after (AI-generated video; ~60–90 sec)
- What's happening this week, suggested order of completion, and what's due (AI-generated text; ~200 words)

---

### 2. Direction Check Anchor Prompt
**Type:** Assignment prompt with embedded guidance
**Absorbs:** 5.02, 5.03, 5.04, 5.05, 5.08

The anchor prompt carries all the instructional weight this week. Organized in before/during/after sections around the Direction Check meeting. The four module activities (60 min of student time) provide the conceptual foundation; the anchor prompt embeds that instruction at the point of need rather than as standalone modules. Activity 5.08 (email coordination, 10 min) is absorbed as a logistics email template in the "before" section.

#### Before the Direction Check

**Presenting for validation, not approval (absorbs 5.02)**

- Why this matters: the difference between presenting to get feedback and presenting to perform — in professional contexts, the goal is to surface problems early, not to impress (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- How to frame your proposed approach so it invites feedback: leading with rationale, naming uncertainties, and asking specific questions rather than "what do you think?" (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- How professionals present recommendations to stakeholders (curated link + framing; source article or video, ~5 min reading/viewing)

**Managing expectations and difficult conversations (absorbs 5.03)**

- What to do when scope shifts, when you need to say "we can't do that," and when the employer's expectations don't match what's feasible — framed as a normal part of professional work, not a failure (AI-generated text; ~200 words)

**Preparing for the Direction Check (absorbs 5.04)**

- What to present, what questions to ask, and how to use the meeting time effectively: a structured approach to the 60-minute meeting (AI-generated text; ~250 words)
- What to do when the meeting doesn't go as planned — the employer hasn't read the submission, scope shifts unexpectedly, or the conversation goes off-agenda (AI-generated text; ~150 words)

**Interactive practice: a simulated Direction Check (Principle 8)**

- Simulated scenario: students encounter a realistic Direction Check moment — the employer redirects the project in a way the team didn't expect. Students interpret what the employer is actually asking for, decide how to respond professionally, and identify what to capture for follow-up. Debrief surfaces the difference between reacting defensively and responding constructively (interactive practice; ~8–10 min)

**Confirming logistics (absorbs 5.08)**

- Email template for confirming meeting time, link, and attendees with the employer — brief, professional, and complete (template; ~75 words)

#### During the Direction Check

- Meeting structure and how to use the time: presenting key findings (~15 min), proposed approach and rationale (~10 min), employer feedback (~15 min), discussion and refinement (~10 min), confirming next steps (~5 min) (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Listening without defending: how to receive feedback that changes your direction — asking clarifying questions when feedback is vague, avoiding the impulse to justify, and translating what you hear into what you'll do (absorbs 5.05) (AI-generated text; ~250 words)
- Active listening in professional settings (curated link + framing; source article or video, ~5 min reading/viewing)

#### After the Direction Check

- The immediate debrief: what to capture while the conversation is fresh — specific feedback, exact quotes where possible, points of agreement and disagreement, and anything ambiguous that needs follow-up (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Writing the Direction Check Summary: format (one page, three components), what goes in each section — feedback received, plan adjustments, confirmed approach — and the distinction between what was heard and what was decided (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- Direction Check Summary template: three-section format with prompting questions for each component (template; ~100 words)
- Revising the project plan: connecting specific employer feedback to concrete planning decisions — what changes, what stays, and why (AI-generated text; ~150 words)

#### AI Guidance

- How AI can and cannot help with the Direction Check Summary: AI can organize and clarify notes after the team has captured what was said, but cannot generate the substance of a conversation it wasn't present for. A Summary that reads as generic rather than specific to what the employer actually communicated has not met the completion criteria (AI-generated text; ~100 words)

#### Submission

- Completion criteria: the Summary documents specific feedback received, identifies concrete adjustments or confirms the approach, and captures next steps (AI-generated text; ~100 words)
- AI disclosure requirements (AI-generated text; ~50 words)

### 3. What's Next
**Type:** Transitional prompt

Bridges to Week 6 — the first real production week. Frames the deliverable structure work (5.14) as the setup for sustained production: the revised project plan and Direction Check Summary have confirmed the approach; now students translate that into report outlines, deck skeletons, and divided sections. Names the shift from "figuring out what to do" (Discovery phase) to "doing it" (Production phase).

- What's coming in Week 6: production begins — your approach is confirmed, now you build (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- How to set yourself up: use the remaining time this week to build deliverable structures and divide the work (AI-generated text; ~100 words)

### Flags / Dependencies
- The interactive practice element (Principle 8) is critical for this week — the Direction Check is the first time students present their own work to a stakeholder, and the scenario should rehearse the hardest version of that experience (employer redirects unexpectedly) so the real thing feels manageable
- Open question from design doc: graded feedback turnaround time — activity 5.06 asks students to review graded feedback on the Context Analysis, but it may not be returned by the Direction Check. If feedback is reliably unavailable, 5.06 should be reframed as prep-only without the feedback review component
- Open question from design doc: how does the Context Analysis reach the employer before the meeting? This affects both 5.04 (preparation guidance assumes employer has read it) and 5.09 (meeting structure assumes employer familiarity). Contingency guidance for when the employer hasn't read it is included in the anchor prompt, but the handoff mechanism remains unresolved from Week 4
- Open question from design doc: employer touchpoint timing — the week assumes the Direction Check falls mid-week, but scheduling variation is inevitable. Course-level guidance on flexible week structure (flagged for Week 1 orientation content) must be in place before this week

# Week 6 — Working Through Ambiguity
## Content Development Plan

---

## Content Architecture

Week 6 is a **Pattern C (production-centered, no single dominant anchor)** week, and also a reflection week — the second of five. There is no employer touchpoint, and no single deliverable that the module content primarily serves. The five modules (new + 6.02–6.05) are genuinely distinct skills — each covering a different dimension of the first production week — so they each get their own standalone page rather than folding into a single anchor. Reflection #2 (6.10) is graded, individual, and always gets its own separate piece per the weekly structure convention. The flow is: Overview → Starting Production as a Team → Formatting Professional Documents → Making Progress Under Uncertainty → From Research to Draft → Navigating Disagreement → Reflection #2 Prompt → What's Next. **8 content pieces total.**

---

## Content Production Plan

### 1. Week 6 Overview
**Type:** Orientation text
**Absorbs:** 6.01

Frames the week as the shift from planning to production. Names the structure: four standalone modules first (just-in-time frameworks for the production work ahead), then a front-loaded production block, then reflection at the end of the week. Also sets the production target for the week — students should leave with a named section drafted to a presentable state, since Week 7 requires significant time on WIP preparation.

- Why this week marks the shift from planning to production — your approach is confirmed, now you build (AI-generated video; ~60–90 sec)
- What's happening this week, the structure (four modules → production → reflection at the end of the week), suggested order of completion, and what "done" looks like by the end of the week: a named section drafted to a point where you can present it in Week 7 (AI-generated text; ~250 words)

### 2. Starting Production as a Team
**Type:** Standalone module
**Absorbs:** New content — not in original design doc

Addresses the gap between "sections are assigned" and actually starting to build a shared document. Written as a direct continuation of the team production workflow established in the Context Analysis page, but focused on what's different about multi-week collaborative production. Covers document setup decisions (one doc, section ownership, formatting standards, terminology) before anyone starts drafting; section dependencies; and how to use the team sync for coordination rather than reporting. No video needed — primarily procedural guidance with a setup checklist.

- Why setup decisions made before drafting determine whether the deliverable reads as one document or assembled pieces (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Four setup decisions: one shared document, section ownership, formatting standards applied first (link forward to formatting page), agreed terminology (AI-generated text; ~250 words)
- Section dependencies: which sections must be developed before others can be written; which can proceed in parallel (AI-generated text; ~100 words)
- How to use the team sync as a coordination tool — surfacing blockers, aligning on cross-section directions, catching divergence early (AI-generated text; ~100 words)
- Before-you-start-drafting checklist: 6 items covering shared doc setup, section ownership, formatting standards, terminology, dependencies, and team sync (checklist; ~75 words)

*Time check: ~600 words (~3 min reading) + checklist (~1 min) + applying to own project (~5 min) = ~9 min. No time budget in the design doc — new content, not absorbed from an existing activity. Fits comfortably within the week's unallocated orientation time.*

### 3. Making Progress Under Uncertainty
**Type:** Standalone module
**Absorbs:** 6.02 (20 min)

Just-in-time instruction for students who are about to spend 60–210 minutes doing independent research and drafting with incomplete information. Teaches the decision framework for when to proceed vs. pause, how to diagnose types of ambiguity, and how to flag a blocker without stalling the team.

- Why this matters: in professional work, production never starts with complete information — learning to draft under uncertainty is a core career skill, not a coping strategy (AI-generated video; ~60–90 sec)
- Types of ambiguity and how to diagnose them: missing information vs. unclear expectations vs. conflicting priorities — and why the distinction matters for what you do next (AI-generated text; ~300 words)
- Decision framework: when you have enough to proceed vs. when you must pause — including how to flag a blocker to your team without stalling everyone (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- How professionals manage ambiguity in knowledge work (curated link + framing; article or short video, ~5–8 min)

*Time check: 20 min budget. ~60–90 sec video + ~500 words (~2.5 min reading) + curated link (~5–8 min) = ~9–12 min. Remaining ~8–10 min = thinking time and applying the framework to students' own project context. Fits.*

### 3. From Research to Draft
**Type:** Standalone module
**Absorbs:** 6.03 (20 min)

Addresses the cognitive gap between having research notes and producing a draft — the step where students most commonly stall. Teaches the notes → outline → prose workflow explicitly, including AI use cases for each stage.

- Why this matters: the gap between research and writing is where students most commonly stall — this section makes the bridge explicit and learnable (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- How to cluster and synthesize raw notes into an organized outline: grouping by theme, labeling clusters, and seeing structure before writing it (AI-generated text; ~250 words)
- Evaluating whether your outline is ready to draft from: questions to ask before committing to prose — what gaps would leave a reader confused? (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Moving from outline to prose: why the first draft doesn't need to be good, how to break the writing-stall pattern (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- AI use cases for notes → outline → draft: the three prompt strategies from the design doc, each with a brief context note on when and why to use it (AI-generated text with prompt examples; ~200 words)
- How to write from notes: a practical guide for research-based documents (curated link + framing; ~5–8 min)

*Time check: 20 min budget. ~900 words (~4.5 min reading) + curated link (~5–8 min) = ~10–13 min. Remaining ~7–10 min = working through the outline evaluation questions against students' own notes. Fits.*

### 4. Navigating Disagreement
**Type:** Standalone module
**Absorbs:** 6.04 (20 min)

Addresses the team and client dynamics students are about to encounter during production. Distinct from the general collaboration norms established in Week 2 — this is specifically about production-phase disagreement: a teammate's section going the wrong direction, or employer Direction Check feedback the team doesn't agree with.

- Why this matters: your first real production week will surface disagreements — about direction, about approach, about what the employer actually meant. How you handle them now sets the tone for the rest of the project (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Disagreeing productively with teammates: types of production-phase disagreement (direction, quality, workload), how to raise a concern while protecting the relationship (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- When employer direction feels wrong: the professional logic of when to defer vs. when to push back gracefully — connects directly to Direction Check feedback students just received (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- Navigating difficult conversations at work (curated link + framing; article or short video, ~5 min)

*Time check: 20 min budget. ~550 words (~2.75 min reading) + curated link (~5 min) = ~8 min. Remaining ~12 min = thinking time connecting the frameworks to specific team and employer dynamics students are navigating this week. Fits.*

---

### 5. Formatting Professional Documents
**Type:** Standalone module
**Absorbs:** 6.05 (15 min)

A practical reference module on document presentation standards, timed to the start of the first production week so students build good habits from the beginning rather than retroactively fixing formatting in Week 11. The formatting checklist is the primary takeaway — students reference it during production and review.

- Why formatting matters from the start — visual hierarchy, consistency across team-authored sections, and how document presentation affects perceived credibility; connects to Deliverable Quality (30%) and Communication (15%) dimensions of the final rubric (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- Practical standards: headings and hierarchy, white space and scan-ability, tables and charts, callout boxes, terminology and style consistency (AI-generated text; ~250 words)
- Formatting checklist: scannable reference students can use while drafting and reviewing (checklist; ~20 items)
- Professional document design principles (curated link + framing; ~5 min)

*Time check: 15 min budget. ~400 words (~2 min reading) + checklist scan (~2 min) + curated link (~5 min) = ~9 min. Remaining ~6 min = applying checklist against a section in progress. Fits.*

### 6. Reflection #2 Prompt
**Type:** Reflection prompt
**Absorbs:** 6.10 (90 min)

The second DEAL reflection in the course. The framework is familiar; the expectation shifts from demonstrating familiarity with DEAL to using it with depth and honesty. Sequenced at the end of the week so students draw from the fullest possible view of the Discovery phase — the Direction Check, the first week of production, and any moments of ambiguity or team tension are all live in memory.

- Why reflection at this moment: you're closing the Discovery phase with more lived experience than you had in Week 3 — the reflection uses that material (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- What Reflection #2 is looking for: the DEAL framework is familiar now; the expectation shifts from demonstrating you can use it to using it with depth and honesty — what's changed since Week 3? (AI-generated text; ~200 words)
- DEAL prompt structure:
  - **Describe:** Name a specific experience from the Discovery phase (Weeks 4–6) — the research process, the Direction Check, the transition into production, a moment of ambiguity or team tension. Be specific and factual: what happened, with who, when? (AI-generated text with prompt questions; ~150 words)
  - **Examine:** What assumptions were operating? What patterns does this experience reveal about how you work? What were you feeling that you didn't say? Push past the surface. (AI-generated text with depth prompts; ~200 words)
  - **Articulate Learning:** What transferable insight can you extract that connects to professional practice beyond this project? Write it as something a colleague or future employer could understand without knowing this course. (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- AI-as-Socratic-partner guidance: using AI to deepen your Examine stage — three specific prompt strategies with a brief note on what each one surfaces (AI-generated text with prompt examples; ~200 words)
- Submission requirements: AI disclosure requirement (what counts as "using AI," what a non-use statement looks like), length expectations, Riipen submission platform (AI-generated text; ~100 words)

*Time check: 90 min. ~1150 words produced guidance (~6 min reading) + student reflection drafting time (~75–80 min). Appropriate — the bulk of time is doing.*

### 7. What's Next
**Type:** Transitional prompt

Bridges to Week 7 — the Work-in-Progress presentation. Frames the remaining production time this week as the window to get an assigned section to a presentable state before drafts move from private to visible.

- What's coming in Week 7: the Work-in-Progress presentation — your drafts move from private to visible, and the employer sees where you are. Use the remaining time this week to get your assigned section to a presentable state (AI-generated text; ~150 words)
- How to define "presentable": a rough draft with structure is better than polished fragments — what the WIP is actually evaluating (AI-generated text; ~100 words)
